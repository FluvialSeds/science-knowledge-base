> Abstract
> The triple-oxygen isotope ( 17 O/16 O, 18 O/16 O) measurement of oxygen-bearing species represents one of the most robust
> tools to directly trace oxygen cycling in the environment. One particularly consequential application of this isotope system
> is the analysis of dissolved oxygen (O 2 ) in aquatic environments to determine gross oxygen production. This approach
> assumes that photosynthesis, microbial respiration, and gas exchange are the main drivers of dissolved O 2 isotope composi-
> tions, and that each process is described by predictable, consistent triple-oxygen isotope effects. However, there currently
> exists large disagreement in the literature on the triple-oxygen isotope effect of respiration, which carries major implications
> for global primary productivity estimates. Recent work has additionally highlighted the ubiquitous production of extracellu-
> lar reactive oxygen species (ROS) such as superoxide (O:
> 2 ) and hydrogen peroxide (H 2 O 2 ) by microorganisms; this flux may
> be responsible for as much as 20% of net oxygen utilization in the ocean. To examine the influence of ROS-mediated O 2 recy-
> cling on the oxygen utilization isotope effect, we measured the triple-oxygen isotope fractionations and mass laws of super-
> oxide dismutase, catalase, and iron-mediated H 2 O 2 degradation. We incorporate these constraints into an oxygen isotope flux
> model to explore the influence of ROS-mediated oxygen cycling on ‘‘respiration” isotope effects in previous studies. We find
> that ROS-mediated oxygen cycling can reconcile the previously reported range of triple-oxygen isotope fractionation factors
> and that typical marine isotope effects are broadly consistent with independent estimates of superoxide-mediated oxygen loss.
> Lastly, we determine that O:
> 2 production likely follows non-canonical triple-oxygen isotope mass laws (6 0:510), indicating
> that this process may exert significant leverage on dissolved O 2 isotope compositions and thus primary productivity estimates.
> We conclude that ROS cycling should be considered when using dissolved O 2 triple-oxygen isotopes to determine marine pri-
> mary productivity.

> Quantifying primary productivity and respiration fluxes
> in the modern environment is essential to understand past,
> present, and future pO2 budgets and the redox state of the
> surface Earth. The triple-oxygen isotope (TOI) method is a
> powerful tool to measure gross primary productivity
> (GPP); its use has expanded significantly following the
> pioneering work of Luz et al. (1999). The central premise
> of this method states that the TOI composition of dissolved
> O 2 in seawater quantitatively reflects three processes, each
> with distinct isotope signatures: (i) oxygenic photosynthe-
> sis, (ii) gas exchange between the surface ocean and the
> lower troposphere, and (iii) oxygen utilization by marine
> microbes. The relative rates at which these three processes
> act on dissolved O 2 in a water parcel will thus determine
> its TOI composition (Luz and Barkan, 2000). By utilizing
> independent constraints on O 2 gas exchange and on the iso-
> tope systematics of oxygen consumption, GPP can be iso-
> lated and readily quantified (Prokopenko et al., 2011).
> While this method is still subject to its own set of uncertain-
> ties, a principal advantage is that it directly quantifies in situ
> O 2 production without bias from ‘‘bottle effects” that pla-
> gue many common ship-based incubation methods
> (Peterson, 1980).
> The TOI method canonically relies on several simplifica-
> tions, most notably: (i) nearly all oxygen-consuming ‘‘respi-
> ration” reactions across diverse biological groups are
> assumed to impart the same oxygen isotope fractionation
> (Luz and Barkan, 2005), and (ii) the TOI composition of
> photosynthetic O 2 is assumed to be equal to or slightly
> more enriched in the heavier isotopes than that of the sur-
> rounding water (i.e., little to no photosynthetic isotope frac-
> tionation; Guy et al., 1993; Eisenstadt et al., 2010). While
> the latter point may be justified, recent observational and
> theoretical work has challenged the assumption that respi-
> ration expresses a single, consistent TOI effect on residual
> dissolved oxygen (Stolper et al., 2018; Ash et al., 2020).
> For example, Stolper et al. (2018) showed that the
> oxygen-isotope fractionation during respiration by Escher-
> ichia coli can exhibit large variability as a function of termi-
> nal O 2 -reductase and temperature when grown in batch
> culture, highlighting the capacity for both environment
> and metabolic factors to influence the expressed TOI effect.
> Previous studies have attempted to reconcile the
> observed range of TOI effects by considering (i) multi-step
> mechanistic respiration models and/or (ii) the potential
> influence of non-respiratory oxygen-consuming reactions
> (Bender et al., 1994; Stolper et al., 2018; Ash et al., 2020).
> While the former has been described in detail (Stolper
> et al., 2018; Ash et al., 2020), the latter is not typically con-
> sidered in culture-based or environmental TOI studies
> despite the existence of non-respiratory oxygen-consuming
> reactions that take place in and around cells. Such reactions
> can comprise a substantial fraction of a cell’s oxygen bud-
> get and may carry a TOI effect that is distinct from respira-
> tion (Kana, 1993; Bender et al., 1994; Helman et al., 2005).
> For example, two major non-respiratory oxygen-
> consuming reactions in phototrophs—photorespiration
> and the Mehler reaction—have both been proposed
> as possible mechanisms to reconcile oxygen isotope mass-
> balance in the environment (Kana, 1993; Bender et al.,
> 1994; Badger et al., 2000; Helman et al., 2005), but the
> impact of non-respiratory reactions on TOI signals has gen-
> erally not been fully constrained.
> Of particular interest here, the non-respiratory produc-
> tion of extracellular superoxide (O:
> 2 ) by one-electron trans-
> fer to O 2 is increasingly recognized as an important process
> for cell metabolism and physiology; it is widespread in the
> microbial world and results from a range of mediated and
> non-mediated biological processes (Hansel and Diaz,
> 2021). Superoxide is present in both phototrophs and het-
> erotrophs, and it plays several important roles in microbial
> physiology, including: cell signaling, maintenance of redox
> homeostasis, growth promotion, and nutrient acquisition
> (Rose et al., 2008; Diaz et al., 2019; Hansel et al., 2019;
> Yuasa et al., 2020). Additionally, O:
> 2 can be produced inci-
> dentally as a byproduct of metabolic reactions, including
> those involved in photosynthesis and respiration (Cleland
> and Grace, 1999; Dro¨ se and Brandt, 2012). Superoxide pro-
> duction may thus represent an important O 2 sink; one
> recent estimate suggests that light-independent O:
> 2 produc-
> tion comprises between 5 and 20% of net dissolved O 2 con-
> sumption in the marine environment (Sutherland et al.,
> 2020). Furthermore, this phenomenon is not isolated to
> marine microorganisms; for example, mammalian cells
> have been shown to consume between 2 and 25% of oxygen
> through O:
> 2 production on the cell surface and in mito-
> chondria (Chance et al., 1979; Manes and Lai, 1995).
> Despite the widespread production of reactive oxygen
> species (ROS) in general and O:
> 2 in particular, their poten-
> tial influence on the oxygen isotope composition of dis-
> solved O 2 in seawater—and thus on TOI-based GPP
> estimates—remains largely unexplored. To address this,
> here we quantify the TOI effects of common ROS-
> metabolizing enzymes as well as Fe-mediated hydrogen per-
> oxide (H 2 O2 ) decay as a non-enzymatic analog. We then
> incorporate these results into an isotope flux-balance model
> to explore the potential influence of ROS recycling on the
> TOI composition of dissolved O 2 in seawater and on corre-
> sponding GPP estimates.

> 2.2. Outstanding questions
> Although the TOI method is a powerful tool to measure
> GPP, the use of a fixed respiration k value inherently
> assumes a uniformity of oxygen utilization, a notion that
> has recently become the subject of increased scrutiny
> (Fig. 1A; Stolper et al., 2018; Ash et al., 2020). Accurately
> quantifying k is critical since this variable exerts significant
> leverage on calculated primary productivity fluxes; for
> example, if the chosen respiration k value differs from the
> true TOI mass law for oxygen utilization by 0:001, this will
> lead to a 10–15% change in the resulting GPP estimates
> (Ash et al., 2020).
> Studies conducted over the last two decades have typi-
> cally used k ¼ 0:518, which stems from early observations
> of diverse organisms, including microbes and animals
> (Angert et al., 2003; Luz and Barkan, 2005). More recently,

> 4.1. TOI measurements of O:
> 2 and H 2 O2 decay
> We have chosen a representative from each enzyme
> group to offer initial isotopic constraints on O2 evolved
> from SOD and catalase. Specifically, we analyzed a repre-
> sentative CuZn SOD (Sigma S5395) and typical catalase
> (heme-binding, clade 3, small subunit, Sigma C1345). The
> same experiment apparatus was used to investigate the iso-
> tope effects of both enzymes. The reaction vessel consisted
> of a closed-system glass bulb with an inlet subject to contin-
> uous He flow (pre-scrubbed with 5 A molecular sieve at liq-
> uid nitrogen temperature) through a glass frit, an outlet
> stream, and an injection port outfitted with a blue butyl
> rubber septum. The outlet stream was passed through mul-
> tiple loops of stainless steel tubing held at liquid nitrogen
> temperature to trap any water vapor that escaped the reac-
> tor. KO 2 or H 2 O2 were added for reactions with SOD and
> catalase, respectively. Effluent He carrier gas and product
> O 2 were then passed through a 5 A molecular sieve trap
> to isolate O 2 gas evolved by the enzyme of interest.
> In the case of catalase, 2 mg of enzyme was dissolved in
> approximately 50 mL of ultra-high purity (18 MX MilliQ)
> water and bubbled with He for a minimum of one hour
> to remove O 2 , at which point 30% H 2 O 2 was introduced
> to the solution through the injection port to an initial con-
> centration of 3.9 mM in the reaction vessel. The reaction
> was allowed to proceed for a range of time intervals (a
> few minutes to several hours) to ensure adequate coverage
> of reaction progress for use with the Rayleigh equation.
> The oxygen isotope composition of the starting H 2 O 2 was
> determined using the same experimental setup (without
> catalase) by adding a slurry containing MilliQ water and
> KMnO 4 (amounting to  10 excess of H 2 O 2 ), which
> quantitatively oxidizes H 2 O 2 to O 2 (Savarino and
> Thiemens, 1999). The average O 2 yeild of the catalase and
> H 2 O 2 treatment was 48% (n ¼ 3) that of the KMnO4 treat-
> ment, consistent with the expected 2:1 stoichiometry of
> catalase (Eq. (8)).

> of reaction between superoxide and SOD, we instead char-
> acterized the oxygen isotope composition of the starting
> material (introduced as KO 2 ) and the fully completed reac-
> tion to determine how oxygen isotopes are partitions
> between the oxidized and the reduced product. Methods
> for both measurements were modified from one previous
> study investigating the 18 O isotope effects of CuZn SOD

> 6. DISCUSSION
> 6.1. The influence of ROS on O 2 triple oxygen isotopes
> The production and degradation of ROS can signifi-
> cantly influence the TOI composition of dissolved O 2 in
> the environment. We show here that Fe-catalyzed H 2 O 2
> degradation as well as two representatives of ubiquitous
> enzymes responsible for degrading ROS—SOD and cata-
> lase—evolve oxygen along a wide range of mass-law slopes
> ranging from 0:5174  0:0014 to 0:5347  0:0006 (Table 3);
> this range encompasses many other biological processes
> including respiration, RuBisCO oxygenase activity, and
> some instances of the Mehler reaction (Helman et al., 2005).
> Given that the standing stock of these ROS in natural
> waters or under culturing conditions are typically low and
> reach steady state on short time scales (i.e., minutes for
> superoxide), it is unlikely that there are any isotopic conse-
> quences associated with sequestering oxygen atoms in the
> various ROS reservoirs. Instead, the primary influence
> exerted by ROS-producing and -degrading enzymes on dis-
> solved O2 is derived from (i) isotope effects present in the
> ROS-generating reaction ( 18aO:
> 2 =O2 and hO:
> 2 =O2 ) and (ii)
> any uneven isotope partitioning between the oxidative
> and reductive halves of the disproportionation reaction
> (xbSOD and xbcat ). Although we do not directly measure
> point (i) in this study, our model results do provide initial
> constraints on the TOI systematics of ROS production
> (see below). Furthermore, by quantifying k values for sev-
> eral processes (Fig. 5; Table 3), we demonstrate that point
> (ii) is indeed a factor governing the isotope evolution of
> O 2 evolved from SOD and catalase.
> Oxygen evolved from CuZn SOD is described by d18 O
> values that are 27:2  0:1‰ higher than those for the
> inferred reduced product (i.e., H 2 O2 , determined by isotope
> mass balance between starting O:
> 2 and the product O 2 ).
> Notably, this is 10‰ higher than the value observed in
> a previous d18 O analysis of the same enzyme (Smirnov
> and Roth, 2006). This difference may be a result of the fact
> that the previous study was working with a KO 2 solution
> prepared in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). DMSO is a polar,
> aprotic solvent that can dissolve KO 2 without significant

> 6.2. Triple oxygen isotope systematics of disproportionation
> Observed k values for the ROS disproportionation reac-
> tions performed here are determined by multiple factors,
> including the h and a values inherent to both the oxidative
> and reductive branches of the disproportionation pathway.
> These values will jointly determine the isotopic evolution of
> total accumulated products; thus, the observed k value for
> accumulated O 2 is ultimately reflected in both the oxidized
> and reduced products. Intuiting differences in oxidation and
> reduction a and h values—including how they interact to
> influence the expressed k in oxidized, reduced, or accumu-
> lated products—is not straightforward. To explore these
> interactions, we calculate how the expressed k values
> respond as a function of oxidation and reduction a and h
> values for a Rayleigh-type disproportionation reaction with
> 1:1 stoichiometry between oxidized and reduced products.
> Specifically, we solve the equation

> 6.4. Reanalysis of marine triple oxygen isotope data
> Finally, we assess if ROS production and recycling can
> explain observed marine dissolved O 2 TOI trends. Placing
> our newly measured k results for ROS degradation within
> the context of those observed for natural marine O 2 utiliza-
> tion is challenging since many published k values come
> from axenic cultures outside of their natural setting. One
> k value for a natural lake microbial consortium has been
> measured (Ash et al., 2020), but extending this result to
> marine microorganisms would require validation. In addi-
> tion to biological isotope fractionation, physical processes
> including advection and diffusion also influence the appar-
> ent fractionation factors of marine dissolved O 2 (Levine
> et al., 2009).
> One possible method of isolating biological O 2 fraction-
> ation from these physical processes is to measure the iso-
> tope composition of O 2 over a time series in which dark,
> biological O2 consumption (via both respiratory and non-
> respiratory sinks) is the primary driver of O 2 concentration
> variability. We therefore devised a reanalysis of time-series
> marine O 2 TOI measurements collected at the same loca-
> tions over a period of months to years. At a given location,
> we assume the physical processes that influence O 2 TOI
> composition below the mixed layer should be constant
> through time, whereas respiration and ROS cycling fluxes
> will increase or decrease with seasonal changes in carbon
> export from the surface ocean. Although imperfect—partic-
> ularly since no two water parcels are described by identical
> physical histories and the influence of advective and diffu-
> sive mixing can vary over time—this practice offers a poten-
> tial method to extract biological TOI effects without the
> obvious pitfalls of lab-based studies (e.g., axenic cultures,
> bottle effects).
> For this reanalysis, we compile dissolved O 2 TOI data
> from the Hawaiian Ocean Time-series (HOT) and Bermuda
> Atlantic Time-series (BATS) long-term oceanographic
> monitoring stations originally presented in Nicholson
> et al. (2012) and Luz and Barkan (2009). We calculated dis-
> solved O 2 TOI fractionation relationships for individual
> depths that are both (i) below the photic zone and (ii) below
> the deepest reported mixed layer depth over the period of
> analysis. For HOT, this includes data collected at depths
> of 150; 200, and 300m beneath the surface between 2006
> and 2008, whereas for BATS, this includes data collected
> at depths of 250; 300, and 2000m beneath the surface
> between 1998 and 2002. Resulting time-series biological
> O 2 consumption k values ranged from a minimum of
> 0:513  0:006 (HOT; 300m depth; n ¼ 18) to a maximum
> of 0:525  0:004 (HOT; 200m depth; n ¼ 21), with an aver-
> age value for the entire dataset of 0:519  0:004 (average of
> n ¼ 6 individual k estimates; Table 4).
> The extracted k values contain a relatively large calcu-
> lated uncertainty likely due to the small range of d18 O at
> any given location (5‰) and heterogeneity in the influ-
> ence of physical fractionating processes. Nonetheless, sev-
> eral site- and depth-specific k results are statistically
> unique (e.g., HOT; 300m depth; two-tailed t test;
> p < 0:05), supporting the idea that k is spatially variable,
> likely due to variation in seawater chemistry and metabolic
> and physiological diversity in the water column. This obser-
> vation is consistent with k values observed here for three
> ROS-degrading pathways, which range from
> 0:5174  0:0014 to 0:5347  0:0006 (Table 3), as well as
> results from a suite of O 2 -producing and O 2 -consuming
> reactions in phototrophs, which range from h < 0:500 to
> h > 0:531 (Helman et al., 2005). Such mass relationship
> diversity underscores the ability of biogeochemical cycling
> in general—and O 2 recycling in particular—to influence
> TOI compositions of environmental O 2 and modulate
> D017 O values.
