> Abstract
> The discovery of mass-independent isotope fractionation in atmospheric gases 40 years ago sparked a revolution in our
> ability to measure and understand Earth’s atmospheric composition, both today and in the geologic past. Despite four
> decades of progress, several open questions remain, including those related to chemical mechanisms, fractionation factors,
> and geologic interpretations. Here, we summarize and compile the community’s current theoretical, experimental, and
> observational understanding of mass-independent fractionation (MIF). We focus primarily on oxygen- and sulfur-bearing
> species, with a particular emphasis on signals that can be transferred into the geologic record, and we highlight targets for
> future research. Finally, we discuss the emerging frontier of MIF in new isotope systems, including mercury and selenium.

> Heterogeneous reaction Atmospheric chemical reactions occurring on the surface of a liquid- or solid-phase particle.
> Homogeneous reaction Atmospheric chemical reactions occurring purely in the gas phase.
> Isotopologue Molecules of the same species that differ only in their isotopic composition, for example 16
> O 3 , 17

> 18 O16 O2 for ozone.
> Isotopomer Molecules of the same species and same isotopic composition but differing in their submolecular positions,
> for example 18 O–16 O–16 O and 16 O–18
> O–16 O for the 18 O16 O2 isotopologue of ozone.
> Mass-dependent fractionation (MDF) Any process that fractionates in three-isotope space with a relationship that is
> controlled primarily by the mass of the isotopologues of interest. These include most kinetic and equilibrium reactions (Eq. 3).
> Mass-independent fractionation (MIF) Any process that fractionates in three-isotope space with a relationship that is not
> controlled by isotopologue mass.
> Ro-vibrational/fine structure Some molecules have both rotational and vibrational (ro-vibrational) energy levels in their
> bonds that are not available to atomic species. Changes in vibrational energy levels generally give rise to broad absorption
> bands within a molecular spectrum. Changes in rotational energy levels are generally much smaller near some resonant
> frequencies, and are said to express as “fine-structure” on the vibrational bands in the absorption cross section.
> Self-shielding A MIF-generating process by which photons that dissociate the major isotopologue (e.g., 12 C16 O for CO)
> become attenuated within a gas column, leaving only photons available to dissociate the minor isotopologues (e.g., 12 C17 O
> and 12 C18 O for CO). Self-shielding occurs when isotopologues have distinct structure in their photodissociation bands
> (see Fig. 1 for example), but only if there is a sufficient abundance (or path-length) of the major isotopologue to fully absorb
> specific photons in one portion of the column.
> Symmetric/asymmetric isotopomers Independent of the bulk isotope composition, symmetric isotopomers are those with
> terminal atoms of the same mass (e.g., 16
> O–18 O–16
> O and 16 O–17 O–16 O) whereas asymmetric isotopomers are those with
> terminal atoms of different masses (e.g., 16 O–16 O–18 O and 16
> O–16 O–17
> O).

> Introduction
> Geochemists have long attempted to constrain the composition of past and present atmospheres, often using the most recently
> discovered—and thus least well understood—proxies available. One of these proxies, MIF signatures, arises nearly exclusively in the
> atmosphere and can be permanently preserved in the geologic record, subject only to dilution in magnitude. We therefore contend
> that mass-independent isotope signals represent the only direct, and therefore strongest, atmospheric proxies discovered to date,
> especially in the deep geologic past. This is particularly true for the two most well-studied and ubiquitous MIF-containing elements:
> oxygen and sulfur. Inferences drawn from MIF signals discussed here are further strengthened by the fact that oxygen and sulfur
> biogeochemical cycles—including major isotope effects—have been extremely well characterized over the past century (Hoefs,
> 2021; Hoefs and Harmon, 2022); this is a foundational requirement for our ability to interpret past atmospheric compositions
> using MIF. Given this long history, here we focus on processes that occur in the Earth’s atmosphere, with an emphasis on those that
> transfer into the geologic record.

> Photons effects and self-shielding
> When a molecule gains energy by absorbing a photon (denoted “hn”) of a given wavelength, l, the excited-state molecule will either
> dissociate (photolyze) or eventually de-excite through collision with another molecule or reemission of a photon. Determining how
> long a molecule remains in the excited state—as well as the specific wavelength, pressure, and temperature constraints on the
> product yields—broadly defines the rich experimental field of atmospheric chemical kinetics. In most cases, atmospheric kinetic
> reaction rates are not affected by isotopic substitutions within a given molecule, with the exception of kinetic isotope effects (KIE)
> that can give rise to mass-dependent fractionation (MDF; Urey, 1947). Atmospheric MIF therefore appears to be restricted
> predominantly to molecules with ro-vibrational fine structure, which enables different isotopologues to differentially absorb and
> process light. A subset of these molecules additionally exhibit subtle quantum properties that allow for curve-crossing between
> excited states, which ultimately enables different excited-state lifetimes for various isotopomers. These two styles of quantum
> deviations lead to nonstandard kinetic rates and ultimately give rise to MIF (additional details on their physical chemistry has been
> discussed in recent reviews by Thiemens et al., 2012; Thiemens and Lin, 2019, 2021).
> In its broadest definition, “shielding” occurs when there is photon limitation as light travels through a medium with more than
> one molecule. In the first part of the path where photons are abundant, all molecules will absorb photons of wavelength l. At some
> point in the path, the molecule with the strongest absorption cross section at a given wavelength might absorb all available photons.
> Beyond that point in the path, all other molecules are defined to be “shielded” by the first, as there are no more photons available at
> wavelength l for them to absorb. Shielding is therefore ubiquitous in any atmospheric column, given the multitude of molecules
> present. At wavelengths shorter than 190 nm, the entirety of the lower atmosphere is shielded by CO2, N2, and O2. Due to solar
> physics, there are fewer photons of these wavelengths (Claire et al., 2012), and the sheer abundance of these molecules combined with
> their strong absorption cross sections absorbs all photons of these energies above 100 km from the surface. Indeed, this observation
> is why we limit our consideration of atmospheric MIF generation to molecules with fine structure in the UV or visible light.

> Nuclear spin effects
> While physiochemical details are beyond the scope of this review, some isotope effects occur due to changes in nuclear spin, which
> can induce mass-independent effects in elements with odd numbers of nucleons (Buchachenko, 2018; Thiemens and Lin, 2021).
> These occur mainly in the solid and liquid states, although they have been induced in gas-phase experiments. Such so-called
> magnetic effects are known to occur in 33
> S (Oduro et al., 2011; Kopf and Ono, 2012) and in 199/201 Hg (Motta et al., 2020), but
> luckily are distinguishable from atmospheric MIF due to their noneffect on even-numbered nucleons. As discussed in detail below,
> effects involving 17 O in the atmosphere appear to be distinct from—or are much greater in magnitude than—any magnetic effect.

> outside the scope of this review). The first observations of O-MIF in natural stratospheric ozone by Mauersberger (1987) initiated

> underlying mechanism(s) leading to O-MIF in atmospheric species was debated for much of the 1990s but has since been accepted
> to result from differences in ro-vibrational states for symmetric vs. asymmetric ozone isotopomers (e.g., Gao and Marcus, 2001;
> Ivanov and Babikov, 2013). Assuming that there is one primary O-MIF-generation reaction, the propagation of this isotope
> signature through other atmospheric gases has become a fundamental tracer that constrains atmospheric oxidation pathways.
> More recently, the focus of O-MIF studies has mainly turned toward attempting to reconstruct primary production and atmospheric
> compositions in the deep geologic past, particularly using geologically preserved sulfate minerals.

> observations performed on several atmospheric gases (Section “Experimental observations”). We then discuss past and current
> theories to explain the underlying MIF-inducing mechanism (Section “Theory and mechanism”) followed by a summary of all

> Experimental observations
> Thiemens and Heidenreich (1983) and Heidenreich and Thiemens (1983) provided the first laboratory-based evidence for
> chemically produced O-MIF in atmospheric gases. By dissociating O2 using electrical discharge and measuring the produced
> O3 as well as the residual O 2, their experiments directly showed that both species evolve along a fractionation line with a mass-
> independent slope in a d018 O vs. d017
> O triple–isotope plot of b ¼ 1.0 (Fig. 2A). The discovery of chemically produced O-MIF
> sparked a revolution in the study of atmospheric gas isotopes and led to experimental observations that outpaced the understanding
> of underlying O-MIF theory and mechanism for a decade. Several follow-up experiments expanded upon the original electric
> discharge results (Heidenreich and Thiemens, 1986; Morton et al., 1990) and similarly investigated ozone production by
> microwave O 2 dissociation (Bains-Sahota and Thiemens, 1987), O2 photodissociation (Thiemens and Jackson, 1987, 1988,
> 1990; Morton et al., 1990; Feilberg et al., 2013), and water electrolysis (Bhattacharya et al., 2009). As seen in Figs. 2A and 3A,
> oxygen was observed to fractionate mass-independently in all cases except for water electrolysis (electric discharge: b ¼ 0.81  0.01,
> r2 ¼ 0.994, n ¼ 70; microwave dissociation: b ¼ 0.85  0.01, r2 ¼ 0.990, n ¼ 93; photodissociation: b ¼ 0.50  0.01, r2 ¼ 0.992,
> n ¼ 22; water electrolysis: b ¼ 0.50  0.01, r2 ¼ 0.992, n ¼ 22). While all fractionations are mass independent (except for water
> electrolysis), the fractionation magnitude was shown to depend on experimental conditions such as pressure and bath gas
> composition (Feilberg et al., 2013). Additionally, the exact triple–isotope slope b was observed to vary between experiments,
> potentially due to the presence of several mass-dependent and MIF steps in the reaction (Bains-Sahota and Thiemens, 1987).
> In addition to ozone generation, O-MIF was subsequently observed in thermal (Bhattacharya and Thiemens, 1988; Wen and
> Thiemens, 1990, 1991) and some photolytic (Bhattacharya and Thiemens, 1988; Wen and Thiemens, 1991; Chakraborty and
> Bhattacharya, 2003; Früchtl et al., 2015b,a; Huang et al., 2019) ozone dissociation experiments (thermal: b ¼ 0.99  0.03,
> r2¼ 0.962, n ¼ 60; photo: b ¼ 0.65  0.01, r2 ¼ 0.916, n ¼ 181). Although both types of experiments can result in MIF
> (Figs. 2B and 3A), thermal dissociation was largely shown to produce resulting O2 that is isotopically heavier than starting
> ozone, whereas early photolytic dissociation experiments produced O2 that is isotopically lighter than starting ozone. Thermal
> dissociation results were again interpreted to reflect the presence of several mass-dependent and MIF effects operating in parallel
> (Wen and Thiemens, 1990), and O-MIF was suggested to result from the initial O3 decomposition step of the reaction (Wen and
> Thiemens, 1991). Furthermore, the mass-dependent vs. mass-independent nature of photolytic ozone dissociation was shown to
> depend strongly on wavelength; broadband experiments with cutoff filters at 455, 550, and 620 nm as well as narrowband light
> sources at 530, 617, and 660 nm (i.e., within the Chappuis band) all fractionate mass-dependently (Fig. 3B; Früchtl et al., 2015b).
> In general, wavelength exhibits a large control on fractionation for several experiments in the Hartley and Chappuis bands, with
> results that deviated significantly from concurrent theoretical predictions (Huang et al., 2019). These results point to the need for
> more coupled experimental and theoretical studies on ozone dissociation.

> Oceanographic and geologic applications
> Quaternary and modern gross productivity estimates
> Luz et al. (1999) first proposed that the known balance between O-MIF-inducing photochemistry in the stratosphere and
> mass-dependent O2 overprinting in the biosphere (Section “Atmospheric observations”; Fig. 6) could be used to quantify global
> GPP fluxes. That is, if the mass-dependent slope for O2 consumption by respiration, bresp , is known precisely and if the magnitude of
> the stratospheric O2 O-MIF can be estimated accurately, then any deviations from this stratospheric value can be used to quantify
> global GPP. Luz et al. (1999) applied this approach to O2 gas trapped in Greenland glacier ice bubbles—critically allowing the
> stratospheric O-MIF signal to change as a function of pCO 2—and concluded that GPP has increased by 10% over the past 82 ka.
> This approach was also utilized by Blunier et al. (2002), who estimated that GPP flux was 17%–24% lower than today during the
> last glacial maximum and likely lower than today during the last interglacial. More recently, Yang et al. (2022) extended this result
> by reporting an 800,000 year record of glacial ice bubble O2 triple-oxygen isotopes; they determined that GPP fluxes were lower
> than today in the past eight glacial periods and that GPP has typically increased several millennia prior to deglaciation. In contrast,
> using a more sophisticated stratospheric O-MIF model, Young et al. (2014) concluded that the entire D017 O variability observed by
> Blunier et al. (2002) can be explained purely by glacial–interglacial changes in pCO 2 without the need to invoke GPP variability,
> thus urging caution when quantitatively estimating global GPP changes through time.
> In addition to global estimates, D017 O values of O 2 in the surface ocean can quantitatively constrain local marine productivity
> (see Juranek and Quay, 2013, for review). First described by Luz and Barkan (2000), this approach utilizes the fact that dissolved
> O2 represents a mixture of tropospheric O2 infiltration by gas exchange, with a D017 OyRL ¼0:5305 value of −0.501  0.012 (Wostbrock
> et al., 2020), and new O2 produced by primary production, with a D017 OyRL ¼0:5305 value equal to the seawater value of zero
> (assuming no fractionation during photosynthesis). Observed dissolved O 2 D017 O values (after correcting for respiration that
> fractionates along a slope bresp ) thus reflect the mixture of these two sources and, using independent gas exchange constraints, can
> be used to quantitatively calculate GPP, with results integrating over several week timescales (e.g., Luz and Barkan, 2000, 2005,
> 2009; Nicholson et al., 2012).

> A paleo-sulfate record of past atmospheric compositions
> Perhaps the most geologically profound application of triple-oxygen isotope measurements is that of the paleo-sulfate minerals
> barite (BaSO4 ), evaporitic gypsum/anhydrite (CaSO 4 ), and carbonate-associated sulfate (CAS). These minerals are canonically
> interpreted as faithful recorders of past tropospheric O2 isotope compositions (e.g., Bao et al., 2008, 2009; Crockford et al., 2018a;
> Hodgskiss et al., 2019), which are in turn controlled by pO 2 /pCO 2 levels and/or GPP fluxes (Section “Molecular oxygen”; Fig. 6).
> The primary mechanism by which sulfate is thought to inherit a negative O-MIF signal is via oxidative weathering on land of the
> iron-sulfide mineral pyrite (FeS 2 ), which in its most simple form proceeds as

> Some experimental results support the interpretation that 10%–30% of sulfate oxygen is derived directly from O2 , although
> the exact proportion is thought to depend on environmental conditions such as solution pH, Fe 3+ concentrations, and microbial

> other pyrite oxidation experiments argue against the direct incorporation of O2 into product sulfate. These studies rather highlight
> the importance of two classes of intermediate species: (i) sulfoxyanions (e.g., S2 O 32− and SO 32−), which are generated on the pyrite

> low-pO 2 Proterozoic Eon; Gu et al., 2020; Shaughnessy et al., 2023). Furthermore, modern weathering environments whose sulfate
> is known to be sourced predominantly by pyrite oxidation yield SO 42− with a slight positive, rather than negative, O-MIF signal; this
> was interpreted as resulting from either H2 O 2 incorporation or kinetic fractionation effects (Hemingway et al., 2020; Cao and Bao,

> Applications: Sulfur MIF
> Although first measured prior to O-MIF (Hulston and Thode, 1965), S-MIF is just entering its third decade of intensive study. The
> first principles understanding of S-MIF lags behind that of O-MIF, but there is a much larger database of measurements available
> (13,000 data points; Caruso et al., 2017; Uveges et al., 2023; Claire, 2024). This substantial data gathering effort was prompted by
> the observation of S-MIF in sediments from early Earth (Farquhar et al., 2000a) and subsequently in ice cores (Savarino et al.,
> 2003b). While there exists a unique subfield using subducted and metamorphosed S-MIF samples as tracers of crustal assimilation,
> magmatism, and contact metamorphism (e.g., Farquhar et al., 2002; Penniston-Dorland et al., 2008; Thomassot et al., 2015;

> paleo-atmospheric compositions. Equally fascinating S-MIF results from meteorites (Farquhar et al., 2000b; Rai and Thiemens,
> 2007; Labidi et al., 2017; Antonelli et al., 2014; Defouilloy et al., 2016), the Moon (Dottin et al., 2023), and Mars (Farquhar et al.,

> Experiments and theory
> The fundamental mechanism(s) that enable MIF of sulfur are not understood at the quantum level and are not yet predictable by
> first principles (Thiemens et al., 2012; Thiemens and Lin, 2019, 2021). Like nearly all of isotope geochemistry, S-MIF is
> fundamentally a measurable observed property with interpretations stemming from experiments. Over the past 20 years, the global
> interpretative structure of S-MIF has not progressed significantly beyond that of an “on/off” switch for reducing atmospheric
> conditions (see Section “Interpretations”), even given substantial data collection and experimental work. Natural and experimen-
> tally generated samples exhibit wide variations in magnitude, sign, and depositional expression of both D33
> S and D36
> S, as well as
> relationships between isotope ratios such as d34 S vs. D33 S and D33
> S vs. D36 S. These variations inspire hope for significant
> interpretative advances as the community better understands the generation of S-MIF fractionation factors.

> Production and preservation
> In the modern atmosphere, several processes conspire against both the production of S-MIF and its preservation. First, SO 2
> photolysis is restricted to photons with wavelengths less than l ¼ 220 nm, which are mostly absorbed in the stratosphere via the
> Chapman reactions that create and destroy ozone (Section “Theory and mechanism”). In this sense, ozone shields most tropo-
> spheric SO 2 from photolysis (see Section “Photons effects and self-shielding”). Even if SO 2 is photolyzed, the most common fate of
> product SO is the three-body recombination reaction
> SO + O + M ! SO 2 + M, (18)
> which reverses the photolytic destruction as well as any isotopic scrambling. Other quantitatively important SO reactions (e.g., SO3 +
> SO, SO + O2, SO + SO) also reform SO2. This logic additionally applies to any S-MIF generated via 3
> SO2 since its primary fate is
> conversion back to ground-state SO2, which seemingly prevents any instantaneously large fractionations arising from 3
> SO2 from
> propagating into the global atmosphere (Claire et al., 2014). Furthermore, the eventual fate of all modern SO2 emission is oxidation to
> gaseous SO3 or aqueous H2SO4, which condenses to sulfate aerosols (Section “Sulfate aerosols”; Langmann, 2014). Given the entire
> reservoir of atmospheric sulfur gases is ultimately converted to a single sulfate aerosol “exit channel,” isotopic mass balance constraints
> during reservoir conversion prevent the preservation of any modern global S-MIF signal.
> In contrast to the modern atmosphere with severely restricted S-MIF preservation, an O 2 - and ozone-free atmosphere would
> enhance both the production and preservation of S-MIF. Concerning S-MIF production, a lack of O 3 would limit shielding and thus
> lead to S-MIF production by tropospheric SO 2 photolysis. Concerning S-MIF preservation, sulfate aerosols can still form in reducing
> oxygen-free conditions, but this formation would compete with an additional chemical network of sulfur polymerization, leading
> to multiple atmospheric sulfur exit channels with differing isotopic ratios. In this process, recombination reactions such as S + S +
> M !S 2 + M, S + S 2 + M !S 3 + M occur in the gas phase; these culminate in the condensation and deposition of S 8 aerosol, a
> stable cyclic molecule (e.g., Babikov, 2017). Thus, the continuous presence of S-MIF in ancient sediments (Section “Geologic
> record”) is widely interpreted as evidence of an oxygen-free atmosphere in the geologic past (Pavlov and Kasting, 2002,
> see Section “Interpretations”).

> Despite substantial technical complexity, multiple-sulfur isotope data collection remains significantly ahead of our capacity for data
> interpretation, although new experiments are starting to close this gap. Here, we primarily discuss bulk-ice and -rock data, which are

> 1965; Gao and Thiemens, 1991; Farquhar and Wing, 2003) or by multicollector inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry
> (MC-ICP-MS) on aqueous sulfate (Fischer et al., 2014; Rodiouchkina et al., 2023). The SO IRMS and sulfate MC-ICP-MS methods

> larger uncertainty than classic SF 6 IRMS techniques (Burke et al., 2021). All results reported here are calibrated against the IAEA-S1

> slightly different DS values (Endo et al., 2024), although differences are within the mass-dependent range and are thus insignificant
> compared to the large-magnitude S-MIF considered here (Ono, 2017).

> S-MIF as tracer of past volcanic activity
> Volcanic plumes provide a singular modern mechanism to understand S-MIF arising from SO 2 photolysis and self-shielding. Plume
> observations generally indicate that SO 2 and H2 S injections are fully oxidized to sulfate within weeks; thus, all volcanic S-MIF must
> be quickly generated subsequent to eruption. Sulfate with variable S-MIF signatures evidently remains in spatially separate
> atmospheric reservoirs during years of global transport. Temporal patterns observed in snow and ice reflect slow sulfate settling
> from the polar stratosphere to the polar troposphere, implying that the first-appearing S-MIF signatures reflect a combination of
> sulfate that is (i) transported most rapidly to polar regions and (ii) generated closest to the troposphere, whereas later-emerging
> signatures reflect a combination of longer transport and higher altitudes. If this conceptual picture is correct, it is possible that
> eruptions with smaller sulfur loads might lead to differential patterns in opposite hemispheres, whereas larger eruptions are more
> likely to generate a symmetric response (Burke et al., 2019). Furthermore, temporal variations in volcanic S-MIF magnitude, sign,
> and slope (Fig. 10) may encode additional information on plume height, paleo-latitude, and/or sulfur load, although any such
> interpretation first requires a better understanding of fractionation factors (Burke et al., 2019).
> Given the complete oxidation of erupted SO 2 to sulfate in the modern atmosphere (i.e., a single exit channel), isotopic mass
> balance provides another constraint on plume dynamics. Specifically, the integrated isotopic composition resulting from the entire
> eruption must equal the isotopic composition of volcanic SO 2 , which is mass-dependent (Mather et al., 2006). Thus, a lack of
> isotopic mass balance in ice cores would indicate a low-altitude eruption with significant S-MIF rainout to the ocean prior to
> polar transport, whereas the complete preservation of isotopic mass balance must reflect a purely stratospheric eruption. The
> preservation in ice cores of S-MIF from volcanic eruptions is therefore enabled exclusively by the spatiotemporal separation of
> signals in a rapidly accumulating depositional environment. Beyond ice cores, other rapidly depositing environments such as
> shallow lakes in the plume path have tephra, which preserves sulfate O-MIF and potentially S-MIF signals (Martin et al., 2014).
> These environments—along with direct plume samples—represent an exciting target for future study.
> In addition to SO 2 photodeexcitation and longer-term integrated aerosol deposition from the oxic atmosphere, complete
> conversion also homogenizes S-MIF signals during SO 2 recombination following photolysis. That is, the above discussion
> hypothesizes a temporal and vertical separation and stratification of sulfate aerosols with unique S-MIF signals; an alternative
> interpretation involves a stronger role for kinetics. Whitehill et al. (2015) hypothesized that the SO + O 2 + M !SO 3 + M three-
> body reaction could capture some of the opposite-magnitude S-MIF encoded in SO generated by SO 2 photolysis (Eq. 17) into
> resulting SO 3 and sulfate aerosol. However, this reaction rate has only been quantified as an upper limit and was shown to be
> unimportant for global sulfate aerosol production (Black et al., 1982). Nevertheless, if incorporated into box models at a rate near
> this upper limit, this three-body reaction can affect predicted S-MIF magnitude of the sulfate aerosol (Whitehill et al., 2015; Endo
> et al., 2022b). Although current box models are not capable of predicting S-MIF from the spatial separation hypothesis, it is clear
> that confirmation of this hypothetical reaction pathway coupled with an absolute measurement of the low- and high-pressure
> reaction rates are an important research target. Regardless of how it acquires S-MIF, the observed temporal data provide evidence
> that stratospheric sulfate aerosol remains partially altitude stratified since this signal does not completely remix before transfer to
> the troposphere and subsequent rapid deposition.
> Nonvolcanic S-MIF
> Beyond volcanism, there exist biogenic and anthropogenic pathways involving OCS that lead to sulfur gas transfer to the
> stratosphere. First, degradation of eukaryotic algae produces methylated sulfur compounds such as DMS and methylsulfonic
> acid. Once released into the atmosphere, the biogenic sulfur is rapidly converted to either sulfate aerosol, which rapidly rains
> out, or OCS. The only significant destruction reaction for OCS is photolysis by photons with l < 290 nm, which are shielded in the
> modern atmosphere by O3 . Because OCS is a stable molecule whose oxidative destruction rates are negligible at Earth-surface
> conditions, its concentrations slowly accumulate in the atmosphere. Once OCS drifts above the ozone layer however, it is rapidly
> photolyzed and oxidized, where it forms sulfate aerosol in the high-altitude Junge layer (Seinfeld and Pandis, 2016). Because this
> oxidation includes an SO 2 intermediate, Junge-layer sulfate may include S-MIF, although this has not yet been directly measured.
> Second, large-scale anthropogenic biomass burning also produces OCS; observed S-MIF in snowpits from nonvolcanic years has
> been interpreted to reflect biomass burning coupled with climate-induced changes in photochemistry (Shaheen et al., 2014;

> S record is shown in Fig. 11. Sulfide data prior to the Neoarchean Era (2800–2500 Ma) display an apparent
> asymmetry, with preserved positive D33 S values exhibiting up to 5 higher magnitude than negative values. Several studies have
> examined this potential mass balance issue, focusing on processes which might preferentially transfer negative D33 S to the mantle
> (Farquhar et al., 2002; Farquhar and Wing, 2003; Gaillard et al., 2011; Farquhar et al., 2014). Although certainly possible, a
> “missing sink” of negative D33
> S is not a required conclusion from the geologic observations for four reasons: First, there is no
> requirement that both positively and negatively fractionated reservoirs will be preserved in the permanent geologic record. Using the
> oxygen example discussed in Section “Applications: Oxygen MIF,” most of the positive O-MIF signal remains in the instantaneous
> gas-phase O3 reservoir, and exhibits only small exit channels of soluble, oxidized end members such as nitrate, sulfate, and
> perchlorate. These exit channels do not yield isotopic mass balance, and their paths to enter the permanent geologic record are
> generally rare. Second, both fractionated reservoirs may exhibit drastically different sizes. Again using modern oxygen as an
> example, fractionation during ozone formation is expressed as a large positive O-MIF in the quantitatively small ozone reservoir
> coupled with an extremely small negative O-MIF in the much larger O2 reservoir (Fig. 5). The observation of only negative O-MIF
> in most of the geologic record (Fig. 8) is thus not interpreted to require a missing geologic sink of positive O-MIF. By analogy, the
> exit channel(s) preserving Neoarchean sulfide with negative D33 S could simply be larger than the exit channel(s) carrying positive
> D33
> S, although this is not without its own complications (see Section “Interpretations”). Third, the large-magnitude D33
> S samples
> represent less than 5% of the total Neoarchean dataset, with the majority of data falling between −1% VCDT < D33 S < 1% VCDT;
> this asymmetry can thus at least partially arise from temporal and spatial sampling bias (Ono, 2017; Uveges et al., 2023).

> sedimenting depositional environments requires an explanation other than repeated explosive stratospheric eruptions into an
> otherwise quiescent oxic atmosphere (Ohmoto, 2020). This is because sulfur injections into oxic atmospheres produce temporal
> S-MIF signals that integrate to zero over timescales of years (Section “Ice-core records”; Fig. 10), orders of magnitude shorter than the

> events. These events record S-MIF values distinct from bulk Archean sulfide signals; they may illuminate how eruptive S-MIF mixes
> with the quiescent background chemistry that produces the majority of the Archean record. Additional data from terrestrial
> depositional environments will help to further constrain volcanism, as these samples may not be subject to the same biospheric
> alteration pathways experienced by most marine sulfur. Beyond data collection, theoretical models of S-MIF resulting from Archean
> volcanic eruptions should provide interesting areas for future work.

> Interpretations
> Despite challenges, the primary interpretations drawn from the S-MIF record have not changed dramatically in two decades of study
> (Farquhar et al., 2000a, 2001; Pavlov and Kasting, 2002; Farquhar and Wing, 2003). Most importantly, the presence of S-MIF in the
> rock record before 2300 Ma appears to be an unequivocal indicator of widespread reducing conditions in the atmosphere, and its
> absence from the subsequent geological record indicates a switch to oxic conditions. Initial modeling placed the upper limit of
> pO 2 that preserves S-MIF at 10−5 present atmospheric level (PAL; Pavlov and Kasting, 2002). More recent detailed modeling has
> reduced this S-MIF upper limit to 10−6 to 10−7 PAL (Ono et al., 2003; Zahnle et al., 2006; Gregory et al., 2021). This oxygen
> “on/off” switch appears to be mediated by positive feedbacks driven by the formation of ozone resulting from increased pO 2 (Claire
> et al., 2006; Goldblatt et al., 2006; Gregory et al., 2021; Wogan et al., 2022). New theory has revealed that atmospheres with
> intermediate pO 2 (i.e., 10−7 PAL < pO 2 < 10−3 PAL) are unstable against small flux perturbations and are thus unlikely to persist
> for geologically long intervals (Gregory et al., 2021; Wogan et al., 2022).
> The interpretative power of S-MIF relies entirely on experimental data. While the pO 2 quantification of the “on/off” switch is
> derived from theoretical atmospheric chemistry models, those interpretations were inspired by the experimental observation of
> S-MIF arising from short-wavelength SO 2 photolysis, which demonstrated variable S-MIF isotope arrays under varying wavelengths
> (Farquhar et al., 2001). Although often interpreted more broadly, initial experiments were performed at extremely high SO 2
> pressures (Masterson et al., 2011; Ono et al., 2013), leading to evidence of self-shielding (Lyons, 2007; Endo et al., 2019), and with
> narrowband lasers to access specific portions of the SO 2 cross section (Claire et al., 2014). Their comparison to geologic data is thus
> arbitrary. Subsequent experiments using broadband—but still not solar-spectrum—light and lower SO 2 partial pressures revealed
> different S-MIF signals stemming from photochemistry of trace SO 2 mixed with background gases such as N 2 , CO 2 , and CO
> (Masterson et al., 2011; Ono et al., 2013; Whitehill and Ono, 2012; Whitehill et al., 2013, 2015; Endo et al., 2019). Still, no single
> experiment to date can predict the fractionation factors observed in the rock record. Photochemical experiments conducted with
> hypothesized levels of Archean reductants and/or with shortwave solar spectrum remain lacking and represent interesting
> research targets.
> Experiments carried out over the past two decades have also illuminated details of the SO 2 absorption cross sections
> (Section “Sulfur dioxide”). High-pressure experiments with large optical SO 2 depths reveal patterns of self-shielding, which vary
> as pressure drops toward optically thin conditions (Masterson et al., 2011; Ono et al., 2013; Endo et al., 2019). Attempts to
> extrapolate the highest resolution natural abundance SO 2 cross sections (Stark et al., 1999) to isotopologue-specific cross sections
> using a wavelength-dependent redshift to account for zero-point energy are unable to explain the self-shielding data (Lyons, 2007;
> Endo et al., 2019). An explanation for the failure of the simple “wavelength shift” model may lie in the lower resolution cross
> section measurements, which unexpectedly reveal different values of the absolute cross section peaks (Fig. 9A; Danielache et al.,
> 2008; Endo et al., 2015, 2022a). If confirmed, true “peak-height shift” in the SO 2 cross section would enable S-MIF in optically thin
> radiative transfer, even in the absence of additional hypothesized quantum effects from curve-crossing prior to dissociation
> (Thiemens and Lin, 2021). Pressure-dependent measurements (Endo et al., 2019) reveal that the finest-level structure evident in
> the highest resolution measurements is expressed only when pressure is <0.1 bar; this structure is progressively eliminated by
> pressure broadening at higher values (Fig. 9C). Tropospheric SO 2 thus exhibits a different cross section to incoming radiation from
> SO 2 in the stratosphere, and thus differing S-MIF patterns should be expected based on the altitude of photolysis. Experiments
> conducted at stratospheric temperatures reveal mass-dependent effects but with substantial variations in dS magnitudes (Endo
> et al., 2022b) that might further diagnose stratospheric from tropospheric S-MIF. Fits to the experimental pressure-dependent data
> enable some decoupling of self-shielding effects from optically thin SO 2 processes in experimental results. These provide an
> extrapolation between existing medium-resolution isotopologue-specific and high-resolution natural abundance cross section
> data (Fig. 9).
> Complete energy loss to the ground state following SO 2 photoexcitation prevents much of the high-magnitude S-MIF generated
> during (de)excitation from propagating into the atmosphere. This ultimately results from the observation that kinetic reaction rates
> of photoexcited SO 2 with other species modeled to date (i.e., O2 , SO 2) are small compared to competing kinetic pathways (i.e.,
> collisional deexcitation, fluorescence) that promote deexcitation to ground-state SO 2 (Claire et al., 2014). Furthermore, 3 SO 2 also
> reacts with CO and hydrocarbons (Heicklen et al., 1980); these reactions may have represented important S-MIF propagation
> pathways if the atmospheric CO (Ueno et al., 2009; Endo et al., 2016) and/or hydrocarbon (Pavlov et al., 2001; Domagal-Goldman
> EARTH’S SURFACE ENVELOPE: FRESHWATER, GROUNDWATER, AND ATMOSPHERE | Mass-independent fractionation 529
>
> ---
>
> et al., 2008; Zerkle et al., 2012) reservoirs were larger in the Archean. Kinetic reaction rates for photoexcited SO 2 species are thus a
> prime target for additional experimental and theoretical study.
> An additional component of the primary S-MIF interpretation involves the existence of two atmospheric end members—sulfate,
> which carries negative D33
> S signals, and elemental/polysulfur, which carries positive D33
> S signals (Farquhar et al., 2001). Despite
> some serendipity in the original formulation (Claire et al., 2014), this interpretation appears to reflect the nature of SO 2 photolysis
> products (Endo et al., 2016; Whitehill et al., 2015; Endo et al., 2022a; Franz et al., 2013; Masterson et al., 2011), which result in SO
> with positive D33 S and residual SO 2 reservoir with negative D33 S. Experiments suggest that an additional organosulfur aerosol exit
> channel (Reed et al., 2022; DeWitt et al., 2010; Oduro et al., 2023) would require some revision of the ancient sulfur cycle; it is thus
> an important target for future research.
> In summary, interpretive structure has been placed over the presence/absence of S-MIF as well as the primary signs of the
> assumed atmospheric end members. The substantial variations in magnitudes and D36
> S/D33 S and D33 S/d34
> S slopes suggest that
> additional constraints on atmospheric composition remain to be uncovered as the community works toward a better understanding
> of fractionation factors. Many studies seek to explain observed D36
> S/D33 S slopes as linear combinations of various experimental
> D36
> S/D33 S slopes and/or by correlation with other proxies, but conclusions based on these arguments must be seen as speculative
> given that individual experiments do not represent the complexity of processes occurring and mixing in the atmosphere. What does
> appear clear is that the Archean S-MIF record predominately captures optically thin SO 2 photolysis (Lyons, 2009), with potential
> minor contributions from other S-MIF source reactions (Farquhar et al., 2001; Endo et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2022). Finally, the
> bulk of the Archean record is described by −1% VCDT < D33 S < 1% VCDT; this implies substantial biospheric involvement via
> sulfate reduction, sulfide reoxidation, and sulfur disproportionation, which act to homogenize atmospheric S-MIF signals by
> postdepositional mixing (Halevy, 2013). This is further evidenced by a decrease in D33
> S with increased d34 S (Fig. 12B), suggesting
> that atmospherically produced, large-magnitude D33
> S has a relatively small d34
> S range.
> Applications: Mercury and selenium MIF
> MIF generated from atmospheric mercury is a frontier field; we summarize this phenomenon here in anticipation of—and
> motivation for—substantial work in the coming decade. Mercury has seven isotopes. Their six isotope ratios relative to 198 Hg
> reflect multiple mass-dependent biological transitions, abiotic reactions, and physical processes in the natural environment
> (Bergquist and Blum, 2007; Blum et al., 2014). Four distinct three-isotope pairings (D199 Hg, D201 Hg, D200
> Hg, and D204 Hg) are
> commonly used, although significant challenges in quantifying d204 Hg limit its utility. Of these, D199 Hg and D201 Hg are the most
> studied, and they appear to arise from interactions with photons in the aqueous phase. These “odd mercury MIFs” are interpreted to
> arise from magnetic isotope and nuclear volume effects (Section “Nuclear spin effects”). Despite important usages in tracing surface
> processes and pollution remediation, they are not gas-phase tracers so will not be further discussed here.
> “Even mercury MIF” has been observed in the stratosphere (Cai and Chen, 2016), but our understanding of mercury photo-
> chemistry and MIF-generating mechanisms is still developing (Lyman et al., 2020). The only experimentally demonstrated
> production mechanism for D200 Hg and D204 Hg to date has been in the gas phase (Sun et al., 2022) and is potentially attributed
> to physiochemical spin characteristics of even number nucleons. Although more research is certainly needed, there is hope that
> D200
> Hg and D204 Hg/D200
> Hg might join D17
> O, D33 S, and D36 S/D33
> S as fundamental tracers of atmospheric chemistry. Intriguingly,
> Archean data from intervals of known D36 S/D33
> S variations reveal complicated variations with D200 Hg (Zerkle et al., 2020, 2021;
> Meixnerová et al., 2021), hinting that mercury MIF might provide additional constraints on atmospheric chemistry when coupled
> with S- or O-MIF variations.
> While mass-dependent selenium isotope variations have revealed interesting features of modern biogeochemistry (Stüeken,
> 2017), Se-MIF has not been detected to date (Pogge Von Strandmann et al., 2014). One potential explanation is that gas-phase
> selenium chemistry is rapid and has limited to no interactions with photons. Biogenic dimethyl selenide (DMSe; Me–Se–Me) is the
> most common gas-phase selenium molecule, but unlike its sulfur analog DMS, DMSe is rapidly oxidized and rained out from the
> troposphere with an estimated atmospheric lifetime of <6 h (Wen and Carignan, 2007). The primary inorganic gas-phase selenium
> molecule H 2 Se, like the more common H 2 S, does not appear to present fine structure in available (although very low resolution)
> absorption cross section measurements (Goodeve and Stein, 1931). SeO2 , unlike SO 2 whose photolysis is known to generate MIF, is
> a solid at Earth-surface conditions, so any SeO2 formed by oxidation of DMSe or H2 Se rapidly condenses to the particulate/aqueous
> phase. Despite the potential for atmospheric Se-MIF to arise within three “even” three-isotope pairings, we speculate that natural
> Se-MIF is more likely to occur only in the “odd” three-isotope pairing D77
> Se as a result of magnetic isotope and nuclear volume
> effects (Section “Nuclear spin effects”).
> Concluding remarks and future directions
> The fields of O-, S-, and Hg-MIF are in different phases of discovery, but can inform each other. Each field was sparked by advances
> in mass-spectrometry leading to unexpected discovery in natural samples. Early interpretations quickly become adopted, and
> lengthy experimental phases are needed to test these interpretations, allowing new interpretations emerge from limited experimen-
> tal datasets. Ultimately, fundamental experiments converge on mechanisms causing fractionation, as well as the associated chemical

>

>
> kinetics needed to accurately predict atmospheric expression of MIF. Data collection from natural samples outpaces the funda-
> mental knowledge base, leading to complicated interactions between interpretations of data and ongoing experimental work. All
> three of these fields require and will benefit from additional experimental work to constrain process and reduce uncertainty on
> interpretations. Compared to oxygen with its three stable isotopes, the sulfur and mercury systems have additional isotope(s) and
> more variations within their sample sets. These isotope systems thus exhibit the most untapped potential for constraining
> additional components of atmospheric chemistry. Even Hg-MIF is a high priority for future MIF study, with an emphasis on
> elucidating mechanism as well as confirmation of solely gas-phase occurrence. The O 3 molecule conspires to prevent the
> atmosphere from simultaneously producing O- and S-MIF in most cases, but if even Hg-MIF remains a true atmospheric indicator,
> combination studies of Hg-MIF with O- or S-MIF would represent a frontier direction with the possibility of additional direct
> constraints on atmospheric chemistry.
