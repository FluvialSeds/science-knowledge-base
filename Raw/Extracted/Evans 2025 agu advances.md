> Abstract Intensifying effects of global climate change have spurred efforts to enhance carbon sequestration
> and the long‐term storage of soil organic carbon (OC). Current soil carbon models predominantly assume that
> inputs of OC are biospheric, that is, primarily derived from plant decomposition. However, these overlook the
> contribution of OC from soil parent material, including petrogenic organic carbon (OCpetro) from OC‐bearing
> (meta‐)sedimentary bedrock. To our knowledge, no soil carbon model accounts for the inputs of OCpetro to soils,
> resulting in significant gaps in our understanding about the roles OCpetro plays in soils. Here, we call for cross‐
> disciplinary research to investigate the transport and stability of OCpetro across the bedrock–soil continuum. We
> pose four key questions as motivation for this effort. Ignoring the inputs of OCpetro to soils has significant
> implications, including overestimating biospheric carbon stocks and turnover times. Furthermore, we lack
> information on the role that OCpetro may play in priming microbial communities, as well as the impacts of land
> management on OCpetro stocks.
> Plain Language Summary Some soils store carbon not just from plants but also from the rocks
> beneath them. This rock‐derived carbon, called petrogenic organic carbon (OCpetro), comes from certain types of
> bedrock (e.g., shale). Most current models of soil carbon focus only on plant‐derived carbon, ignoring the role of
> OCpetro. This oversight could mean we are underestimating the total carbon stored in soils, especially in deeper
> layers. Furthermore, the geologic age of OCpetro means that it may significantly impact 14C signatures of soil
> OC, and hence apparent carbon turnover times. Additionally, we still don't fully understand how OCpetro might
> affect soil microbes or how land management practices influence this type of carbon. To address these gaps,
> researchers need to explore how OCpetro moves from bedrock into soil and how stable it is once there. This
> requires collaboration between scientists studying soils, rocks, and ecosystems. Answering key questions about
> OCpetro's behavior could improve our understanding of soil carbon and help us manage soils more effectively for
> climate change mitigation. Including OCpetro in soil carbon models is a critical step toward better predictions of
> soil carbon storage and its role in the global carbon cycle.
> 1. The Absence of OCpetro in Soil Carbon Cycle Models
> Soils constitute one of the most substantial terrestrial reservoirs of carbon. They harbor in excess of 1,500 Pg of
> organic carbon (OC; Scharlemann et al., 2014), a pool more than twice the size of that currently held in the
> atmosphere. Efforts to enhance both carbon sequestration and the long‐term storage of soil OC have enriched our
> understanding about the different pools and transfers of OC within soils (Wang et al., 2023). Whilst there is no
> single soil carbon model applicable for all land‐use contexts, most if not all are based on the principle that the
> major input of OC to soils is biospheric in origin and driven by photosynthetic carbon fixation (Janzen, 2004; Noë
> et al., 2023). Predominantly, this includes litter and plant residues, root exudation, the inputs of animal wastes
> (e.g., farmyard manure) and decomposition products of plants or organic matter present as microbial residues and
> necromass. Evidently absent from soil carbon models, and arguably the wider discourse within soil science, is the
> OC which is inherited from the underlying soil parent material during soil formation.
> The roles that soil parent materials play in storing photosynthetically‐derived (i.e., biospheric) OC is emerging
> (Evans, 2025; Moreland et al., 2021; Tye et al., 2022). However, some parent materials also act as sources of OC
> to soils. Here, we specifically refer to geogenic or petrogenic organic carbon (hereafter, OCpetro) from OC‐bearing
> (meta‐)sedimentary bedrock such as shale. Observations of the upward conveyance of rock‐derived minerals and,

> 2. The Presence and Role of OCpetro as a Topic of Broader Relevance to the Geoscience
> Community
> The geologic cycling of OCpetro has seen a flurry of research following studies that reveal considerable amounts of
> OCpetro are reburied in new sedimentary strata (e.g., Galy et al., 2008). With contributions across disparate
> geoscience disciplines including palynology, organic petrology and geochemistry, OCpetro has been detected in
> many modern soil and sedimentary environments (Blattmann et al., 2018). In particular, the popularization of
> radiocarbon measurements and thermal methods (e.g., ramped pyrolysis) have succeeded in providing quanti-
> tative constraints on the abundance of OCpetro, disentangling it from the typically larger “biospheric” OC
> component (e.g., Hilton & West, 2020). Its prevalence in surface environments indicates that a fraction of OCpetro
> released from bedrock embarks on a journey from the geosphere to the pedosphere and eventually back into the
> geosphere along sedimentary source‐to‐sink pathways (Figure 1). This, in turn, implies that a portion of OC burial
> operates in a CO2 neutral manner, and it is therefore important to distinguish this from the burial of fresh
> biospheric OC to accurately assess carbon sequestration (Galvez et al., 2020). Daines et al. (2017) introduce a
> general concept on OCpetro cycling operating through geologic time, with greater reburial during the Archean and
> Proterozoic owing to a lower oxygen content in the atmosphere, and relatively little OCpetro reburial during times
> similar to modern‐day oxygen levels that lead to a high degree of oxidation. With its oxidation, not only is CO2
> released, but O2 is also consumed, resulting in an interplay of OCpetro cycling with, and responding to, atmo-
> spheric chemistry (Canfield, 2021; Nabhan & Canfield, 2023). Episodes of accelerated OCpetro cycling
> contributing to substantial CO2 release to the atmosphere have been suggested during times of deglaciation that
> exposed reactive OCpetro to rapid oxidation (Blattmann, 2022) as well as release during greenhouse conditions
> that prevailed during the Paleocene‐Eocene Thermal Maximum (e.g., Hollingsworth et al., 2024; Lyons et al.,
> 2019). Throughout the vast expanse of geologic time, a fraction of OCpetro also appears to undergo exhumation
> and reburial over multiple cycles (Galy et al., 2008; Sparkes et al., 2020). Recalcitrant, more highly condensed
> (graphitized) forms of OCpetro, are thus stable enough to withstand repetitive cycling through the geosphere–
> pedosphere domains.
> The recycling efficiency of OCpetro, as well as the loci and conditions that promote its oxidation, are the subject of
> current debate (e.g., Fox et al., 2020; Li & Li, 2024; Ruben et al., 2023). What has been missing to date is an
> understanding of the processes by which OCpetro transfers from the lithosphere to the pedosphere (i.e., across the
> bedrock–saprolite–soil continuum). Grant et al. (2023) make the case that OCpetro is not only a component of soil
> OC, but it is sufficiently abundant to warrant a paradigm shift in current conceptual models of soil carbon storage
> and turnover. Adding to the dynamic nature of soil OC, the degradation kinetics of OCpetro are also sensitive to
> temperature changes (Roylands et al., 2024; Soong et al., 2021). Merging geoscience and soil science perspectives
> on OCpetro cycling will advance our understanding of the carbon cycle amidst global climate change.

> 3. Emerging Questions on OCpetro Across the Bedrock–Soil Continuum
> 3.1. To What Extent Is the Input of OCpetro From Bedrock to Soil Limited by the Soil Production
> Function?
> One of the most important yet understudied interfaces relevant to OCpetro is that marked by the transition between
> OC‐bearing bedrock and the overlying soil profile. Across the bedrock–soil continuum, physical and chemical
> weathering processes transform consolidated bedrock into saprolite that supports the development of a pro-
> gressively thickening layer of soil (Banwart et al., 2019; Evans et al., 2019). These processes transfer OCpetro from
> the parent material into the soil profile (Doetterl et al., 2015). The rate at which this occurs remains largely
> uncertain. Soil formation rates from bedrock are governed by many factors (Evans et al., 2019), one of which is a
> self‐limiting mechanism (i.e., the soil production function) whereby soil formation slows as a soil progressively
> thickens. For example, a thickening soil can insulate the bedrock from diurnal temperature variations which are
> ultimately responsible for freeze‐thaw weathering, a process particularly key for the frost‐shattering of shale
> (Soulet et al., 2021). From this, we would hypothesize that the rate at which OCpetro is mobilized from bedrock to
> soil via weathering is similarly governed by the soil production function. Reconciling this relationship is
> important so that we can accurately integrate the inputs of OCpetro in soil carbon models. Moreover, new
> knowledge about the dynamics of the mobilization of OCpetro from bedrock to soils will also advance our un-
> derstanding about the weathering of other key nutrients (e.g., N and P) from sedimentary rocks and their supply
> rates to soils (Zaharescu et al., 2019). This is especially pertinent given recent research showing, for example, that
> bedrock sources of nitrogen rival atmospheric nitrogen inputs (Houlton et al., 2018; Moreland et al., 2022).
> 3.2. How Readily Is OCpetro Assimilated by Microbial Communities in Subsoils?
> Controls on the short‐to long‐term stability of OCpetro mobilized from bedrock to soils remain unclear. We have
> only a sparse understanding about how OCpetro is assimilated by subsoil microbial communities (Bertassoli
> et al., 2016; Hemingway et al., 2018). On the one hand, OCpetro is largely comprised of “kerogen”—insoluble,
> nonhydrolyzable macromolecular material disseminated in sediments that is at least partially composed of
> condensed carbon structures with a high degree of aromaticity (Bouchez et al., 2010; Durand, 1980). This implies
> that it is difficult to break‐down chemically, although the degree of stability likely depends on carbon composition
> and the degree of diagenetic and thermal alteration of the bedrock, as well as the microbial community structure,
> the availability of other essential nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus that may regulate microbial meta-
> bolism, and the redox conditions (Bukombe et al., 2021; Hicks et al., 2021; Kidinda et al., 2022, 2023; Manzoni
> et al., 2021). On the other hand, many sedimentary rocks bearing OCpetro, such as shale, are generally highly
> weatherable and can thus provide a large surface area for OCpetro mineralization. Like the rates of bedrock

> 3.3. To What Extent Do Subsoil Priming Processes Directly or Indirectly Affect the Stability of OCpetro?
> Attention is required to understand how OCpetro is affected by soil priming effects (Kuzyakov et al., 2000).
> Research has found that the supply of fresh organic matter (labile inputs such as litter inputs, root exudates,
> transport of DOC) can trigger the enzymatic activity of dormant microbial communities, leading to the miner-
> alization of resistant OC pools (X. Chen et al., 2022; Y. Chen et al., 2022). Consequently, calls to study the
> vulnerability of resistant OC pools to priming effects are growing (e.g., Wu et al., 2024). So far, however, these
> have not included OCpetro. As a result, questions remain about the size and the type of fresh carbon inputs required
> to prime the mineralization of OCpetro, as well as the microbial communities capable of driving this priming effect,
> and the impact of OCpetro composition on the efficacy of priming. By extension, there may also be positive
> feedback processes. One such example is the production of CO2 from microbial respiration which dissolves in soil
> pore water to create carbonic acid (Sánchez‐Cañete et al., 2018). This carbonic acid, in turn, helps to promote
> bedrock dissolution and therefore the susceptibility of OCpetro to microbial oxidation.
> 3.4. To What Extent Does Land‐Use and Management Impact OCpetro Across the Bedrock–Soil
> Continuum?
> To date, most of the research on OCpetro has taken place in unmanaged or uncultivated environments, such as
> glacial chronosequences, where the impacts of land management practices on the stability of OCpetro are either
> minor or difficult to quantify (see Hilton and West (2020) and references therein). The vulnerability of biospheric
> soil OC to changes in land use and management has been thoroughly studied and reviewed (e.g., Beillouin
> et al., 2021; Smith et al., 2018), yet these efforts have ignored the dynamics of OCpetro. This omission in part
> reflects the difficulty of partitioning the oxidation of recently photosynthesized OC and ancient OCpetro (Tune
> et al., 2023). Nevertheless, some studies have developed methodologies that allow these OC fractions to be
> quantified. For instance, Vindušková et al. (2015) compare and evaluate information from a combination of
> isotopic, spectroscopic and thermal methods to show that OCpetro contributed up to 99% of the total OC in soils
> developed on a mine spoil heap. The authors state that a reliable method for quantifying the reintroduction of
> OCpetro into the modern OC cycle is needed, which is also relevant for evaluating the effectiveness of vegetation
> colonization strategies. For example, Zhang et al. (2023) report soil OC accumulation along a chronosequence of
> vegetation restoration, and show that an increase in microbial biomass with colonization over time can stimulate
> the decomposition of recalcitrant OCpetro. Although the conversion of croplands to grasslands or forest stands
> tends to increase soil OC stocks, deeper‐rooted grass or tree species may enhance the susceptibility of OCpetro to
> oxidation by inducing bedrock weathering. The penetration and development of roots in bedrock has been shown
> to promote mineral dissolution (Wen et al., 2021) although the effects this has on the oxidation of OCpetro remains
> unknown and warrants further study (Tune et al., 2023).
> 4. Incorporating OCpetro Into Studies of the Soil Carbon Cycle
> There are significant and fundamental knowledge gaps in our understanding of OCpetro and its role in the carbon
> cycle. Addressing these “known unknowns” is required so that we can advance efforts to understand the role of
> soils as a critical resource as well as to combat global climate change. Bridging these knowledge gaps represents
> an urgent challenge. In many high‐latitude and high‐altitude environments, bedrock weathering rates are
> accelerating due to climate warming, implying faster release rates of OCpetro into soil profiles (Zondervan
> et al., 2023). Meanwhile, in managed landscapes, accelerated soil erosion is grossly outpacing the production of

> System, is needed to resolve some of the “known unknowns” in OCpetro research. In this context, the continued
> development of Critical Zone Research (Singha et al., 2024) constitutes an integrative solution to bring disparate
> geoscientific disciplines together to address the evidence needs.
