> 14C MEASUREMENTS OF DISSOLVED INORGANIC AND ORGANIC CARBON IN
> QINGHAI LAKE AND INFLOWING RIVERS (NE TIBET, QINGHAI PLATEAU), CHINA
> A J T Jull1,2,3,4,5 • G S Burr1,3,6 • W Zhou7 • P Cheng7 • S H Song7 • A G Leonard1,2 • L Cheng1 • Z S An7
> ABSTRACT. There have been a number of studies that have attempted to estimate the past radiocarbon reservoir effects
> in Qinghai Lake, China. This article reports on measurements on modern samples collected at the lake in October 2003
> and October 2009, which allow us to better understand the systematics of the lake and shed new insights on the processes
> occurring in the lake. The results indicate that atmospheric exchange of 14C is the main process affecting surface dissolved
> inorganic carbon (DIC) in the lake, but dissolved organic carbon (DOC) can be explained as a combination of sources. We
> also conclude that sediment carbon can be explained by a model where input from the surrounding rivers and groundwater
> are important, in agreement with the model of Yu et al. (2007).
> INTRODUCTION
> Radiocarbon studies in lakes are beset by two problems: first, the input of carbonate-rich sediments
> and river waters (Deevey et al. 1954); and second, the potential storage of carbon for long periods of
> time, which may also be recycled into the lake carbon reservoir. In general, the first effect is called
> a hardwater effect and the second, a reservoir effect, although the latter term is often applied to both
> phenomena. For the sake of simplicity, we bundle both effects together here as a general “reservoir
> effect,” although this is referred to by other names in the literature, for example, Henderson and
> Holmes (2009) refer to an “old carbon effect” (OCE).
> Qinghai Lake
> Qinghai Lake is an evaporative lake that lies in the semiarid Chinese interior, where climate condi-
> tions are controlled by both the Asian monsoon system and the westerly jet stream. It extends from
> 36°32′ to 37°15′N and 99°36′ to 100°47′E, close to the current limit of the present (southeast) Asian
> monsoon, and just beyond that of the Indian (southwest) monsoon (Figure 1). Its current surface
> area is ~4400 km2 and it has an average depth of ~21 m, up to 25–26 m at its deepest part (Yu and
> Kelts 2002). In recent years, the size of the lake has been declining; it covered 4548 km2 in 1959
> (Li et al. 2007a,b), although the level of the lake has been increasing recently (Zhang et al. 2011).
> Between 1959 and 2000, mean annual precipitation over the lake was 357 ± 10 mm and mean an-
> nual evaporation was 924 ± 10 mm (Li et al. 2007a,b). Most of the precipitation (60%) falls during
> summer months (Wang et al. 2003) and more than 60% of the evaporation also occurs during the
> summer. Winds blow onshore during the daytime and offshore at night with an average speed of
> 4–6 m/s. The mean annual temperature is –0.7°C and exhibits a large seasonality, ranging from
> –11°C (winter) to 12°C (summer). The lake surface is usually frozen from December to March, with
> a maximum ice thickness estimated to be ~0.8 m (Tao et al. 2009).
> The overall drainage basin is ~29,660 km2, in which about 50 rivers exceeding 5 km in length
> seasonally discharge to the closed lake basin, with annual mean runoff of 16 × 108 m3 (Wang et al.
> 2003). The large perennial Buha River (Figure 2), with a watershed of 14,337 km2, is the largest
> inflow both by runoff (50% of the total) and by the amount of sand discharge (70% of the total).
> Most river runoff occurs in summer months (Hu 1989). Meltwater from surrounding mountain gla-
> ciers only accounts for 0.3% of the total runoff. The volume of the lake was estimated by Kelts et al.
> (1989) to be 8.545 × 1010 m3, although Henderson and Holmes (2009) quote a value of 7.16 × 108 m3.

> The lake has apparently been a closed-basin evaporative lake for at least the last 36,000 yr and
> probably a much longer time (Yan et al. 2002). Its chemistry is typical of a lake dominated by water
> evaporation and precipitation of calcium carbonate. The lake water is brackish to saline (Na-Mg-
> SO4-Cl– type) with an average salinity of 14.1 g/L and a pH of 9.2. The salinity of lake water shows
> moderate temporal and spatial variability; for example, at the mouth of the Buha River, it averages
> 12.3 g/L and it increases towards the center of the lake. The inflowing waters, particularly from the
> Buha River, reflect the chemistry of the carbonate-rich rocks over which it flows (Yan et al. 2002;
> Zhang et al. 2009). As a closed basin lake in a semiarid climate, the lake is an evaporative system,
> so that the main climatic signal is related to the degree of evaporative enrichment, which varies with
> changes in climatically driven hydrology. Information about evaporative enrichment, in turn, is
> captured by the lake’s sediments. The lake had been experiencing a general reduction in lake level
> due to evaporation and local groundwater drainage, but recent remote sensing indicates the lake
> level has stabilized in the period since about 2003 (Zhang et al. 2011). Figure 3 shows a summary
> of recent lake-level measurements.

> GEOCHRONOLOGICAL QUESTIONS
> A key part of any paleoclimatic reconstruction based on lake sediments is to establish a firm geo-
> chronology for the cores. Past studies have anticipated some of the potential concerns with 14C dat-
> ing of the lake sediments. Preliminary sediment trap studies were carried out in 1989 by the Institute
> for Saltwater Lake Studies at a site near the Fishery village on the south shore of the lake, as well as
> in Hiyan Bay and Lake Erhai by Kelts et al. (1989). Their results for the Fishery site reveal that the
> elements Ti, Fe, Cr, Cd, Al, Cu, Mn, and V are highly concentrated due to human activity. Kelts et
> al. (1989) did not detect any significant chemocline due to wave turbulence and high dissolved oxy-
> gen (5.72 mg/L). The average sedimentation rate was estimated to be 0.48 mm/yr, close to 210Pb- and
> 137Cs-based estimates for the past ~150 yr discussed below. Authigenic carbonates in suspended ma-
> terial are dominated by aragonite (34%), whereas in suspended sediments in river water, calcites are
> dominant. Terrigenous clay minerals include illite (58%), kaolinite (28%), and nontronite (14%).
> Kaolinite appears to be unstable in this alkaline environment. Based on mineralogy, it appears that
> eolian sediment dominates over fluvial sediment in the terrigenous fraction.
> Loss-on-ignition (LOI) measurements have been used to determine the total organic carbon (TOC)
> and carbonate content of Qinghai Lake sediments (Zhang et al. 2003). The LOI fraction at 550°C
> captures the organic carbon, and the subsequent LOI fraction at 950°C captures the carbonate frac-
> tion. Zhang et al. (2003) showed a range of 13–22% total organic carbon (TOC) and 13–22% calcar-
> eous silts and clays from the last 800 yr. The dominant carbonate mineral in Qinghai Lake sediments
> is aragonite (An et al. 2012), with lesser amounts of low-Mg and high-Mg calcite, and traces of
> dolomite in places. Magnesium is highly concentrated in the lake water (Mg/Cawater = 100), leading
> to the formation of aragonite and also resulting in some precipitation of hydromagnesite.
> Sedimentation rates in the lake basin for the last 200 yr have been measured using the activities of
> naturally occurring 210Pb and atomic-testing-derived 137Cs (Huang and Sun 1989; Zhang et al. 2003;
> Henderson 2004). Sedimentary 137Cs signals are exceptionally strong in this lake, and the maximum
> in activity is consistent with a 210Pb age of AD 1963. The activity of unsupported and total 210Pb
> decreases exponentially downcore in almost all cases. Sedimentation rates for the main body of the
> lake vary from as low as 0.4 mm/yr in the southern basin of the lake to as much as 1.4 mm/yr in
> the eastern part. Higher sedimentation rates in the eastern subbasin may be due to its location at the
> downstream end of the fetch of the prevailing westerly winds. Sedimentation rates in the south basin
> compare well with those estimated by Lister et al. (1991), who suggest a rate of about 0.4 mm/yr
> for the whole Holocene.
> Over the past 2 decades, there have been a number of studies to try to date Qinghai Lake sediments
> and various attempts have been made to try to understand the 14C chronology of the lake sediment
> record, beginning with the pioneering studies of Kelts et al. (1989) and Lister et al. (1991). Since
> that time, a number of other studies have tried to expand on this initial work. These various studies
> are summarized in Table 2. As can be seen from the table, these different groups came to varying
> conclusions about the size and importance of 14C reservoir effects in Qinghai Lake. However, they
> all consistently point to the existence of persistent offsets in the 14C content compared to the expect-
> ed age, of the order of a few hundred to thousands of years.

> RESERVOIR EFFECTS AT QINGHAI LAKE
> Kelts et al. (1989) had initially assumed that there was no reservoir effect and the 14C ages (of which
> there were only five) could be used directly. Zhang et al. (1989, 1994) conducted a similar study
> with a different core, with very similar results to Kelts et al. (1989) and Lister et al. (1991). Both sets
> of results are shown in Figure 4. Yu et al. (2007) give a detailed discussion of the early 14C results,
> as well as stable isotopic information and other characteristics of the cores collected in the 1980s
> by the Swiss-Chinese team. Henderson (2004) and Shen et al. (2005) had determined that there was
> a definite 14C offset in sediment cores collected from Qinghai Lake. As reported by Henderson et
> al. (2010), the Arizona group had previously also determined that the DOC in the lake waters in
> 2003 had a 14C age of ~661 14C yr BP. Zhang et al. (1994) and Shen et al. (2005) had noted offsets
> in the estimated age of surface sediments when sediment ages were extrapolated to the surface, and
> Henderson et al. (2010) also noted this result. Yu et al. (2007) developed a simple box model in
> an attempt to explain the observations of 14C in both dissolved inorganic (DIC) and organic carbon
> (DOC), as well as those in the lake sediments themselves. They concluded that there should be a
> reservoir effect of up to ~1500 yr in sediments. Later, Henderson et al. (2010) investigated some
> modern surface sediments and observed 14C ages of ~650 yr BP. Recently, Mischke et al. (2013)
> reinterpreted the result reported by Henderson et al. (2010) to suggest the lake reservoir effect in
> DOC is larger than 661 14C yr due to the effects of bomb carbon. In their interpretation, the effect
> is a simple mixing of old and post-bomb material from the year or sampling in the formation of the
> DOC 14C age. This assumes immediate mixing of the post-bomb DIC, which seems unlikely.

> METHODS
> Water samples were collected in the field during a survey collection in October 2003 and an ex-
> tensive collection in October 2009. All of the samples were taken from surface water from the
> shore. The 2009 samples were filtered with 0.7-mm glass filters in the field using a portable vacuum
> filtration device (see Figure 5). One-liter glass bottles with tight-fitting lids were filled to the brim
> with the filtered water and sealed to prevent exchange with atmospheric CO2. The water samples
> were processed for both DIC and DOC at the Xi’an AMS Laboratory. The samples were placed in
> a large container and acidified with H3PO4 on a vacuum line to liberate CO2 from the DIC fraction.
> Subsequently, they were evaporated and the remaining salts were combusted to extract DOC, as de-
> scribed by Leonard et al. (2013). In the case of the sediment samples, they were treated with 1N HCl
> and then washed and dried in distilled water. They were combusted at 900°C with CuO and then
> converted to graphite at the Arizona laboratory. AMS measurements were all made at Arizona using
> standard procedures (Jull et al. 2006).

> lyzed the results of Kelts et al. (1989) and Zhang et al. (1994) and recognized that they both plotted
> on the same graph with a similar slope and intercept (Figure 3). Hence, we can establish that these
> two cores, taken at different location in 1985, are very similar. Our calculation is that the 14C offset
> is 635 yr BP, using the approach of Shen et al. (2005). In our new study, we took a number of short
> cores collected in 2006 as part of the ICDP drilling program (see Figure 1 for locations). These
> results are shown in Figure 6. The findings show similar trends to those observed in earlier studies
> (e.g. Henderson et al. 2010; Zhou et al. 2014). The total organic fraction of the cores shows a distinct
> reservoir offset, as determined by the core intercept method (Shen et al. 2005) and in addition, the
> carbonates in the core show even older 14C values (in agreement with the observations of Henderson
> 2004), and in contradiction to the recent surface water DIC values. This suggests strongly that the
> carbonate being deposited in the lake is not in equilibrium with the surficial dissolved bicarbonate.
> The incoming DIC from the rivers, and likely groundwater as discussed by Yu et al. (2007), is need-
> ed to resolve this apparent discrepancy. We know from lake chemical studies (e.g. Henderson et al.
> 2010; Xu et al. 2010) that the lake is very saline, and the levels of Ca2+ are relatively low, reflecting
> the continual precipitation of authigenic carbonates. However, as pointed out by Yu et al. (2007),
> groundwater carbonate-rich waters or detrital carbonates entering the lake would have a substantial
> age, explaining the offset (of 1700–2400 yr) in the inorganic phase in these two fractions. The fact

> 960 yr) may be due to aging of the organic material in the water column and possibly diagenesis of

> CONCLUSIONS
> This study presented a consistent set of results on DIC, DOC, and POC in Qinghai Lake water and
> incoming river water. We believe this is the most complete data set of 14C information available to
> date. Previous 14C studies have been rather fragmentary. The results show clearly that the incoming
> DIC and DOC from rivers has some 14C age, and DOC in the lake is easily understood in terms of
> the model of Yu et al. (2007). By contrast, the DIC in the lake is modern, as we expect from an
> alkaline lake, but the precipitation of authigenic carbonates produces carbonate with lower 14C con-
> tent, indicating another source of “old” carbon. We propose that these sources are river carbonates
> carried as particulates, DIC, and groundwater sources. Indeed, Yu et al. (2007) proposed DIC-rich
> groundwaters to explain this effect. There are to date no measurements of DIC, DOC, and POC as
> a function of depth in the lake to our knowledge. Such information would be of great importance to
> understand the controlling processes beyond the existing models, and would contribute greatly to a
> better understanding of lake processes.
