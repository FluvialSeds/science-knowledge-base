# Improved chromatography reveals multiple new bacteriohopanepolyol isomers in marine sediments

## Overall Scientific Topic

Bacteriohopanepolyols (BHPs) are pentacyclic triterpenoid lipids synthesized by bacteria with extensive structural diversity reflecting different bacterial producers and environmental conditions. BHPs and their diagenetic products persist in sediments and rocks over geological timescales, making them valuable biomarkers of ancient bacterial activity and organic matter sources. However, methodological limitations have hindered investigation of structural isomerism among composite BHPs in environmental samples. This study develops an improved high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) method using three serial Phenomenex Kinetex C₁₈ columns that successfully resolves new structural isomers of composite BHPs in marine sediments, revealing previously undetected structural diversity that has implications for interpreting BHP-based environmental proxies and compound-specific isotope work.

## Methods

Marine sediment samples were analyzed using an improved HPLC method employing three serial Phenomenex Kinetex C₁₈ columns (1.7 μm, 2.1 × 150 mm each). This multi-column configuration provided enhanced resolution and baseline separation compared to single-column approaches previously used for BHP analysis. The method was optimized for gradient elution and temperature control to maximize separation of composite BHP structures while maintaining sensitivity for detection via evaporative light-scattering detection (ELSD) and mass spectrometry (MS).

BHP isomers were identified based on their retention times, mass spectral fragmentation patterns, and comparison to reference standards where available. The relative abundances of different isomers were quantified from chromatographic peak areas. Analysis focused on commonly observed composite BHPs in marine sediments, including various configurations of hydroxylated hopanoids and aminohopanoids.

## Results

The improved three-column HPLC method successfully resolved numerous new structural isomers of BHPs in marine sediment samples, revealing substantially greater structural diversity than previously appreciated. Investigated samples consistently showed high BHP isomer diversity, with distinct suites of isomers and varying relative abundances between different sediment samples.

Key findings include:
- Enhanced baseline separation of previously co-eluting BHP compounds
- Discovery of new isomeric configurations of composite BHPs
- Significant sample-to-sample variation in isomer relative abundances across the analyzed marine sediments
- Some isomers were present consistently in all samples while others showed variable abundance

The differences in relative abundances of specific BHP isomers between samples suggest these variations reflect differences in depositional setting, redox conditions, or microbial community composition active during sediment deposition.

## Implications

The improved chromatographic resolution enables accurate quantification of individual BHP isomers rather than treating composite BHPs as single compound classes. This methodological advance is essential for future development of BHP-based environmental proxies that exploit isomer-specific information to fingerprint different bacterial taxa or environmental conditions.

The discovery that BHP isomer composition varies systematically between samples opens new avenues for using these biomarkers to reconstruct past environmental conditions. For example, different redox states, oxygen concentrations, or nitrogen availability might favor production of specific BHP isomers by different bacterial taxa.

The improved baseline separation also enables future compound-specific radiocarbon and stable isotope analysis of individual BHP isomers, allowing detailed reconstruction of the sources and residence times of bacterial-derived organic matter in marine systems. This is particularly valuable because bacteria are major contributors to the organic matter pool exported from soils and coastal systems to the ocean.

The method provides a foundation for more detailed isotope work linking BHP isomer identity to their isotopic signatures, which will improve interpretation of bacterial biomarkers in ancient sediments and rocks.
