# Mineral protection regulates long-term global preservation of natural organic carbon


## Overall Scientific Topic

The balance between photosynthetic organic carbon production and respiration controls atmospheric composition and climate. While the majority of organic carbon is respired back to CO₂, a small fraction escapes remineralization and is preserved over geological timescales. Two major mechanisms have been proposed to explain organic carbon preservation: selective preservation of biochemically unreactive compounds, and protection resulting from interactions with a mineral matrix. This global study uses activation energy distributions and radiocarbon dating to assess the relative importance of these mechanisms across soils, sediments, and dissolved organic carbon, providing evidence that mineral protection is the dominant control on long-term OC preservation.

## Methods

The researchers assembled a global dataset of organic carbon activation energy distributions and corresponding radiocarbon ages from soils, sediments, and dissolved organic carbon samples. Activation energy was measured using ramped pyrolysis/oxidation (RPO), which progressively heats samples from 150–1000°C while measuring the radiocarbon activity (¹⁴C/¹²C) and isotopic composition of released carbon at each temperature step. This approach reveals how carbon age varies with thermal stability, allowing assessment of whether preservation mechanisms are selective (narrowing E distributions) or protective (broadening E distributions).

## Results

Across all mineral-containing samples, activation energy distributions broaden over time, indicating increasing bond-strength diversity. This broadening contradicts selective preservation, which would predict narrowing distributions as labile compounds are preferentially removed. Instead, the results demonstrate formation of organo-mineral bonds, where diverse organic compounds—not just recalcitrant ones—become increasingly protected by mineral interactions. Radiocarbon ages reveal that high-energy, mineral-bound organic carbon persists for millennia (often >10,000 years) relative to low-energy, unbound organic carbon (modern to young ages). This age stratification is consistent with mineral protection extending OC lifetime by orders of magnitude.

## Implications

These results provide globally coherent evidence for the proposed importance of mineral protection in promoting organic carbon preservation. Mineral interactions, not biochemical selectivity, appear to be the dominant mechanism controlling how long organic carbon persists in the biosphere before respiration. By controlling how much carbon escapes remineralization, mineral protection fundamentally regulates atmospheric CO₂ over geological timescales, suggesting that tectonic activity, mountain erosion, and mineral weathering may have regulated Earth's climate over million-year cycles. Future studies of bond-strength diversity in ancient sediments may reveal how organic carbon preservation—and thus atmospheric composition and climate—has varied over geological time.
