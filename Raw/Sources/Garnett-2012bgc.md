# Radiocarbon dating of methane and carbon dioxide evaded from a temperate peatland stream

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study presents new methods for radiocarbon analysis of methane and carbon dioxide evaded from peatland streams. Research on a temperate raised peat bog demonstrates that evaded methane was aged 1,617-1,987 years B.P., much older than evaded CO2 (303-521 years B.P.), indicating different source areas within the peat profile and distinct carbon cycling pathways.

## Methods

A large volume floating chamber (42,580 ml total volume, 1,520 cm2 cross-sectional area) was constructed from an inverted plastic barrel and deployed on a small peatland stream at Langlands Moss in southwest Scotland. The chamber captured gas emitted from the stream surface without disturbing the streambed. Auto-shutoff couplings in the chamber top allowed sampling of headspace gas while maintaining an air-tight seal. Four sets of paired CH4 and CO2 samples were analyzed from two separate locations with spatial and temporal variation assessment. Samples were analyzed for dual isotopes (¹⁴C and δ¹³C) using established analytical protocols.

## Results

Methane was aged between 1,617 and 1,987 years B.P., while CO2 had an age range of 303-521 years B.P. Isotope mass balance modeling indicated that evaded CO2 and CH4 were derived from different source areas, with most evaded CO2 originating from younger peat layers near the surface while CH4 came from deeper, older peat layers. The study demonstrated the feasibility of collecting sufficient CH4 samples for dual isotope analysis through evasion from small first-order peatland streams.

## Implications

The significant age difference between evaded CH4 and CO2 reveals important insights into peatland carbon cycling and greenhouse gas production pathways. This methodological development enables investigation of soil-water-atmosphere carbon transfers through complementary analysis of both CH4 and CO2, advancing understanding of how ancient peat carbon is mobilized and released as greenhouse gases from peatland ecosystems.
