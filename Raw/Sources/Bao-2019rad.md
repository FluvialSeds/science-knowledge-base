# Influence of different acid treatments on the radiocarbon content spectrum of sedimentary organic matter determined by RPO/accelerator mass spectrometry

## Overall Scientific Topic

Radiocarbon measurements of organic matter in sediments require prior removal of inorganic carbonate through acid treatment. Two standard methods exist: acid rinsing (direct HCl titration followed by water rinsing) and acid fumigation (vapor-phase HCl exposure in sealed desiccators). However, these methods can differentially affect measured radiocarbon ages depending on sample composition. This study uses ramped-pyrolysis-oxidation (RPO) coupled to radiocarbon analysis to systematically compare how acid treatments alter the radiocarbon age spectrum of sedimentary organic components, with implications for accurate paleoclimate reconstruction and understanding organic matter source and reactivity.

## Methods

Six aquatic sediment samples representing marine and fluvial settings were selected: low-carbonate marine sediments from Nantucket Mud Patch and Buzzards Bay; high-carbonate deep-sea sediments from Bermuda Rise, Sierra Leone Rise, and Ceara Rise; and suspended particulate matter from Taunton River. Samples were subjected to three pretreatment conditions: no acidification, HCl rinsing (RinseHCl—1.0 N HCl at 60°C for 1 hour with repeated centrifugation and rinsing), and HCl fumigation (FumeHCl—vapor-phase exposure in desiccator). RPO analysis was performed by heating samples at a linear temperature ramp and collecting CO₂ evolved over user-defined temperature intervals for separate radiocarbon measurement. This approach enabled measurement of the radiocarbon age distribution (Fm values) across the thermal decomposition spectrum, revealing age differences among organic components with varying thermal stability.

## Results

Acid treatments affected radiocarbon ages differentially based on sample carbonate content:

**Low-carbonate samples (Nantucket Mud, Buzzards Bay, Taunton River):**
- RinseHCl preferentially removed young, labile organic carbon, leaving residual organic matter enriched in older components (Fm values decreased)
- FumeHCl removed thermally labile, usually young organic carbon, also resulting in decreased Fm values
- Both methods systematically biased bulk radiocarbon ages toward older apparent ages

**High-carbonate samples (Bermuda Rise, Sierra Leone Rise, Ceara Rise):**
- RinseHCl removed relatively old organic components along with carbonate, causing Fm values to increase (remaining organic matter became younger-biased)
- FumeHCl also preferentially removed labile carbon, resulting in lower Fm values
- High carbonate abundance created competing effects: the physical removal of carbonate could liberate old carbon, while acid exposure removed young labile components

Bulk stable carbon isotope compositions (δ¹³C) also shifted with acid treatment, indicating selective loss or alteration of specific organic fractions. The magnitude of these changes correlated with carbonate abundance and the proportion of labile versus refractory organic carbon in each sample.

## Implications

Three critical factors must be considered when acid-treating sediments for radiocarbon analysis: (1) inorganic carbonate abundance—high-carbonate samples show opposite effects to low-carbonate samples; (2) proportions of labile and refractory organic carbon—samples with different organic matter composition respond differently to the same acid treatment; and (3) environmental matrix—the depositional setting (marine vs. fluvial) influences which organic components are most vulnerable to acid-catalyzed loss.

These findings have important consequences for paleoclimate studies using radiocarbon-dated biomarkers. In high-carbonate systems, acid rinsing may remove old mineral-associated organic matter, artificially rejuvenating apparent radiocarbon ages. Conversely, in low-carbonate systems, acid rinsing removes young labile material, artificially aging bulk measurements. RPO methods, which measure radiocarbon across the thermal decomposition spectrum before acid treatment, may provide more defensible age estimates than bulk acid-treated samples, particularly in systems with mixed organic matter sources and variable preservation mechanisms.
