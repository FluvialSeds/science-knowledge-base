# Towards online ramped oxidation (ORO)-AMS for thermal dissection and serial radiocarbon analysis of complex organic matter

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study develops and tests an automated online ramped oxidation (ORO) system directly coupled to accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) for measuring radiocarbon ages of organic carbon components separated by thermal stability. Traditional bulk radiocarbon analysis masks age variability within complex organic matter mixtures by yielding only average ages. Ramped oxidation addresses this by systematically decomposing organic matter into CO₂ at progressively higher temperatures, collecting fractions over defined thermal windows, and analyzing each fraction's ¹⁴C content. The innovation presented here eliminates the offline, labor-intensive manual steps that have limited previous ramped oxidation methods, enabling faster sample throughput and improved reproducibility while maintaining analytical precision comparable to routine AMS measurements.

## Methods

The ORO-DTI-AMS system comprises three integrated modules. The online ramped oxidation (ORO) unit consists of two sequential ceramic fiber furnaces: a sample furnace ramped from room temperature to ~900°C at constant rate (4-6°C·min⁻¹), and a second furnace held at 900°C containing copper oxide and silver catalysts to ensure complete oxidation to CO₂. Samples (soil or sediment) are first acid-fumigated to remove carbonates, then loaded into precombusted quartz tubes with CuO and Ag catalysts. A carrier gas mixture (18% O₂, 82% He) transports evolved products through the system at ~90 mL·min⁻¹. The dual-trap interface (DTI) collects CO₂ from prescribed thermal fractions (typically 9 fractions over 3.5 hr analysis time) using alternating molecular sieve (zeolite) traps held at ~10°C during collection and heated to 400°C for release. CO₂ concentration is monitored continuously at 1 Hz using non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) sensing to generate thermograms. The DTI directly injects fractionated CO₂ into an AMS ion source equipped with a gas inlet, eliminating graphitization steps.

## Results

Initial results demonstrate that the ORO-DTI-AMS system achieves long-term precision and accuracy equivalent to routine gas-phase ¹⁴C measurements at ETH Zurich. Measured ¹⁴C content uncertainties and blank background values for well-characterized standards are comparable to established gas measurement protocols. The system successfully generates mass-weighted average ¹⁴C values from multiple thermal fractions that align with independent bulk ¹⁴C measurements of the same samples. Thermograms clearly separate organic components based on thermal stability, allowing recovery of ¹⁴C-age depth profiles that reveal age heterogeneity previously masked by bulk analysis. Carbon content of individual thermal fractions is quantified using calibration curves established from combustion of standards with known carbon masses (acetanilide and phthalic anhydride).

## Implications

The ORO-DTI-AMS system significantly enhances the utility of ramped oxidation for paleoclimate and biogeochemical research by streamlining sample processing and improving throughput. Direct online coupling eliminates manual CO₂ trapping, graphitization, and target preparation steps that previously required days per sample. Rapid, automated analysis enables large-scale surveys of soil carbon turnover dynamics, sediment provenance, organo-mineral interactions, petrogenic carbon transport, and age constraints for chronostratigraphic models. The ability to rapidly generate high-resolution ¹⁴C age depth profiles for organic matter components will improve understanding of carbon cycling across soils, sediments, and fluvial systems at multiple temporal scales.
