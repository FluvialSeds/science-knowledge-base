# Chemical and isotopic signature of old groundwater and magmatic solutes in a Costa Rican rain forest: evidence from carbon, helium, and chlorine

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study applies multiple isotopic tracers (carbon, helium, and chlorine) to elucidate groundwater mixing and carbon sources in a lowland tropical rain forest. The research demonstrates that interbasin groundwater flow brings old bedrock groundwater (2,400-4,000 ¹⁴C years B.P.) carrying magmatic solutes into the lowland discharge area, where it mixes with younger local groundwater, with significant implications for tropical ecosystem carbon cycling.

## Methods

Carbon, helium, and chlorine concentrations and isotopes were measured in groundwater and surface water collected from the La Selva Biological Station region in Costa Rica. Radiocarbon dating was applied to distinguish bedrock groundwater (older, high-solute) from local groundwater (younger, low-solute). Measurements of ¹⁴C activity, δ¹³C ratios, 3He/4He ratios, and 36Cl/Cl isotopic ratios were used to identify different groundwater sources. Sampling sites were selected to represent headwater streams and major springs. Analysis of water chemistry and isotopic tracers allowed determination of groundwater mixing proportions and source signatures.

## Results

Bedrock groundwater showed high δ¹³C (4.89‰), low ¹⁴C (7.98 pM), elevated 3He/4He ratios (6.88), and low 36Cl/Cl ratios (17×10-15), indicating magmatic outgassing and/or weathering of volcanic rock beneath nearby Volcan Barva. Local groundwater showed absence of magmatic signatures with atmospheric sources for helium and chlorine and biogenic soil gas CO2 for dissolved inorganic carbon. Radiocarbon dating indicated bedrock groundwater ages of 2,400-4,000 years B.P. (lower end of range most likely), while local groundwater showed ¹⁴C activity >100 pM indicating presence of "bomb carbon" and ages <55 years. Data consistently demonstrated mixing of two distinct groundwater types.

## Implications

These findings reveal that significant transport of magmatic CO2 into the lowland tropical rain forest through interbasin groundwater flow may be substantial relative to other large ecosystem-level carbon fluxes. The multi-tracer approach successfully distinguished groundwater sources and validated a conceptual hydrologic model, demonstrating the power of integrating different isotopic systems for understanding groundwater dynamics and carbon cycling in complex tropical watersheds.
