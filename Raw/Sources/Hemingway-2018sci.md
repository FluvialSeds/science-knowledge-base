# Microbial oxidation of lithospheric organic carbon in rapidly eroding tropical mountain soils


## Overall Scientific Topic

This study examines how ancient organic carbon ("petrogenic" carbon locked in bedrock) is oxidized and released as CO₂ during mountain erosion and soil formation. While erosion and weathering of mountains consume atmospheric CO₂ through silicate weathering and burial of biospheric organic carbon, the oxidation of petrogenic organic carbon releases CO₂ back to the atmosphere. Understanding this process is crucial for constraining the carbon cycle at geological timescales and understanding how tectonics and erosion influence global climate over million-year timescales.

## Methods

The researchers studied hillslope soil profiles in the Central Range of Taiwan—one of the fastest-eroding mountain belts on Earth (3–6 mm/year erosion rates). They measured the molecular and isotopic composition of organic carbon and lipid biomarkers at different depths in multiple soil profiles. They used radiocarbon dating, elemental analysis, and isotope ratio mass spectrometry to track the evolution of organic carbon as it moved through the soil profile. Monte Carlo uncertainty analysis was applied to determine oxidation rates and fluxes.

## Results

Approximately 67 ± 11% of the petrogenic organic carbon present in exhumed bedrock is oxidized during soil formation on hillslopes. This oxidation produces CO₂ emissions of 6.1 to 18.6 metric tons of carbon per square kilometer per year. The molecular and isotopic evolution of organic carbon indicates that this remineralization process is microbially mediated. Importantly, these CO₂ emission fluxes increase with erosion rate, creating a coupling between tectonics (which drives erosion) and atmospheric CO₂ emissions.

## Implications

The oxidation of petrogenic organic carbon in mountain soils represents a significant source of atmospheric CO₂ that can partially or fully counteract the CO₂ drawdown achieved by silicate weathering and burial of biospheric carbon. This finding changes our understanding of the carbon cycle in rapidly eroding mountain systems and suggests that tectonically active regions may act as net sources of CO₂ to the atmosphere rather than net sinks. These results have important implications for long-term climate regulation and understanding how mountain building influences global biogeochemical cycles over geological timescales.
