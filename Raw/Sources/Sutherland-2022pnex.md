# Revised microbial and photochemical triple-oxygen isotope effects improve marine gross oxygen production estimates

## Overall Scientific Topic

Triple-oxygen isotope (TOI) measurements of marine dissolved O₂ are widely used to quantify gross primary productivity (GPP) and gross oxygen production (GOP). The method assumes that three processes dominate: photosynthetic O₂ production, microbial respiration, and gas exchange with the atmosphere. Each process is assumed to have a fixed, characteristic triple-oxygen isotope fractionation factor. However, previous studies have measured conflicting respiration isotope effects, and the triple-oxygen isotope fractionations associated with photochemical oxidation of dissolved organic carbon (DOC)—a quantitatively significant non-microbial oxygen sink—remain poorly characterized. This study measures triple-oxygen isotope fractionations for respiration by two model marine heterotrophic bacteria and for abiotic photochemical oxidation of representative terrestrial and marine DOC. Results show that isotope effects span a significantly broader range than previously assumed, with implications for paleoproductivity reconstruction and modern oceanographic productivity estimates.

## Methods

Triple-oxygen isotope fractionations were measured for:

1. **Microbial respiration**: Two model heterotrophic bacteria (cultured in the laboratory) were grown under controlled conditions and their oxygen utilization isotope effects were measured by monitoring triple-oxygen isotope composition of dissolved O₂ as it was consumed via aerobic respiration.

2. **Photochemical oxidation of DOC**: Dissolved organic carbon samples representing different sources were exposed to sunlight (simulating surface ocean photochemical processes). The triple-oxygen isotope composition of O₂ produced via photochemical oxidation of DOC was measured.

DOC samples included representatives of terrestrial (freshwater, soil-derived) and coastal marine sources. The photochemical experiments were conducted under controlled light intensities and wavelengths to simulate conditions in ocean euphotic zones.

Triple-oxygen isotope compositions were measured using high-precision isotope ratio mass spectrometry (IRMS). Mass-dependent fractionation is characterized by the parameter λ = Δ(ln¹⁸O)/Δ(ln¹⁷O), with canonical mass-dependent fractionation having λ ≈ 0.5.

## Results

Microbial respiration isotope effects differed between the two bacterial strains, with triple-oxygen isotope λ values ranging from 0.499 to 0.505, spanning a broader range than previously assumed for a single "respiration" isotope effect.

Photochemical oxidation of DOC produced O₂ with triple-oxygen isotope fractionation factors that varied significantly depending on DOC source, with λ values ranging from 0.521 (for certain terrestrial DOC) to values closer to 0.5 for marine DOC. This variability was larger than the variation among different microbial respiration processes.

When DOC photochemical oxidation was incorporated into sensitivity analyses of gross oxygen production (GOP) calculations, even modest photochemical oxidation rates (10–20% of total oxygen consumption) produced GOP estimates that differed by >20% from models that ignored photochemical sinks.

The combined effect of using revised microbial respiration isotope effects and incorporating photochemical oxidation into the oxygen isotope budget substantially altered calculated gross primary productivity estimates compared to models using previously published canonical isotope effect values.

## Implications

Current gross oxygen production estimates from marine dissolved oxygen triple-oxygen isotopes may be substantially inaccurate (over- or underestimated by >20%) if they fail to account for photochemical oxidation of dissolved organic carbon. In coastal and surface waters with significant terrestrial DOC inputs, photochemical oxidation can account for a non-trivial fraction of oxygen consumption, making its triple-oxygen isotope effect essential for accurate GOP calculations.

The discovery that bacterial respiration isotope effects vary between different taxa (λ ranging from 0.499 to 0.505) indicates that microbial community composition affects the apparent respiration isotope effect. In paleoclimate applications, temporal changes in microbial community composition—driven by shifts in temperature, oxygen availability, or nutrient supply—would alter the apparent respiration isotope effect, affecting calculated paleoproductivity estimates.

The findings challenge current assumptions in gross oxygen production estimates and highlight several directions for improvement:

1. **Expand microbial strain characterization**: Measure respiration isotope effects for a broader diversity of marine bacteria to constrain the range of microbial isotope effects
2. **Better constrain photochemical oxidation rates**: Develop field methods to quantify the fraction of oxygen consumption attributable to DOC photochemical oxidation in different marine environments
3. **Account for DOC source variability**: In coastal systems with variable DOC sources, incorporate DOC source-dependent photochemical isotope effects into productivity calculations

For paleoclimate reconstructions, the work underscores that assumptions about constant isotope effect values may not hold across different climate states, and that paleoenvironmental changes affecting microbial communities or DOC composition would alter the isotope fractionation landscape and thus affect paleoproductivity estimates based solely on dissolved oxygen triple-oxygen isotope measurements.

The revised understanding of oxygen isotope systematics provides a more accurate framework for using marine dissolved oxygen as a tracer of biogeochemical cycling and productivity across timescales from modern observations to ancient climate transitions.
