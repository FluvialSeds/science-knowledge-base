# Millennial-aged organic carbon subsidies to a modern river food web

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study uses radiocarbon (¹⁴C) analysis to trace aged organic matter entering river food webs. The research demonstrates that crustacean zooplankton in the Hudson River are significantly subsidized by highly aged particulate organic carbon sourced from materials thousands of years old. These findings suggest that modern river food webs may derive a substantial portion of their energy from ancient carbon stores rather than contemporary primary production.

## Methods

Water samples were collected monthly to bimonthly from 2004-2005 in the freshwater tidal section of the Hudson River and tributaries. Natural abundance ¹⁴C, ¹³C, and deuterium analyses were conducted on suspended particulate organic carbon (POC) and dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and samples were further fractionated by size categories. Phytoplankton and aquatic plant samples were analyzed for ¹⁴C and ¹³C to establish baseline signatures of contemporary carbon production. IsoSource isotope mixing models were applied to determine the proportional contribution of aged versus modern carbon sources to zooplankton diets.

## Results

Zooplankton showed highly ¹⁴C-depleted signatures (mean Δ¹⁴C = -240‰) compared to modern primary production (Δ¹⁴C = -60% to +50%), indicating incorporation of aged material aged 3,400 to 50,000+ years. Isotope modeling suggests aged carbon comprises 21-57% of the zooplankton diet depending on assumed source age. The magnitude of this aged subsidy was substantial enough to buffer river food webs from limitations imposed by contemporary primary production rates.

## Implications

The recognition that ancient carbon significantly subsidizes modern aquatic food webs has major implications for carbon cycling, food web stability, and ecosystem energy flow. This demonstrates that terrestrial carbon stored in soils and floodplains for millennia can be mobilized and incorporated into contemporary aquatic ecosystems, challenging traditional assumptions about the rapid cycling of organic matter.
