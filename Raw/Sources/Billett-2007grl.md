# UK peatland streams release old carbon dioxide to the atmosphere and young dissolved organic carbon to rivers

## Overall Scientific Topic

Since the end of the last ice age, peatlands in the northern hemisphere have accumulated a large carbon pool comparable to the global atmosphere. Understanding whether old or young carbon is being released from peatlands is critical for predicting climate feedback responses. This study presents isotopic evidence from four UK peatlands showing that whilst DOC released in drainage systems is young, CO₂ lost by evasion is much older, suggesting different carbon sources for these two forms.

## Methods

Isotopic analysis of carbon lost as CO₂ (vertical evasion) and DOC (lateral downstream transport) was carried out on streams draining four peatland catchments in the UK over the period August 2004–August 2006. Three to six samples were collected at different times and at two locations within each catchment to represent a range of seasonal flow conditions. The isotopic signature of evaded CO₂ was measured directly using a floating chamber with zeolite molecular sieve collection system. DOC was isolated and analyzed for its isotopic composition.

## Results

The age of DOC released in the drainage system of peatlands is consistently young (modern to 202 years BP). In contrast, the age of CO₂ lost by evasion from the water surface is much older, varying from modern to 1449 years BP. δ¹³C data suggest that the sources of DOC and CO₂ are different. Drainage waters are characterized by saturated CO₂ with values ranging from ePC O₂ 2.3–71.3, indicating significant evasion potential.

## Implications

The source of older CO₂ is likely to be both geogenic (carbonate weathering) and biogenic (decomposition of soil organic matter). These findings suggest that destabilization of the peatland carbon pool through climate change may preferentially release old carbon to the atmosphere as CO₂, potentially amplifying climate feedback mechanisms. Antecedent moisture conditions affect both within- and between-site differences in the isotopic signature of DOC and CO₂.
