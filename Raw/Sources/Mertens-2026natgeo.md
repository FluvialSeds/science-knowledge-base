# Sedimentary conditions drive modern pyrite burial flux to exceed oxidation

## Overall Scientific Topic

Pyrite (iron sulfide) formation and burial in sediments fundamentally regulates atmospheric CO₂ and O₂ levels through competition between two processes: oxidative weathering of pyrite on land (which consumes atmospheric O₂ and releases CO₂) and anaerobic bacterial sulfate reduction in sediments (which buries pyrite and increases atmospheric O₂). However, the environmental and sedimentological conditions controlling pyrite burial remain poorly constrained. This study develops a non-dimensional diagenetic model that predicts pyrite formation rates and sulfur isotopic composition (δ³⁴S) using only globally-interpolated boundary conditions of organic carbon content, sulfate availability, and sedimentation rates. The model successfully predicts signals in 216 sediment cores across the modern ocean and reveals that the global pyrite burial flux (7.0 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹) substantially exceeds terrestrial pyrite oxidation, indicating the sulfur cycle is currently not in steady state and that net pyrite burial is driving atmospheric oxygen accumulation.

## Methods

A non-dimensional diagenetic model was developed to extract natural variables governing pyrite formation rate and sulfur isotopic composition. The model uses local ratios of organic carbon content to sulfate concentration and organic carbon reactivity to sedimentation rate as primary controls on pyrite formation. Additional sensitivity to reactive iron delivery is incorporated. Model inputs include globally-interpolated boundary conditions of organic carbon flux, sulfate concentration, sedimentation rate, and reactive iron availability extracted from observational datasets. The model was validated against 216 sediment cores distributed across the modern ocean, predicting both pyrite formation rates and δ³⁴S values. Predictions of both formation rate and isotopic composition were compared to measured values in the sediment cores to assess model accuracy.

## Results

The diagenetic model accurately predicts pyrite formation signals in sediment cores across diverse oceanic environments using only globally-interpolated boundary conditions. The global pyrite burial flux is estimated at 7.0 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹ (sensitivity test range: 2.5 × 10¹² to 19.0 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹) with a weighted-average δ³⁴S value of −4‰ (range: −8 to +3‰). This flux substantially exceeds terrestrial pyrite oxidation estimates, indicating the sulfur cycle is currently not in steady state. The model demonstrates that flooded shelf area is the primary control on pyrite burial in the modern ocean and identifies it as the dominant variable regulating sulfur cycling throughout the Phanerozoic Eon (the last 541 million years).

## Implications

The finding that modern pyrite burial exceeds oxidation indicates Earth's atmosphere is currently in a state of net oxygen accumulation driven by sedimentary sulfur burial processes. This result has profound implications for understanding atmospheric oxygen evolution and long-term carbon-oxygen cycling in Earth's climate system. The identification of flooded shelf area as the primary control on pyrite burial throughout the Phanerozoic provides a mechanistic framework for interpreting the geologic record of atmospheric composition changes in response to sea-level variation and continental configuration. These results demonstrate that sedimentological conditions controlling organic carbon and reactive iron delivery to marine sediments are critical determinants of planetary atmospheric composition on geological timescales.
