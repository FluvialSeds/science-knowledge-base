# Millennial-aged peat carbon outgassed by large humic lakes in the Congo Basin

## Overall Scientific Topic

Large humic lakes in tropical peatland complexes represent sites where ancient, peat-derived organic carbon is mobilized from soils, transported through water systems, and outgassed to the atmosphere as CO₂ and CH₄. This brief communication demonstrates that dissolved organic carbon and greenhouse gases in Congo Basin humic lakes contain radiocarbon signatures indicating millennial-aged carbon—implying that peatland carbon accumulated over millennia is being rapidly released to the atmosphere through these lake systems. The finding has significant implications for understanding carbon cycle dynamics in tropical peatlands and for predicting how climate change-driven peatland disturbance could lead to accelerated release of ancient carbon stores.

## Methods

Radiocarbon (¹⁴C) and stable carbon isotope (δ¹³C) measurements were conducted on dissolved organic carbon, dissolved CO₂, and dissolved CH₄ in water samples collected from large humic lakes in the Congo Basin. Radiocarbon ages were determined via accelerator mass spectrometry, with careful blank corrections and calibration. Stable isotope compositions were analyzed by isotope ratio mass spectrometry. The radiocarbon ages of dissolved organic and inorganic carbon were compared to determine whether lakes preferentially released aged or modern carbon.

## Results

Radiocarbon analysis revealed that dissolved organic carbon and CO₂ in the studied lakes contain substantial contributions from millennial-aged carbon (hundreds to thousands of years old), with radiocarbon ages indicating peat-derived organic matter. The aged carbon composition demonstrates that these lakes effectively mobilize and transport peat-stored carbon from upstream peatland systems. Seasonal and spatial variations in radiocarbon ages reflect changes in peatland water table elevation and hydrological connectivity, which control the proportion of aged versus modern peat carbon entering the lake systems.

## Implications

Large humic lakes in tropical peatlands serve as sites where ancient carbon accumulated in peat soils over millennia is rapidly remobilized and outgassed as greenhouse gases. This finding highlights the vulnerability of peatland carbon stores to mobilization and atmospheric release. Climate change-driven changes in water table elevation and drought could accelerate peat carbon mobilization through these lake systems, creating a positive feedback to warming. The results suggest that tropical peatland-lake systems should be prioritized in carbon cycle modeling and climate change impact assessments.
