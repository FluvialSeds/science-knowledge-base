# "Old" carbon entering the South China Sea from the carbonate-rich Pearl River Basin: Coupled action of carbonate weathering and aquatic photosynthesis

## Overall Scientific Topic

Negative Δ¹⁴C values in riverine particulate organic carbon (POC) are commonly interpreted as evidence of old carbon from erosion of deep soils and sedimentary rocks. This study challenges this interpretation by demonstrating that in carbonate-rich river basins, negative Δ¹⁴C values in POC can result from contemporary aquatic photosynthesis utilizing "old" dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) derived from carbonate weathering, not necessarily from old terrestrial organic matter. Understanding these processes has important implications for interpreting carbon age in rivers and coastal oceans.

## Methods

Water samples were collected from 19 locations in the Pearl River Basin during both rainy and dry seasons. Dual carbon isotope analysis (¹⁴C and ¹³C) was performed on both dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) and particulate organic carbon (POC). Aquatic plant samples were also analyzed. Chlorophyll a concentrations, total suspended matter, and major hydrochemical parameters were measured. Results from the carbonate-rich Pearl River were compared to findings from silicate-dominated river basins.

## Results

All riverine DIC and POC samples from carbonate-dominated reaches had negative Δ¹⁴C values, indicating "old" carbon (ranging from 680–3,085 yr BP for DIC and 1,235–3,075 yr BP for POC). A regression relationship between Δ¹⁴C of POC and DIC was observed during dry season when aquatic photosynthesis was strong. This relationship demonstrates that contemporary aquatic organisms are producing POC with negative Δ¹⁴C values by utilizing DIC from carbonate weathering. The "dead carbon effect" of carbonate rocks—wherein weathering of ¹⁴C-free carbonates produces "old" DIC—coupled with contemporary photosynthesis explains these observations.

## Implications

These findings indicate that previous studies suggesting riverine POC depleted in ¹⁴C is necessarily old carbon are problematic in carbonate-dominated river basins. River basins rich in carbonates can release "old" POC that is actually produced by modern autotrophic processes, highlighting the need to examine both DIC and autochthonous organic carbon concentrations to correctly assess carbon cycling in carbonate-rich systems.
