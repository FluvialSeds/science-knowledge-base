# Mass-independent fractionation processes in the atmosphere

## Overall Scientific Topic

This comprehensive review synthesizes the theoretical, experimental, and observational understanding of mass-independent fractionation (MIF) processes in Earth's atmosphere—a phenomenon discovered 40 years ago that revolutionized our ability to measure and interpret atmospheric composition in the past and present. MIF occurs when isotope fractionation deviates from expected mass-dependent relationships, a process limited to elements with more than three stable isotopes forming gas-phase molecules with complex rotational-vibrational structure in the UV-visible wavelength range. The review focuses primarily on oxygen-bearing (O-MIF) and sulfur-bearing (S-MIF) species while introducing emerging applications in mercury and selenium. MIF signals preserve information about atmospheric composition in the geologic record and provide the most direct proxies for understanding past atmospheric conditions, especially in the deep geologic past.

## Methods

This is a comprehensive review synthesizing existing literature from multiple research subdisciplines. The authors compile theoretical understanding of MIF mechanisms (photon-mediated self-shielding, nuclear spin effects, symmetry effects), summarize experimental observations from controlled laboratory studies, and synthesize observational data from modern atmosphere measurements and the geologic record. They compile all existing experimental, observational, and geologic O-MIF and S-MIF data, including oxygen isotope signatures from ozone, stratospheric water vapor, hydrogen peroxide, carbon monoxide/dioxide, perchlorate, nitrous oxide, carbonate aerosols, nitrate aerosols, sulfate aerosols, and molecular oxygen. For sulfur MIF, they compile data from sulfur dioxide and other gaseous species. The review includes examination of ice-core records, geologic records from the Archean and Proterozoic, and recent reinterpretations of sulfur isotope slope changes (Δ³⁶S/Δ³³S) and carbonate-associated sulfate data.

## Results

The compilation reveals that: (1) MIF arises in Earth's atmosphere through photon-mediated processes including self-shielding (where abundant isotopologues shield minor ones from available photons), nuclear spin effects (primarily in ³³S and mercury isotopes), and symmetry effects in certain molecules; (2) O-MIF signatures are ubiquitous in atmospheric gases and preserve information about ancient atmospheric composition, with applications to gross primary productivity reconstruction and paleo-atmospheric composition; (3) S-MIF in the Archean and Proterozoic geological record reflects episodes of reduced atmospheric oxygen; (4) Multiple fractionation regimes exist within gas columns depending on photon availability and distance from radiation source; and (5) Open questions remain regarding exact fractionation factors, chemical mechanisms in complex atmospheric systems, and interpretations of certain geologic signals.

## Implications

MIF signals represent the strongest and most direct proxies available for understanding Earth's atmospheric composition in the deep geologic past, particularly before the Great Oxidation Event. O-MIF and S-MIF records in the geologic archive directly reflect atmospheric oxidation state and are minimally affected by secondary processes like diagenesis compared to other paleoclimate proxies. Future research should target: mechanistic understanding of MIF in emerging isotope systems (mercury, selenium); improved quantification of fractionation factors across atmospheric conditions; reconciliation of apparent inconsistencies in certain geologic MIF records; and development of in situ MIF measurement techniques. These advances will enhance our ability to reconstruct ancient atmospheric compositions and validate hypotheses about atmospheric evolution through Earth history.
