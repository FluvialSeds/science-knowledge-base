# Radiocarbon distribution and the effect of legacy in lakes of the McMurdo Dry Valleys, Antarctica

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study investigates radiocarbon distribution and reservoir effects in Antarctic ice-covered lakes of the McMurdo Dry Valleys. The research identifies highly ¹⁴C-deficient lake waters with ages up to 27,000 ¹⁴C years B.P., attributed to mixing of glacial meltwater, permafrost seeps, and subglacial discharge with distinct radiocarbon signatures. The hydrologic history of these lakes can be traced through ¹⁴C profiles and legacy effects from previous water sources.

## Methods

Samples were collected from various depths within perennially ice-covered Antarctic lakes and the streams feeding them. Dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), particulate organic carbon (POC), and sediment samples were analyzed for radiocarbon abundance. Water samples were collected using Kemmerer bottles from discrete depths and stream samples from source, middle, and mouth locations. Large volumes of lake water (20-40 liters) were filtered for POC analysis onto precombusted glass-fiber filters. All radiocarbon analyses were performed using accelerator mass spectrometry with corrections for age determination.

## Results

Stream gradient and length affected equilibration of water with the modern atmosphere prior to entering lakes. DIC in seasonal ice-free 'moat' water depended on the ratio of modern meltwater input versus old glacial input. Under ice cover, ¹⁴C ages of lake-water DIC and organic matter depended on lake history, composition, and particle fallout. The bottom waters of Lake Bonney's west lobe exhibited a DIC age of approximately 27,000 ¹⁴C years before present, among the most radiocarbon-deficient lake waters identified globally. Comparison of radiocarbon profiles revealed that deep saline water is currently being displaced over the sill separating the lake's two lobes.

## Implications

These findings demonstrate how radiocarbon reservoir effects in polar lake systems are controlled by complex mixing of waters with distinct carbon sources and ages. Understanding these ¹⁴C patterns provides insights into hydrologic history, permafrost contributions, and glacial dynamics in Antarctica. The results have implications for accurate radiocarbon dating of polar lake sediments and for constraining paleoclimate reconstructions in these sensitive polar environments.
