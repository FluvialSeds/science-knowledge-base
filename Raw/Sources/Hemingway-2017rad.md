# Assessing the blank carbon contribution, isotope mass balance, and kinetic isotope fractionation of the ramped pyrolysis/oxidation instrument at NOSAMS

**Journal:** Radiocarbon, Volume 59, Issue 1, Pages 179–193 (2017)

## Overall Scientific Topic

Radiocarbon dating using ramped pyrolysis/oxidation (RPO) analysis requires careful quantification and correction of blank carbon—non-sample carbon introduced during sample preparation and analysis. This study characterizes the NOSAMS (National Ocean Sciences AMS Facility) RPO blank across a typical analytical temperature range (150–1000°C at 5°C/min heating rate), reporting a mean blank of 3.6 μg C with a modern radiocarbon composition (Fm = 0, indicating no ¹⁴C content).

## Methods

The researchers directly trapped and analyzed CO₂ evolved from empty, precombusted reactor inserts over the full analytical temperature range to characterize blank carbon mass and isotope composition. Blank carbon was assessed using both isotope dilution and "modern-dead" correction methods, comparing results to accepted standard reference materials (SRM). The study also evaluates kinetic isotope fractionation during thermal analysis—the preferential release of ¹²C versus ¹³C at different temperatures—which can bias stable carbon isotope measurements if not properly accounted for.

## Results

The NOSAMS RPO blank averages 3.6 μg C distributed across the full temperature range, with a radiocarbon signature indicating modern (non-radioactive) carbon. Kinetic isotope fractionation was observed during heating, with implications for accurate δ¹³C measurements. The study demonstrates that blank carbon correction is essential for accurate radiocarbon dating, particularly for small samples where blank contribution can represent a significant fraction of total measured carbon. Standard reference materials analyzed alongside blanks show the effectiveness of both isotope dilution and "modern-dead" correction methods.

## Implications

Careful characterization of blank carbon contribution is critical for radiocarbon measurements using RPO, especially for environmental samples with low carbon concentrations or high radiocarbon ages. The results provide guidelines for blank correction protocols and demonstrate the importance of rigorous analytical quality assurance in radiocarbon dating. Understanding kinetic isotope fractionation during thermal analysis ensures accurate stable isotope measurements alongside radiocarbon determinations.
