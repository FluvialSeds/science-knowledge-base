# Theoretical estimates of sulfoxyanion triple-oxygen equilibrium isotope effects and their implications


## Overall Scientific Topic

Triple-oxygen isotope analysis of sulfate (δ¹⁸O and Δ¹⁷O) is increasingly used to assess sulfur-cycle processes in modern environments and the geologic past. However, many important sulfur redox reactions proceed through intermediate sulfoxyanion species—such as sulfite, sulfoxylate, and thiosulfate—which may rapidly exchange oxygen atoms with water. To interpret sulfate isotope records, quantitative knowledge of equilibrium oxygen isotope fractionation factors between water and each sulfoxyanion species is essential. This study uses quantum computational chemistry to theoretically estimate oxygen isotope fractionation factors for a comprehensive suite of sulfoxyanions, addressing the lack of experimental constraints on these short-lived intermediate species.

## Methods

Oxygen isotope fractionation factors (¹⁸α and ¹⁷α) were estimated using quantum computational chemistry, specifically density functional theory calculations with the B3LYP/6-31G+(d,p) functional. To assess methodological sensitivity, higher-order computational methods (CCSD/aug-cc-pVTZ and MP2/aug-cc-pVTZ) were used to derive scaling factors for different redox states. Anharmonic zero-point energy (ZPE) corrections were qualitatively evaluated using gaseous sulfoxy compounds. Theoretical predictions were compared against available experimental fractionation factor data for sulfate, sulfite, and thiosulfate systems across a range of pH values and temperatures.

## Results

Theoretical bulk-solution ¹⁸α predictions yielded root-mean-square errors (RMSE) of 4.5‰ for sulfate/water, 3.7‰ for sulfite/water, and 2.2‰ for thiosulfate/water equilibrium when compared to experimental data. Agreement improved significantly when predictions were compared only to single species (SO₃(OH) for sulfite, RMSE = 1.6‰; SO₂(OH) for sulfite, RMSE = 2.2‰) rather than bulk solutions, particularly at high and low pH where individual species dominate. Methodological scaling factors greatly affected ¹⁸α predictions, while ZPE corrections were small (≤1‰) at Earth-surface temperatures. The study calculated that sulfate, sulfite, sulfoxylate, and thiosulfate species can exhibit Δ¹⁷O values 0.199‰, 0.205‰, 0.101‰, and 0.186‰ more negative than equilibrated water, respectively (reference line slope = 0.5305).

## Implications

The theoretical fractionation factor framework provides a foundation for interpreting triple-oxygen isotope observations in sulfur-cycle processes including pyrite oxidation, microbial sulfate reduction, thiosulfate disproportionation, and hydrothermal anhydrite precipitation. The results highlight that individual sulfoxyanion species (not bulk solutions) control isotope fractionation, particularly when specific species dominate at particular pH conditions. This work enables more mechanistic interpretation of sulfate δ¹⁸O and Δ¹⁷O records in modern and ancient environments, improving constraints on Earth's redox state evolution and sulfur biogeochemistry.
