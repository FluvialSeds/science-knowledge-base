# Biomarker Approaches for Reconstructing Terrestrial Environmental Change

## Overall Scientific Topic

Terrestrial paleoenvironmental reconstruction relies on organic biomarkers—source-specific organic molecules whose abundance, distribution, and isotopic composition preserve information about past environmental conditions on land. This review synthesizes biomarker approaches for reconstructing three classes of paleoenvironmental variables: physical climate variables (land temperature and precipitation), ecosystem state variables (vegetation composition and fire regime), and biogeochemical cycling variables (soil carbon residence time and methane cycling). By analyzing multiple biomarkers in tandem across key compound classes including lipids, lignin, and carbohydrates, paleoceanographers and paleoclimatologists can gain unique insights into how the Earth system has responded to climate perturbations across geological time.

## Methods

The review evaluates biomarker approaches across multiple organic compound classes that vary in source organism and environmental sensitivity. Plant-derived lipids (n-alkanes and n-alkanoic acids) from plant leaf waxes and roots provide chemotaxonomic signals that differentiate among desert shrub, conifer forest, and herbaceous vegetation types. The diagnostic information in molecular distributions—particularly the carbon number patterns of different compound classes—enables classification of paleovegetation and paleoclimate conditions in modern systems, which can then be applied to ancient sediments. Additional biomarker approaches leverage isotopic composition of biomarkers to track soil carbon residence time, track vegetation-specific carbon fractionation patterns, and reconstruct temperature variations. Machine learning approaches can enhance classification power from multivariate biomarker distributions.

## Results

The review demonstrates that multiple independent biomarker proxies, when analyzed together, provide complementary insights into terrestrial paleoenvironmental change. Plant wax lipid distributions successfully distinguish among modern vegetation types and can be calibrated to paleoclimate variables including annual mean temperature and precipitation. Isotopic composition of biomarkers—including hydrogen, carbon, and oxygen isotope ratios—records information about plant water stress, photosynthetic pathways, and soil processes. Fire-related biomarkers including polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons and charcoal abundance provide records of past fire regimes and their relationships to climate and vegetation changes. The integration of multiple biomarker classes in tandem reveals Earth system responses to major climate perturbations that individual proxies might not capture.

## Implications

Biomarker approaches provide high-resolution paleoenvironmental records that enable quantitative reconstruction of physical climate variables, ecosystem composition and function, and biogeochemical cycling in terrestrial systems across millions of years. These proxies are particularly valuable for understanding how terrestrial ecosystems respond to abrupt climate change, with direct implications for predicting terrestrial ecosystem responses to future anthropogenic warming. The methodological frameworks reviewed here enable paleoclimatologists to move beyond binary ecosystem state descriptions toward quantitative, continuous paleoclimate reconstructions. Analyzing multiple biomarkers in tandem maximizes the information extracted from geological records while also providing internal consistency checks on paleoenvironmental interpretations.
