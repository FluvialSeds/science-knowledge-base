# Carbon isotope biogeochemistry of tropical small mountainous river, estuarine, and coastal systems of Puerto Rico

## Overall Scientific Topic

Small mountainous rivers (SMRs) may act as significant sources of aged and/or refractory carbon to coastal oceans, potentially increasing organic carbon burial and subsidizing coastal food webs. However, the characteristics and spatial-temporal variability of carbon exported from tropical SMR systems remain poorly constrained. This study examines the abundance and isotopic character of dissolved and particulate organic carbon (DOC and POC) and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in two Puerto Rico SMRs with different land uses and their associated estuaries and coastal waters.

## Methods

Two Puerto Rican small mountainous rivers with catchments dominated by different land uses (agricultural vs. non-agricultural recovering forest) were sampled for DIC, DOC, and POC concentrations and carbon isotope composition (δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C). Associated estuarine and coastal water samples were also analyzed. The abundance and character of carbon pools were measured across spatial and temporal gradients with particular attention to seasonal and land-use-related variability.

## Results

Riverine DOC and POC concentrations were highly variable, ranging from modern to highly aged (2,340 years BP), while DIC was always modern. The agricultural river and irrigation canals contained very old DOC (1,184 and 2,340 years BP respectively), consistent with findings in temperate SMRs and indicating tropical SMRs provide a source of aged DOC to the ocean. During high river discharge, organic matter in estuarine and coastal waters had isotopic signatures reflective of direct terrestrial input, indicating relatively unaltered organic matter is transported to the coastal ocean at these times.

## Implications

Carbon transported to coastal oceans by tropical SMRs differs from that of larger rivers, being exported from smaller catchments with steeper terrains and fewer land-use types. These systems export a mixture of young and aged carbon with important implications for understanding carbon cycling in tropical coastal regions affected by small mountainous rivers.
