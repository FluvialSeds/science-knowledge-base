# Sources of dissolved inorganic carbon in two small streams with different bedrock geology: insights from carbon isotopes

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study determines the sources of dissolved inorganic carbon in two small streams with contrasting bedrock geology using stable and radiocarbon isotopes. In the limestone stream, carbonate weathering provided 14-22% of DIC, while in the granite stream, carbonate input was minimal. The research demonstrates that in small streams, dissolution of atmospheric CO2 and carbonate rock weathering are more important DIC sources than in larger rivers.

## Methods

Fieldwork was conducted over four seasons (June, September, December 2009, and March-April 2010) at the Seri River (limestone bedrock, 30 km2 watershed) and Fudoji Experimental Watershed (granite bedrock, 6.0 km2 watershed) in central Japan. For each site and season, four replicates of DIC, DOC, and POC were collected. Water samples were collected in glass bottles and analyzed for δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C. Limestone cobbles from the Seri site were analyzed for carbonate δ¹³C values. Mass balance calculations using δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C values determined the proportional contributions of three end-members (atmospheric CO2, organic matter degradation, and carbonate rock) to stream DIC.

## Results

Δ¹⁴C values of DIC were less variable (-238 to -174‰ for limestone stream; -23 to +10‰ for granite stream) compared to DOC and POC. Based on mass balance, atmospheric CO2 contributed 47-57% of DIC in the limestone stream and 74-90% in the granite stream. Organic matter degradation contributed 29-35% in limestone and 4-21% in granite. Carbonate rock weathering contributed 14-22% in limestone and only 4-6% in granite. Results were compared with previous studies of larger rivers, supporting the hypothesis that atmospheric CO2 dissolution and carbonate weathering are more important carbon sources in small streams.

## Implications

The finding that carbonate weathering provides a more significant proportion of DIC in small streams compared to larger rivers suggests that stream size and carbonate substrate type are important controls on carbon cycling pathways. This affects understanding of how small streams contribute to terrestrial carbon cycling and has implications for predicting how land-use changes affecting small streams might alter regional carbon export patterns to downstream systems.
