# Clay hydroxyl isotopes show an enhanced hydrologic cycle during the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum

## Overall Scientific Topic

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) was an abrupt global warming event approximately 56 million years ago, characterized by a massive injection of carbon into the ocean-atmosphere system and substantial hydrologic perturbations. This study uses the isotopic composition of hydroxyl groups (OH⁻) in clay minerals as a novel proxy for reconstructing past hydrologic cycling during this climate transient. Clay hydroxyl isotopes provide a more robust record of water cycle variability than traditional bulk oxygen isotopes because they are less influenced by clay mineral compositional changes and inheritance effects. High-resolution measurements of clay OH⁻ oxygen and hydrogen isotopes from a well-preserved PETM section in the North Sea Basin reveal that the hydrologic cycle was significantly enhanced during the carbon isotope excursion, indicating increased precipitation intensity and water cycling in response to the rapid warming event.

## Methods

The study isolated hydroxyl groups from clay minerals extracted from a highly expanded Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) section at well 22/10a-4 in the North Sea Basin. Oxygen (δ¹⁸O) and hydrogen (δD) isotope ratios were measured from both the isolated hydroxyl groups (δ¹⁸O_OH and δD_OH) and bulk clay minerals (δ¹⁸O_bulk). The hydroxyl isotope approach is specifically designed to avoid potential biases introduced by clay mineral compositional variations and inherited isotopic signatures from source regions. By measuring the isotopic composition of the hydroxyl groups specifically—rather than relying solely on bulk oxygen isotope ratios—the authors expected this record to provide a more robust and direct reflection of hydroclimate variability during the PETM. Vertical sample resolution through the PETM section enabled high-resolution reconstruction of hydrologic changes across the carbon isotope excursion.

## Results

Clay hydroxyl oxygen and hydrogen isotope records from the North Sea Basin section reveal dramatic changes across the onset of the PETM carbon isotope excursion (CIE). Hydrogen isotope values (δD) of clay OH⁻ decrease abruptly by approximately 8‰ at the CIE onset, indicating a substantial shift toward more negative values. This large negative shift in δD is characteristic of enhanced precipitation and increased water cycling in response to the warming event. The comparison of clay OH⁻ isotope trends with bulk clay δ¹⁸O values demonstrates that the hydroxyl group isotopes provide a cleaner signal of hydrologic variability, less affected by compositional mixing effects and detrital inheritance. The timing of the isotopic shift coincides precisely with the onset of the carbon isotope excursion, indicating a rapid hydrologic response to the initial carbon input event. The magnitude and character of the isotopic change indicate that the hydrologic cycle was substantially intensified during the PETM, consistent with model predictions of enhanced water cycling during rapid warming events.

## Implications

The clay hydroxyl isotope records demonstrate that the hydrologic cycle underwent dramatic intensification during the PETM in response to rapid global warming, directly validating paleoclimate model predictions of enhanced water cycling in warm climates. This study establishes clay hydroxyl isotopes as a novel and robust paleoproxy for reconstructing past hydrologic variability, offering advantages over traditional bulk clay oxygen isotopes by avoiding compositional mixing biases and detrital inheritance effects. The rapid hydrologic response at the onset of the carbon isotope excursion indicates tight coupling between carbon cycle perturbations and hydrologic changes, suggesting that abrupt carbon inputs to the ocean-atmosphere system trigger immediate intensification of the water cycle. These findings have implications for understanding modern and future climate change, as they demonstrate that warming events drive substantial increases in precipitation intensity and water cycling. The PETM represents a natural analog for rapid warming and provides insight into how the hydrologic cycle responds to CO₂-driven climate change on relatively short geological timescales. The methodology developed here—measuring isotopes of specifically isolated mineral hydroxyl groups—opens new avenues for paleoclimatic reconstruction using clay minerals, which are abundant and widespread in sedimentary records across geological time.
