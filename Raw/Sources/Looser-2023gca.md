# Thermally-induced clumped isotope resetting in belemnite and optical calcites: towards material-specific kinetics

## Overall Scientific Topic

Clumped isotope paleothermometry provides quantitative constraints on ancient temperatures preserved in carbonate minerals. However, the utility of this technique depends on understanding how thermal burial alters the original clumped isotope signature through bond reordering. This study characterizes the kinetics of thermally-induced clumped isotope resetting in two contrasting calcite materials: biogenic belemnite rostral calcite and abiogenic optical calcite. High-temperature annealing experiments reveal that the rate of clumped isotope reordering depends strongly on carbonate mineralogy and material properties, with activation energies and frequency factors differing by orders of magnitude between materials. The results establish a framework for interpreting clumped isotope records from naturally buried samples and for developing material-specific paleothermometric calibrations.

## Methods

Belemnite rostral calcite and natural optical calcite samples were heated in sealed capsules at temperatures ranging from 500–1,000°C for durations from minutes to weeks. At multiple time intervals, samples were rapidly quenched and analyzed for clumped isotope composition (Δ47) using high-precision isotope ratio mass spectrometry. The degree of reordering was quantified by tracking deviation of Δ47 values from the initial unheated composition and from the equilibrium high-temperature value. Kinetic parameters were extracted by fitting experimental data to first-order reaction models and Arrhenius equations relating reaction rate to temperature.

## Results

Both belemnite and optical calcite showed predictable, temperature-dependent rates of clumped isotope reordering, but kinetic parameters differed substantially between materials. Belemnite calcite showed activation energies of ~168–181 kJ mol⁻¹, while optical calcite showed higher activation energies of ~217–291 kJ mol⁻¹. Frequency factors differed by >20 orders of magnitude between materials, indicating fundamentally different reaction mechanisms. The results establish that calcite mineralogy, crystal structure, and impurity content control the kinetics of isotope reordering. Biogenic calcites (such as belemnites) generally reset faster than abiogenic optical calcites at equivalent burial temperatures.

## Implications

Material-specific kinetic parameters enable more accurate paleothermometric interpretation of clumped isotope records from naturally buried carbonate minerals. The results demonstrate that paleothermometric calibrations must account for the source and type of carbonate material being analyzed. Kinetic understanding allows forward modeling of how clumped isotope signatures change during burial, enabling thermal history reconstruction from carbonate samples that have experienced complex thermal pathways. The framework provides a template for improving paleoceanographic and paleoclimate reconstructions based on carbonate-hosted isotopic proxies.
