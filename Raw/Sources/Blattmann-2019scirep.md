# Sulphuric acid-mediated weathering on Taiwan buffers geological atmospheric carbon sinks

## Overall Scientific Topic

Taiwan is one of the most rapidly uplifting orogens with erosion rates of 3–6 mm/yr, continuously exposing fresh minerals for chemical weathering. This study investigates how chemical weathering pathways, particularly sulphuric acid-mediated weathering, affect the carbon budget of rapidly eroding mountain systems by analyzing the Gaoping River, Taiwan's second largest river by sediment discharge.

## Methods

Surface water samples were collected from the Gaoping River catchment during the dry season (February 2017, 2018) and wet season (June and October 2017). Filtered water samples were analyzed for dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) using ¹³C and ¹⁴C isotope compositions via CO₂ sparging-mass spectrometry. Major cations and anions were measured by ion chromatography. A constrained linear least-squares inverse modelling approach was used to determine contributions from different weathering reactions based on mineral unit-normalised stoichiometries, DI¹⁴C fingerprints, and ionic ratios.

## Results

Major dissolved ion chemistry and radiocarbon signatures of DIC reveal the importance of pyrite-derived sulphuric acid weathering on silicates and carbonates. Two-thirds of the dissolved load of the Gaoping River derives from sulphuric acid-mediated weathering of rocks within its catchment. The river exhibits the lowest reported DI¹⁴C signatures for a small mountainous river (43 to 71 percent modern carbon), with rock-derived carbonate constituting a ¹⁴C-free DIC source. Weathering on Taiwan releases 0.31 ± 0.12 MtC/yr of CO₂ to the atmosphere over geological timescales.

## Implications

The results reveal that weathering on Taiwan is offset by burial of terrestrial biospheric organic carbon in offshore sediments, tipping the balance such that the overall system acts as a net sink, consuming 0.24 ± 0.13 MtC/yr of atmospheric CO₂ over geological timescales. This study demonstrates that sulphuric acid weathering plays a crucial role in regulating long-term atmospheric CO₂ concentrations in rapidly eroding orogens.
