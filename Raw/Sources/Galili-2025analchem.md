# High-precision oxygen-isotope analysis of iron (oxyhydr)oxides using high-temperature conversion isotope ratio mass spectrometry

## Overall Scientific Topic

Iron (oxyhydr)oxide minerals such as goethite and hematite are ubiquitous in environmental contexts and their oxygen-isotopic compositions provide robust proxies for reconstructing past hydrologic conditions and paleoclimate on timescales ranging from millennia to billions of years. However, access to oxygen-isotope measurements of iron oxides has been limited because existing methods rely on specialized and hazardous fluorination techniques available in only a small number of laboratories worldwide. This study introduces a novel high-precision method for oxygen-isotope analysis using high-temperature conversion isotope ratio mass spectrometry (HTC-IRMS), where finely ground iron (oxyhydr)oxide and graphite mixtures are heated to 1450°C in helium, converting oxygen to CO gas with nearly 100% yield for continuous-flow IRMS analysis, achieving precision of ±0.15‰ with excellent agreement to traditional fluorination methods.

## Methods

A mixture of finely ground iron (oxyhydr)oxide sample and graphite is heated to 1450°C in a helium flow environment using a high-temperature conversion inlet. The conversion process liberates oxygen from the mineral matrix by generating CO gas with nearly 100% yield. The resulting CO gas is analyzed via continuous-flow isotope ratio mass spectrometry (CF-IRMS) to determine oxygen-isotopic composition (δ¹⁸O). Sample preparation involves precise grinding and weighing of samples combined with graphite in stoichiometric proportions optimized for conversion efficiency. Analysis is conducted with replicate measurements to establish precision and accuracy, with comparison to reference materials and traditional fluorination methods to validate methodology.

## Results

The HTC-IRMS method achieves precision of ±0.15‰ (1σ, n = 28) for oxygen-isotope analysis of iron (oxyhydr)oxides. Results show excellent agreement with traditional fluorination methods and demonstrate improved precision over existing techniques. The method successfully liberates oxygen from diverse iron oxide mineralogy including goethite (α-FeOOH) and hematite (α-Fe₂O₃), with conversion yields approaching 100%. The technique is practical, safe, and does not require handling of reactive or hazardous halogen gases necessary for traditional methods, making it accessible to a broader range of laboratories.

## Implications

This novel HTC-IRMS method dramatically expands access to oxygen-isotope measurements of iron oxides by eliminating the need for specialized fluorination equipment and hazardous chemical handling. The improved precision and accessibility enhance the utility of iron oxide oxygen isotopes as palehydrologic and paleoclimate proxies in Earth and environmental sciences. The technique enables more researchers worldwide to employ iron oxide δ¹⁸O as a quantitative tracer for reconstructing past environmental conditions, hydrologic variability, and paleoclimate evolution across diverse settings and timescales.
