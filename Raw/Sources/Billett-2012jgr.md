# Variable source and age of different forms of carbon released from natural peatland pipes

## Overall Scientific Topic

Soil pipes are significant subsurface structural features of peatlands that have been recorded in UK blanket peats but have received little research attention regarding their role in carbon release from peatlands. This study uses carbon isotope composition (¹⁴C and δ¹³C) to measure the source and age of different carbon forms (DOC, POC, dissolved CO₂, and CH₄) released from natural peat pipes under different hydrological conditions.

## Methods

Samples were collected from three natural peat pipes and the downstream catchment outlet of a small peatland in northern England. Sampling was conducted under different hydrological extremes (high flows associated with storm events and low flows before or after storms) to explore variability in carbon sources as flow paths change. Carbon isotopes and stable isotope compositions were analyzed for all carbon species collected from pipe outlets and stream waters.

## Results

The δ¹³C composition of organic carbon differed significantly from dissolved gases, with δ¹³C-DOC ranging from -28.6‰ to -27.6‰ and δ¹³C-POC from -28.1‰ to -26.1‰, while δ¹³C-CO₂ ranged from -20.5‰ to +1.1‰ and δ¹³C-CH₄ from -67.7‰ to -42.0‰. The ¹⁴C age of DOC was consistently modern at all sites; POC varied from modern to 653 years BP, and evasion CO₂ from modern to 996 years BP. The isotopic composition of dissolved gases was more variable than DOC and POC, with individual pipes showing different ¹³C enrichment or depletion patterns during storm events.

## Implications

Natural pipes vent old, deep peat CO₂ and POC (but not DOC) to the atmosphere, indicating that pipeflow provides connectivity with deep peat carbon stores. The sources of CO₂ and CH₄ are significantly more variable and dynamic than DOC and POC, suggesting that changing pipeflow patterns associated with environmental change may alter carbon export dynamics from peatlands.
