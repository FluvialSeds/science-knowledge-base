# Aquatic export of young dissolved and gaseous carbon from a pristine boreal fen: Implications for peat carbon stock stability

## Overall Scientific Topic

The stability of northern peatland carbon stores under changing climate is critical for the global carbon cycle. This study presents a full-year characterization of radiocarbon content (¹⁴C) of dissolved organic carbon (DOC), carbon dioxide (CO₂), and methane (CH₄) exported from a boreal peatland catchment, coupled with ¹⁴C characterization of the catchment's peat profile to assess whether ancient carbon stocks are being mobilized.

## Methods

Samples were collected throughout one year from a pristine boreal fen catchment in Sweden. Radiocarbon analysis was conducted on DOC, CO₂, and CH₄ collected from stream water at multiple locations and times. The catchment's peat profile was also characterized for radiocarbon content of the same carbon species at various depths. Complementary analyses included water chemistry and hydrological measurements to understand flow pathways and carbon transport mechanisms.

## Results

The age of aquatic carbon in runoff varied little throughout the year and appeared to be sustained by recently fixed carbon from the atmosphere (less than 60 years old), despite stream DOC, CO₂, and CH₄ being primarily sourced from deep peat horizons (2–4 m) near the mire's outlet. The ¹⁴C content of DOC, CO₂, and CH₄ across the entire peat profile was considerably enriched with post-bomb carbon compared with the solid peat material. Results demonstrate little to no mobilization of ancient carbon stocks from this boreal peatland.

## Implications

The findings show a relatively large resilience of the source of aquatic carbon export to forecasted hydroclimatic changes. Young radiocarbon signatures of exported carbon suggest limited vulnerability of deep peat stores to mobilization through aquatic pathways. The enrichment of dissolved and gaseous carbon with post-bomb ¹⁴C throughout the peat profile indicates that peat pore waters are characterized by recently fixed carbon even at depth, possibly due to diffusion of modern carbon and continuous exchange with younger material.
