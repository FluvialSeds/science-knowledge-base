# Widespread occurrence of dissolved oxygen anomalies, aerobic microbes, and oxygen-producing metabolic pathways in apparently anoxic environments

## Overall Scientific Topic

While nearly all molecular oxygen (O₂) on Earth is produced via oxygenic photosynthesis, recent research indicates that light-independent O₂ production occurs at much larger scales than historically appreciated. This minireview synthesizes evidence that both abiotic processes (water radiolysis) and biotic pathways (chlorite dismutation, nitric oxide dismutation, and methanobactin-mediated water lysis) generate O₂ in dark, apparently anoxic environments. The presence of strictly aerobic microorganisms thriving in anoxic habitats, nanomolar concentrations of dissolved O₂ in groundwaters and sediments, and metagenomic evidence for widespread distribution of oxygen-producing metabolic capabilities across bacterial phyla indicate that "dark oxygen production" (DOP) is an important yet overlooked biogeochemical process with far-reaching implications for subsurface biogeochemistry and microbial ecology.

## Methods

This minireview compiles and synthesizes existing experimental, microbiological, metagenomic, and geochemical studies of light-independent oxygen production. The authors review published work on abiotic oxygen production via water radiolysis and on three known biotic DOP pathways: chlorite dismutation (CLD), nitric oxide (NO) dismutation by heme-copper oxidoreductases (putative NOD), and methanobactin-dependent water lysis. They reanalyze published metagenomic datasets to determine phylogenetic distribution of NO dismutation genes (nod) across bacterial phyla. Compilation of isotopic signatures of dissolved O₂ in groundwater is reviewed to identify evidence for in situ O₂ production. The review synthesizes biochemical characterization of CLD (well-understood) and putative NOD (poorly characterized), and compares physiologies of different NO dismutation pathways in cultured organisms (Candidatus Methylomirabilis oxyfera, Nitrosopumilus maritimus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa).

## Results

Metagenomic analysis reveals that the enzyme putatively capable of NO dismutation forms four major phylogenetic clusters and occurs in at least 16 bacterial phyla, most notably in the Bacteroidota. Evidence indicates that NO dismutation evolved independently at least twice (different enzymatic mechanisms). Re-analysis of published isotopic signatures of dissolved O₂ in groundwater suggests in situ production in up to half of studied environments. Nanomolar concentrations of O₂ (nanaerobic conditions) are now recognized as common in nature through high-sensitivity oxygen sensors. Strictly aerobic microorganisms are confirmed to be present and metabolically active in many apparently anoxic habitats, indicating survival via DOP rather than photosynthesis or diffusion alone. Physiological comparisons reveal different NO dismutation pathways produce different O₂/byproduct stoichiometries and accumulate O₂ at different concentrations.

## Implications

Dark oxygen production represents a significant, previously underappreciated biogeochemical process that fundamentally changes our understanding of subsurface microbial ecology and physiology. The widespread occurrence of oxygen-producing metabolic pathways and the persistence of aerobic microbes in apparently anoxic environments indicates that cryptic O₂ cycling is ubiquitous and likely influences redox dynamics, nutrient cycling, and microbial community structure in subsurface ecosystems globally. Recognition of DOP has important implications for: (1) understanding microbe survival strategies in anoxic habitats; (2) modeling subsurface redox geochemistry; (3) predicting pollutant transport and degradation in groundwater; and (4) interpreting biogeochemical signals in sediments and natural waters. Future research should characterize the kinetics and distribution of DOP pathways, quantify their contribution to global oxygen fluxes, and determine their roles in coupled biogeochemical cycles throughout the subsurface biosphere.
