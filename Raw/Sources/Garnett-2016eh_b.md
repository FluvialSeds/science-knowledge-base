# A rapid method to collect methane from peatland streams for radiocarbon analysis

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study describes rapid methods for collecting methane samples from peatland streams for radiocarbon analysis based on headspace equilibration techniques. The research demonstrates that these methods enable swift (10-15 minutes per sample) collection from streams with varying methane concentrations, revealing substantial spatial variation in methane ages (approximately 400 to 3,000 years B.P.) that reflect different depths within peat profiles.

## Methods

An airtight 1-liter glass container (Kilner jar) with auto-shutoff couplings was used as the field method to rapidly quantify methane availability in stream water. Stream water samples were partially filling the container, which was then sealed and shaken to equilibrate methane across the water-air interface. A Detecto Pak infrared gas analyzer measured methane concentrations in the headspace. Based on field measurements, sufficient water volume was determined to collect required methane amounts. Manual headspace equilibration was performed using appropriate water-to-headspace ratios. Methane samples were then processed for radiocarbon analysis using standard protocols.

## Results

The method enabled collection of methane samples from two contrasting locations in a temperate raised peat bog with ages ranging from approximately 400 years B.P. in a ditch in afforested peatland to approximately 3,000 years B.P. in a bog perimeter stream. The substantial spatial variation in methane ages suggests that methane in stream water is derived from a wide range of peat depths. Field testing verified the reliability of the method through replicated measurements and comparison with methane standards. The rapid nature (10-15 minutes per sample) makes the method practical for deployment across multiple sites.

## Implications

This new rapid methodology makes ¹⁴C dating of methane in surface waters more accessible and practical, enabling increased understanding of carbon cycling within soil-water-atmosphere systems. The substantial age variation in stream methane reveals that both shallow and deep peat layers contribute to methane emissions, with important implications for understanding greenhouse gas production pathways and carbon mobilization in peatland ecosystems under climate change scenarios.
