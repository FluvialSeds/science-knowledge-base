# Ancient dissolved methane in inland waters revealed by a new collection method at low field concentrations for radiocarbon (¹⁴C) analysis

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study presents a novel method for collecting dissolved methane samples from freshwater systems for radiocarbon analysis where dissolved CH4 concentrations are typically low. The technique uses a hydrophobic, gas-permeable membrane coiled inside a collapsible vessel to accumulate sufficient CH4 for ¹⁴C analysis in short time periods, enabling radiocarbon characterization of dissolved methane sources and ages.

## Methods

A novel "Coiled Membrane Vessel" (CMV) was constructed using 6 meters of hydrophobic, gas-permeable membrane tubing inside a 15-liter collapsible water carrier with an 8-liter headspace. Water is pumped through the membrane tubing allowing dissolved gas to pass into the headspace. Following collection, the accumulated gas is transferred to foil gas bags and processed by removing CO2 with soda lime, oxidizing CH4 to CO2 at 950°C with a platinum catalyst, and analyzing the resulting CO2 for both ¹⁴C and δ¹³C using accelerator mass spectrometry.

## Results

Laboratory and field tests demonstrated that reliable dissolved δ¹³CH4 and ¹⁴CH₄ samples can be collected over short time periods of 4-24 hours at relatively low cost. Initial results support previous findings that dissolved CH4 in freshwater systems is significantly older than dissolved CO2 and other forms of aquatic carbon, indicating different source pathways. The method successfully collected samples from various surface water types including low-concentration environments.

## Implications

This new collection method enables radiocarbon analysis of dissolved methane in remote locations and low-concentration environments, making ¹⁴CH₄ analysis more accessible for terrestrial carbon cycle studies. The technique has potential applications for detecting unique ¹⁴C signatures from point sources like coal seam gas and landfill gas leaking into waterways, important for environmental monitoring and carbon accounting.
