# Biosynthetic isotope fractionation negligibly impacts biomarker ¹⁴C ages


## Overall Scientific Topic

Radiocarbon (¹⁴C) ages of acetogenic lipid biomarkers such as n-alkanes represent a powerful tool to track carbon-cycle turnover times in soils, fluvially exported sediments, and marine sediments. However, the fidelity of this approach requires that biomarker ¹⁴C ages accurately reflect the true age of the organic carbon, without bias from biosynthetic isotope fractionation. Biosynthetic fractionation—the preferential incorporation of lighter or heavier isotopes during organic compound synthesis—could potentially affect measured ¹⁴C ages if it preferentially incorporates younger or older carbon. This study investigates whether biosynthetic isotope fractionation of carbon during lipid synthesis impacts the radiocarbon ages of biomarkers.

## Methods

The researchers used both simple isotopic mass-balance approximations and computational chemistry methods to calculate the extent of mass-dependent fractionation during biosynthesis of acetogenic lipids (n-alkanes and related compounds). Both approaches assessed whether biosynthetic fractionation would introduce measurable bias to radiocarbon ages. The study considered fractionation during various metabolic steps including acetyl-CoA formation, chain elongation, and product formation.

## Results

Kinetic analysis reveals that biosynthetic mass-dependent fractionation (¹³C/¹²C) during lipid biosynthesis is modest, ranging from approximately 1.890 to 1.995. Importantly, such fractionation would introduce negligible bias to measured ¹⁴C ages—estimated impact is less than 5–10 years radiocarbon age, far below typical analytical uncertainty. The computational chemistry and approximation approaches yielded consistent results, validating the conclusions. These findings indicate that biosynthetic fractionation does not introduce significant systematic bias to radiocarbon ages of lipid biomarkers.

## Implications

Radiocarbon ages of acetogenic lipid biomarkers accurately reflect carbon-cycle turnover times without significant bias from biosynthetic isotope fractionation. This validates the use of biomarker ¹⁴C ages for reconstructing organic matter provenance, turnover times, and aging in environmental samples. The work provides confidence in paleoenvironmental and paleoclimate applications relying on biomarker radiocarbon measurements, particularly in contexts where understanding carbon-cycle dynamics is critical.
