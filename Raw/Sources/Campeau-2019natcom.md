# Current forest carbon fixation fuels stream CO₂ emissions

## Overall Scientific Topic

Stream CO₂ emissions contribute significantly to atmospheric climate forcing. Whilst groundwater inputs are known to sustain stream CO₂ emissions, the specific biogeochemical pathways and timescales involved have remained obscure. This study uses extensive radiocarbon (¹⁴C) characterization to demonstrate that stream CO₂ emissions are fueled by recently fixed forest carbon rather than ancient soil carbon stores.

## Methods

Radiocarbon characterization of CO₂ and DOC was conducted in stream water and groundwater sources from an old-growth boreal forest catchment. Sampling followed a three-level Upslope-Riparian-Stream transect, repeated over three occasions during the growing season. Year-round characterization of stream water ¹⁴C-CO₂ and ¹⁴C-DOC was supplemented with automated sensors recording hourly CO₂ concentrations to derive a complete annual carbon budget.

## Results

The ¹⁴C-CO₂ is consistently in tune with the current atmospheric ¹⁴C-CO₂ level (ranging from 102.5 to 105.3% modern) and shows little association with ¹⁴C-DOC in the same waters. Stream water ¹⁴C-CO₂ was surprisingly constant throughout the year, while stream water ¹⁴C-DOC was more variable. The findings demonstrate that soil respiration derived from current forest carbon fixation is the main source of stream CO₂ fluxes.

## Implications

These findings indicate that stream CO₂ emissions act as a shortcut returning CO₂ recently fixed by forest vegetation to the atmosphere. The results expose a positive feedback mechanism within the carbon budget of forested catchments, where stream CO₂ emissions will be highly sensitive to changes in forest carbon allocation patterns associated with climate and land-use changes. Understanding this linkage is critical for predicting how boreal forest carbon cycles will respond to future environmental changes.
