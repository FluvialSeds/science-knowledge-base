# Effects of agricultural and tillage practices on isotopic signatures and fluxes of organic and inorganic carbon in headwater streams

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study evaluates how agricultural practices influence carbon sources and export in six headwater streams across different land-use watersheds in northeastern Ohio. Results demonstrate that tilled corn agriculture produces the greatest carbon cycling impacts, with total carbon fluxes 314% higher than non-tilled corn and 39-76% higher than other land uses, driven by distinctive sources of C4 plant biomass and soil organic carbon.

## Methods

Six sub-watersheds in the North Appalachian Experimental Watershed in Coshocton County, Ohio were studied with land uses including: tilled corn, non-tilled corn, large mixed use, small mixed use, pasture, and hardwood forest. Headwater streams were sampled on one base-flow day in fall, spring, and summer, and one storm-flow day in spring and winter between October 2008 and August 2009. Water samples were analyzed for concentrations and δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and particulate organic carbon (POC). Soil samples were collected in spring 2009 from three watersheds. Bayesian mixing models using MixSIAR were applied to estimate relative carbon source contributions incorporating measured environmental variables and isotopic uncertainties.

## Results

Mixing model results indicated that in tilled and non-tilled corn watersheds, carbon sources were dominated by C4 plant biomass and soil organic carbon. In all other watershed types, carbon was dominated by C3 plant biomass. δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C values of forested stream carbon were distinct from other watershed types and showed greater contributions from modern-aged C3 biomass. Total carbon (DIC+DOC+POC) fluxes were significantly elevated in tilled corn watersheds relative to other land uses. Results demonstrate differential impacts of tillage practices on carbon cycling.

## Implications

Agricultural land use, particularly tilled corn cultivation, serves as a strong control over sources and fluxes of organic and inorganic carbon to headwater streams. The substantial increases in carbon export from tilled corn watersheds demonstrate how soil disturbance dramatically accelerates mobilization and transport of terrestrial carbon to aquatic systems. These findings have implications for understanding how global agricultural intensification affects carbon cycling at landscape scales and carbon delivery to coastal waters under contemporary and future land-use scenarios.
