# The geologic history of marine dissolved organic carbon from iron oxides

## Overall Scientific Topic

Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) is the largest reduced carbon reservoir in modern oceans and regulates marine communities and atmospheric CO₂ levels, yet the geologic history of marine DOC remains largely unconstrained. This study develops and validates a novel direct proxy for past DOC signatures by measuring the carbon-13 isotope composition and redox state of organic matter preserved in iron oxides. Using this proxy in paleoceanographic samples spanning Earth's history, the authors reconstruct how marine DOC reservoirs have evolved in response to ecosystem development, changes in sinking particle dynamics, and variations in deep-ocean oxygen availability. The results reveal that dissolved organic carbon dynamics have been fundamental to regulating atmospheric composition and marine ecosystem structure throughout Earth's history, with implications for understanding how climate change and ocean deoxygenation will affect future carbon cycling.

## Methods

Iron (oxyhydr)oxide minerals preserved in marine sediments are isolated and analyzed for their organic carbon content, carbon-13 isotope composition (δ¹³C), and redox state of bound organic matter. These oxides adsorb and preserve organic molecules from seawater, creating a direct archive of dissolved organic carbon composition. Paleoceanographic samples spanning billions of years are analyzed to reconstruct the evolution of marine DOC. The δ¹³C composition of adsorbed organic carbon is used to distinguish between photosynthetically derived DOC from different depths and to track ecosystem structure and autotrophic metabolism through time. Complementary analyses assess the oxidation state of preserved organic matter to distinguish between labile, semi-labile, semi-refractory, and refractory DOC compounds.

## Results

Oxygen-isotope measurements and organic carbon analysis of iron oxides in paleoceanographic samples reveal substantial changes in marine DOC composition through time. Modern ocean observations validate that iron oxides effectively preserve DOC signatures. Geologic records show that DOC reservoir size and composition have varied dramatically in response to changes in marine ecosystem structure, particle sinking speeds, and deep-ocean oxygen availability. The results indicate that during the Neoproterozoic Era (1,000-540 million years ago), before the evolution of large, rapidly-sinking eukaryotic organisms, the DOC reservoir was 100-1,000 times larger than modern levels. Subsequent evolution of efficient particle sinking substantially altered DOC dynamics, establishing modern-like conditions.

## Implications

This study establishes iron oxide-bound organic carbon as a powerful direct proxy for reconstructing marine DOC history and demonstrates that DOC reservoir dynamics have been fundamental to regulating both atmospheric composition and marine ecosystem evolution throughout Earth's history. The results indicate that changes in particle sinking efficiency (a consequence of evolving eukaryotic life forms) fundamentally altered carbon cycling in the ocean and atmosphere on billion-year timescales. These findings provide a mechanistic framework for understanding how future changes in ocean circulation, productivity, and oxygen availability will affect marine DOC reservoirs and atmospheric CO₂ levels in response to anthropogenic climate change.
