# Dissolved inorganic and organic carbon in an ephemeral fresh water stream in southern Arizona

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study presents a seven-year radiocarbon record of dissolved inorganic and organic carbon from an ephemeral stream in southern Arizona. The research reveals distinct relationships between carbon ages, stable carbon isotope values, and stream flow rates, with higher-flow events characterized by younger, more atmospheric-like carbon, while lower-flow periods show older, more depleted carbon derived from terrestrial soil sources.

## Methods

Freshwater samples from Sabino Creek were collected at various times during 2009-2016 from water exiting the Lower Sabino Dam (32°19'00"N, 110°48'35"W). Water samples were collected in 2-liter plastic containers, sealed, and transported to the AMS laboratory. Approximately 250 mL of water was transferred to sample vessels, evacuated of headspace, and acidified with phosphoric acid for DIC analysis. The evolved CO2 was collected cryogenically, measured volumetrically, and converted to graphite using standard AMS protocols. DOC samples were processed using KMnO4 oxidation after DIC removal. Both δ¹³C (offline analysis on VG Optima spectrometer) and ¹⁴C (Pelletron AMS) measurements were conducted. Flow rate data from the USGS stream gauge were integrated with radiocarbon results.

## Results

Distinct relationships emerged between ¹⁴C data and flow rates. Higher-flow events were characterized by F(DIC) values closer to atmospheric with less negative δ¹³C values. Lower-flow periods showed more negative δ¹³C for DIC while DOC δ¹³C remained relatively unchanged. During low-flow periods, particularly after summer rainy season, DIC showed depressed ¹⁴C while DOC remained close to contemporary. These trends suggest summer rainy season dislodges older organic carbon from soils which is released during large flow events. DOC consistently remained near modern carbon signatures despite seasonal and flow variations.

## Implications

The distinct relationship between flow rate and carbon age indicates that hydrologic conditions strongly control the mobilization and release of different aged carbon sources from terrestrial systems to streams. The observation that older carbon is released during high-flow events suggests organic matter previously stored in soils becomes labile and is transported during precipitation-driven runoff. This has implications for understanding carbon export from semi-arid watersheds under varying precipitation patterns and potential climate change scenarios.
