# Cavity ring-down spectroscopy performance and procedures for high-throughput δ¹⁸O and δ²H measurement in water using 'Express' mode

## Overall Scientific Topic

Cavity ring-down spectroscopy (CRDS) has emerged as a rapid alternative to isotope ratio mass spectrometry for measuring stable hydrogen (δ²H) and oxygen (δ¹⁸O) isotopic compositions in water. However, long-term accuracy and precision of CRDS remain underreported in the literature, and the instrument's performance during continuous, high-throughput analysis has not been thoroughly characterized. This study provides a comprehensive one-year evaluation of CRDS performance at ETH Zurich using Picarro's "Express" mode for rapid sequential analysis of ~200 samples per week. By developing and implementing a robust calibration and correction strategy, the authors demonstrate that CRDS can achieve long-term precision better than ±0.5‰ for δ¹⁸O and ±1.0‰ for δ²H while addressing inherent analytical challenges including memory effects and matrix effects.

## Methods

The study evaluated CRDS performance on a Picarro high-precision vaporizer (V1102-i) coupled to a CRDS isotope and gas concentration analyzer (L2130-i) using Picarro's "Express" mode. In this mode, 12 injections of 2.1 μL water are made per analysis: the first six injections flush the vaporizer and cavity, and the final six are measurement pulses—of which the first two are discarded due to anomalous peak shape, leaving four pulses for data reduction. Water samples were introduced using an autosampler syringe with a fixed needle. Calibration used three IAEA reference materials (SLAP-2, GRESP, VSMOW-2) analyzed with three-point ordinary least squares regression. Quality control employed three USGS reference materials (W-67444, W-67400, USGS-50) analyzed as unknown samples throughout the one-year period. A systematic analytical session protocol included in-house standards analyzed at session beginning and end, IAEA reference materials, and unknown samples. Post-processing applied three sequential corrections: (1) ChemCorrect software reduction with three-point calibration, (2) drift correction using in-house standards regressed against time, and (3) IAEA standard regression offset correction applied to unknowns.

## Results

Long-term precision evaluated over one year using three USGS reference materials treated as unknowns achieved: δ²H = −399.32 ± 0.96‰ (n = 30) and δ¹⁸O = −51.07 ± 0.45‰ (n = 30) for W-67444; δ²H = 2.55 ± 0.49‰ (n = 140) and δ¹⁸O = −1.85 ± 0.13‰ (n = 140) for W-67400; and δ²H = 33.68 ± 0.91‰ (n = 21) and δ¹⁸O = 5.03 ± 0.04‰ (n = 21) for USGS-50, demonstrating precisions of ±0.5–1.0‰ (±1σ). IAEA standard regressions exhibited slopes of 0.9856–0.9999 and showed no statistically significant instrument drift over the year. Memory effects were successfully mitigated through use of "pre" vials of standards approximating upcoming sample composition and careful monitoring of flushing procedures. Matrix effects were evaluated and addressed through systematic bracketing of unknown samples with reference materials.

## Implications

The demonstrated long-term CRDS performance establishes that cavity ring-down spectroscopy using "Express" mode is a reliable tool for high-throughput water stable isotope analysis when implemented with careful methodology and continuous quality control. This framework enables widespread application of CRDS in hydrology (tracing water provenance and mixing), paleoclimatology (understanding past hydrological cycles), and biogeochemistry (tracking biological water uptake and cycling). The study provides practical guidance for CRDS users, documenting the system's capabilities and limitations based on empirical long-term operational experience. Results confirm that independent (manufacturer-independent) long-term precision and accuracy evaluation is essential for establishing confidence in optical isotope measurement methods and that continuous methodological refinement is required to maintain high-quality analytical results.
