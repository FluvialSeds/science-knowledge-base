# Isotopic composition of carbon dioxide lost by evasion from surface water to the atmosphere: Methodological comparison of a direct and indirect approach

## Overall Scientific Topic

Two approaches exist for determining the isotopic composition of CO₂ lost by evasion (degassing) from water surfaces: direct measurement using molecular sieve trapping systems attached to floating chambers, or indirect calculation from dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC). This study presents a comparison of these two methods using samples from low-productivity UK peatland streams supersaturated in CO₂ to evaluate their relative merits and limitations.

## Methods

On six occasions, samples of evaded CO₂ were collected above the water surface using a floating chamber and molecular sieve for determination of δ¹³C and ¹⁴C. Simultaneously, samples of streamwater were collected for determination of the isotopic composition of DIC through gas stripping and mass spectrometry. Supporting geochemical analyses including pH, temperature, and major ion concentrations were conducted to aid interpretation of the isotopic data.

## Results

Whilst on some occasions the radiocarbon ages of evaded CO₂ and DIC were similar, on others the measured ¹⁴C-DIC age was significantly older than the directly measured CO₂ age. Differences were also observed between directly measured and calculated δ¹³C-CO₂ values. These discrepancies highlight the importance of making appropriate isotopic corrections for evasion CO₂ and demonstrate that some inherent assumptions in the indirect approach may not hold in certain aquatic systems.

## Implications

Care must be taken in applying the indirect approach to estimate evasion CO₂ isotopic composition, particularly in systems with variable pH, high CO₂ saturation, or where equilibrium conditions may not hold. The direct method using molecular sieves provides more reliable measurements of evaded CO₂ isotopic composition, especially in low-productivity systems where degassing may cause significant pH changes that affect DIC partitioning calculations.
