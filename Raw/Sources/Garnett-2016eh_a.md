# A new field approach for the collection of samples for aquatic ¹⁴CO₂ analysis using headspace equilibration and molecular sieve traps: the super headspace method

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study presents the "super headspace method," a novel field technique for rapid radiocarbon collection of aquatic CO2 from low-pH waters. The method combines scaled-up headspace equilibration with molecular sieve traps, enabling high-speed, portable sample collection suitable for remote field sites. Results verify the method's reliability for collecting radiocarbon samples of aquatic CO2 with high portability and no special preservation requirements.

## Methods

A collapsible water carrier (5-10 liter Accordion Water Carrier) was modified as an equilibration chamber by replacing the screw-cap lid with a two-holed rubber bung containing stainless steel pipes and quick-connect couplings for sampling ports. Before use, the chamber is collapsed to remove atmospheric air and flushed with CO2-free air. Headspace gases are injected via syringe into molecular sieve CO2 traps for convenient and stable storage. Laboratory and field tests verified the method using infrared gas analysis to confirm gas-tight conditions and adequate equilibration. Samples were collected from various inland water types at peatland sites in remote Arctic locations.

## Results

Laboratory and field tests verified that the super headspace method provides reliable rapid collection of aquatic CO2 samples for ¹⁴C analysis. The method demonstrates high portability, speed of use, and absence of special sample preservation requirements. Collection is rapid compared to existing techniques, and field results from Arctic peatlands show the method is effective across a range of surface water types. The technique enables collection of samples from a specific moment in time rather than time-integrated samples, allowing capture of individual parts of hydrographic events such as storm responses.

## Implications

This new sampling technique advances radiocarbon dating capabilities for aquatic CO2 by providing a fast, portable, field-friendly method suitable for remote locations with minimal equipment. The ability to capture instantaneous CO2 samples enables better comparison with other simultaneous ¹⁴C measurements (dissolved organic carbon, particulate organic carbon), improving source attribution and carbon cycle understanding in dynamic aquatic systems, particularly in Arctic regions where 'old' carbon mobilization is critical.
