# Vitamin B12-dependent biosynthesis ties amplified 2-methylhopanoid production during oceanic anoxic events to nitrification

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study investigates the biosynthesis of 2-methylhopanoids in Nitrobacter species and demonstrates that this reaction mechanism depends on both a radical S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) enzyme and the enzymatic cofactor cobalamin (vitamin B12). The research establishes a metabolic link between vitamin B12-dependent hopanoid methylation and nitrification, revealing how 2-methylhopanoid abundance can serve as a biomarker for oceanic anoxic events and enhanced nitrogen cycling. The findings suggest that synergistic interactions between Nitrobacter species and cobalamin-producing nitrifying archaea control 2-methylhopanoid production in marine environments.

## Implications

The discovery that 2-methylhopanoid production depends on vitamin B12-dependent enzymatic mechanisms links this biomarker directly to microbial processes during oceanic anoxic events. Since Nitrobacter species require exogenous cobalamin, the production of 2-methylhopanoids signals enhanced nitrification activity coupled with favorable conditions for syntrophic interactions between ammonia- and nitrite-oxidizing nitrifiers. These findings provide a mechanistic basis for interpreting 2-methylhopanoid distributions in paleoceanographic records as indicators of nutrient-driven perturbations to the marine nitrogen cycle during past anoxic episodes. The research demonstrates how understanding the biochemistry of biomarker synthesis improves paleoclimate and paleontologic interpretations.
