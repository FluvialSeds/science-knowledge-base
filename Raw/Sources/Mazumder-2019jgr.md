# Carbon Uptake in Surface Water Food Webs Fed by Palaeogroundwater

## Overall Scientific Topic

A longstanding paradigm in aquatic ecology is that modern autochthonous carbon dominates energy flows in freshwater food webs. However, this study challenges this assumption by demonstrating that in groundwater-dependent ecosystems, old carbon from palaeogroundwater can be the dominant carbon source even at the highest trophic levels. This research provides the first evidence of old carbon incorporation into aquatic food webs in semiarid regions, with significant implications for ecosystem function and radiocarbon dating methods.

## Methods

Stable isotopes (δ¹³C and δ¹⁵N) and radiocarbon (¹⁴C) measurements were used to quantify food chain links and connections between groundwater and surface water food webs in the Pilliga stream, a waterbody fed by groundwater from the Great Artesian Basin (GAB), Australia. Biota samples collected included fish (Gambusia holbrooki), freshwater shrimp (Paratya australiensis), aquatic insects (Corixidae larvae), filamentous algae, particulate organic matter (POM), and sediment organic matter (SOM). Water samples were analyzed for δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C signatures of DOC and DIC.

## Results

Very old carbon was found to be the dominant carbon source throughout the food chain, with fish showing apparent radiocarbon ages of up to 11,000 years. Stable isotope analysis identified trophic links between fish, aquatic insects, and algae, with smaller contributions from POM. The fractional contribution of old groundwater carbon was approximately 99.58% in the water column, 82% in algae, and 52% in fish. These results represent the oldest radiocarbon ages ever recorded for living vertebrates and demonstrate the bioavailability of very old dissolved organic carbon previously assumed to be recalcitrant.

## Implications

This study has important implications for understanding ecosystem function in arid and semiarid regions where groundwater is the primary water source. It also highlights the need for caution in radiocarbon dating of artifacts (particularly fish bones) from archaeological sites associated with groundwater-dependent ecosystems, as biological samples may return ages several thousand years older than the actual age of human occupation.
