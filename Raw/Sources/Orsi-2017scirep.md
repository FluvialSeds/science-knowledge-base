# Climate oscillations reflected within the microbiome of Arabian Sea sediments

## Overall Scientific Topic

Microbial communities in marine sediments are typically shaped by the sequential depletion of energy-yielding electron acceptors (oxygen, nitrate, sulfate, CO₂) during diagenesis. However, some taxa show depth-independent distributions suggesting they preserve a paleoenvironmental signal reflecting conditions at the time of deposition. This metagenomic study of sediments from the Arabian Sea oxygen minimum zone (OMZ) examines whether microbial communities preserve paleoceanographic signals across the last glacial-interglacial cycle. Results demonstrate that 5–15% of taxa per sample exhibit paleoenvironmental selection that is decoupled from sediment depth, with specific taxa clustering based on depositional redox conditions (OMZ vs. bioturbated intervals) rather than post-burial diagenetic environment. The genomic potential for denitrification and fermentation correlates with independent paleoclimate proxies, indicating that paleoenvironmental conditions exert measurable control on microbial community composition even after burial.

## Methods

Sediments underlying the Arabian Sea OMZ were recovered at centennial-scale resolution across a stratigraphic interval spanning climate variations. Metagenomic analysis of sedimentary DNA was performed at high depth resolution (multiple depths within single cores) to examine how microbial taxa distribute vertically in relation to diagenetic processes and paleoclimate history.

Taxonomic composition was determined from metagenomic sequencing and classified using standard phylogenetic databases. Metabolic pathways were inferred from the genetic potential encoded in the metagenomic data, including pathways for denitrification, sulfate reduction, methanogenesis, fermentation (acid and Entner-Dourdoroff pathways), and aerobic respiration.

Paleoclimate proxies were developed from independent geochemical and sedimentological data to characterize depositional conditions. OMZ indicators (such as authigenic minerals or biomarker assemblages) were used to identify intervals deposited under oxygen-depleted conditions versus intervening bioturbated intervals deposited under higher oxygen concentrations.

Statistical analyses examined whether microbial community composition at a given depth correlated more strongly with the modern diagenetic environment (post-burial electron acceptor availability) or with the paleoclimate conditions present at the time of deposition.

## Results

Vertical distributions of typical anaerobic bacteria (sulfate-reducing bacteria, methanogens) showed expected patterns of energy-based selection, with relative abundances varying with depth as different electron acceptors became sequentially depleted during diagenesis.

However, 5–15% of taxa per sample exhibited anomalous depth-independent distributions, indicating paleoenvironmental selection independent of present-day diagenetic conditions. Strikingly, taxa deposited under OMZ conditions clustered together across multiple stratigraphic intervals despite being vertically separated in the core. These taxa were compositionally distinct from those deposited in intervening bioturbated intervals with higher oxygen levels at the time of deposition.

The genomic potential for denitrification (nirS, nirK, nosZ genes) showed significant correlation with independent paleoclimate proxies indicating OMZ conditions, independent of sediment depth and the actual amount of available nitrate or nitrite in modern pore waters. This indicates that the denitrification genetic capacity reflects the selective enrichment of denitrifiers during depositional periods with OMZ conditions.

Metagenomic analysis revealed that many denitrifier taxa also encoded mixed acid and Entner-Dourdoroff fermentation pathways, indicating a capacity for facultative anaerobic metabolism. This fermentation potential may explain how these taxa subsist in buried sediments where their original respiratory electron acceptor (nitrate produced in OMZs) is no longer available.

## Implications

This study provides evidence that sedimentary microbial communities, while shaped primarily by energy availability and post-burial diagenesis, preserve a paleoenvironmental signal that reflects depositional conditions. The paleoenvironmental selection of specific microbial taxa occurs on relatively short geological timescales (centuries to millennia), indicating rapid selective pressures during deposition that create compositional signatures robust enough to persist after burial.

The correlation between genomic denitrification potential and independent paleoclimate proxies suggests that paleoceanographic conditions (specifically OMZ development) directly select for specific bacterial taxa adapted to those conditions. The discovery of mixed fermentation pathways in these taxa provides a mechanism by which they can survive extended burial in anoxic sediments after the removal of nitrate and nitrite.

These findings establish sediment microbiomes as a novel archive of paleoclimate information. In the Arabian Sea and other OMZ-influenced systems, microbial community composition may serve as an additional paleoceanographic proxy complementing conventional geochemical and paleontological indicators.

The work highlights the sensitivity of microbial communities to paleoceanographic conditions and suggests that careful analysis of sedimentary microbial genetic content can extract paleoclimate signals from "fossilized" microbiomes preserved in ancient sediments.
