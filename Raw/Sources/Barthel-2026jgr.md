# Constraining greenhouse gas cycling and emissions in Africa's largest humic lake

## Overall Scientific Topic

Humic tropical lakes and wetlands are globally important sources of atmospheric greenhouse gases (CO‚āā, CH‚āĄ, N‚āāO), yet mechanistic understanding of greenhouse gas cycling in such systems remains limited‚ÄĒparticularly in understudied central Africa. This study measured high-, falling-, and low-water seasonal concentrations and isotopic compositions of major dissolved greenhouse gases in Lake Mai Ndombe, Africa's largest humic lake (Democratic Republic of Congo). Results show that the water column is weakly stratified and highly supersaturated with respect to atmospheric equilibrium for all greenhouse gases across all seasons and depths. Carbon-isotope signals indicate that CO‚āā is largely sourced from respiration of bioavailable organic carbon, whereas CH‚āĄ reflects sedimentary methanogenesis followed by aerobic methanotrophy in the water column. Nitrogen-isotope measurements reveal a nitrogen cycle dominated by sedimentary denitrification with near-quantitative reduction to N‚āā prior to outgassing. ## Methods

Water samples were collected from Lake Mai Ndombe during high-water, falling-water, and low-water seasons across multiple sampling stations and depths. Dissolved CO‚āā, CH‚āĄ, and N‚āāO concentrations were measured using standard headspace equilibration and gas chromatography techniques. Field measurements of temperature and wind speed were recorded to enable flux calculations using standard gas exchange equations. Greenhouse gas fluxes were estimated by extrapolating measured water column concentrations using Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation.

## Results

All three greenhouse gases (CO‚āā, CH‚āĄ, N‚āāO) showed supersaturation relative to atmospheric equilibrium across all seasons, sampling stations, and water depths. Greenhouse gas concentrations increased steadily with water depth, reflecting both physical mixing-driven atmospheric gas exchange in the upper water column and in situ biological production at depth. Carbon-isotope analysis revealed that CO‚āā is primarily sourced from respiration of labile organic carbon, while CH‚āĄ reflects sedimentary methanogenesis. Aerobic methanotrophy in the water column reoxidizes up to 90% of pelagic methane before outgassing. Nitrogen-isotope signatures show that N‚āāO is primarily formed through sedimentary denitrification, with approximately 97% subsequently reduced to N‚āā before upward diffusion and outgassing. Integrated flux calculations yielded annual emissions of 375 ¬Ī 32 Gg C yr‚ĀĽ¬Ļ as CO‚āā, 623 ¬Ī 136 Mg C yr‚ĀĽ¬Ļ as CH‚āĄ, and 223 ¬Ī 20 Mg N yr‚ĀĽ¬Ļ as N‚āāO.

## Implications

Lake Mai Ndombe exemplifies how carbon and nitrogen inputs, total water depth, and dissolved oxygen saturation serve as major drivers of greenhouse gas flux and composition from tropical humic lakes. The high consumption rates of methane and nitrous oxide through microbial processes, despite substantial outgassing, indicates that these emissions are sensitive to small perturbations in lake depth, dissolved oxygen content, and microbial community composition. The results provide critical mechanistic insight into greenhouse gas cycling in tropical African lakes and highlight how such systems will likely respond to anthropogenic climate change‚ÄĒwith implications for global greenhouse gas budgets and climate feedbacks.
