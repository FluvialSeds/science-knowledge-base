# The Ephemeral Signature of Permafrost Carbon in an Arctic Fluvial Network

## Overall Scientific Topic

Arctic fluvial networks process, outgas, and transport significant quantities of terrestrial organic carbon, particularly dissolved organic carbon (DOC), but the proportion of permafrost carbon in these fluxes remains poorly constrained. This study investigated the production of bacterial respiratory carbon dioxide (CO₂) during maximum late-summer thaw in sites spanning the Kolyma River fluvial network (Siberia) to assess whether permafrost carbon degradation could be detected through aged (¹⁴C-depleted) signatures in the dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) pool. Using Keeling plot interpretation of DIC produced in bioincubations, the authors demonstrated that bacteria respire varying sources of DOC moving downstream through the fluvial network. Permafrost carbon respiration (production of aged CO₂) was only detected in heavily permafrost thaw-influenced sites, while in non-permafrost-impacted sites, modern DIC signatures predominated. This research demonstrates the difficulty of tracing inputs of highly reactive substrates to systems with diverse organic matter sources.

## Methods

Water samples were collected from 17 sites spanning the Kolyma River Basin in August-September 2015, with sites classified by watershed position and stream width as permafrost thaw streams, erosion streams, headwater streams, tributaries, and the main stem. For dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) analysis, water samples were collected using sterile syringes and injected into preevacuated borosilicate glass serum vials immediately upon collection (t₀) and following 28-day incubations at room temperature (t₂₈). Samples were acidified to pH ~2 with phosphoric acid. Stable isotope analysis of DIC (δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C) was undertaken using isotope ratio mass spectrometry, with radiocarbon analyses conducted at the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution. The isotopic composition of respiratory CO₂ produced during incubations was estimated using Keeling plot methods, which exploit the linear relationship between DIC isotopic signature and the inverse of DIC concentration. Dissolved organic carbon concentration was determined via high-temperature catalytic oxidation, and dissolved organic matter (DOM) composition was characterized using Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR MS), which identifies thousands of molecular formulae and classifies compounds by aromaticity and elemental composition.

## Results

Dissolved inorganic carbon concentrations ranged from 153 to 3,903 μM and were highest in permafrost-dominated streams, decreasing downstream toward the main stem. The change in DIC over 28-day bioincubations (ΔDIC produced) ranged from 22.7 to 632 μM and reflected a similar downstream trend. Ambient δ¹³C-DIC values ranged from -15.9 to -5.8‰ and were most depleted in low-order streams, becoming enriched in larger tributaries and the Kolyma River main stem. Respiratory δ¹³C-DIC values derived from Keeling plots ranged from -28.8 to -16.3‰ and were universally depleted relative to ambient values. Ambient Δ¹⁴C-DIC values were near modern except in the Yedoma thaw stream (permafrost-dominated) and erosion streams, which exhibited aged signatures (Δ¹⁴C = -713 to -329‰). Respiratory Δ¹⁴C-DIC was predominantly near modern throughout the fluvial network except in permafrost-dominated sites. Dissolved organic carbon concentrations ranged from 327 to 12,492 μM and followed a similar decreasing trend as DIC through the fluvial network. FT-ICR MS revealed that aliphatic and nitrogen-containing compounds—unique to permafrost-derived DOM—were significantly correlated with aged respiratory DIC, indicating these highly biolabile compounds are preferentially respired and derive from permafrost sources.

## Implications

The findings demonstrate that substrate availability determines the source of carbon respired by microbes in Arctic streams and rivers, with a downstream continuum from permafrost-derived organic carbon to modern terrestrial carbon to aquatic biomass. The rapid respiration of permafrost-derived organic matter and masking of aged DIC by modern DIC production and physical processes (CO₂ off-gassing) confound efforts to quantify permafrost carbon mobilization to fluvial networks. The high biolability of aged organic carbon and the lability-age continuum observed in the Kolyma may apply to other systems receiving aged components mixed with modern organic matter. The integration of DOM and DIC age dynamics into existing biogeochemical frameworks will help researchers better partition modern versus aged carbon cycling in Arctic and other fluvial systems, with implications for understanding how permafrost thaw and mobilization responds to climate change.
