# An inverse method to relate organic carbon reactivity to isotope composition from serial oxidation

## Overall Scientific Topic

Serial oxidation methods such as ramped-temperature pyrolysis-oxidation (RPO) enable measurement of organic carbon reactivity by characterizing sequential CO₂ release across temperature intervals, simultaneously coupled with stable carbon (δ¹³C) and radiocarbon (¹⁴C) isotope analysis. However, the interpretation of thermal decay profiles depends on experimental conditions (temperature ramp rate, total mass analyzed) and oxidation kinetics are not uniquely determined by observed decay curves. This technical study presents a regularized inverse method to estimate the distribution of organic carbon activation energy (E)—a proxy for bond strength—using serial oxidation data. By validating that activation energy distributions are independent of experimental parameters, the method provides a novel proxy for organic carbon thermal reactivity that can be robustly correlated with isotope composition to infer organic matter source and environmental residence time.

## Methods

A decarbonated sediment test sample was analyzed by RPO at multiple sample masses (5–30 mg) and different oven temperature ramp rates (0.5–1.0°C/min). CO₂ evolved over sequential temperature intervals was collected and analyzed for δ¹³C (via isotope ratio mass spectrometry) and Δ¹⁴C (via accelerator mass spectrometry).

An inverse kinetic model was developed based on the principle that organic carbon decay during heating follows a superposition of parallel first-order kinetic reactions, each characterized by a specific activation energy E. The model infers the probability distribution of activation energies from the observed CO₂ release profile across temperature intervals using regularization techniques to avoid overfitting. Activation energy was calculated from Arrhenius-type kinetics:

$$E = RT \ln\left(\frac{A}{r}\right)$$

where r is the reaction rate determined from the observed CO₂ release and A is the pre-exponential factor.

The stability of E distributions was tested by varying sample mass and ramp rate independently and comparing the resulting E distributions for overlapping temperature regions.

## Results

The activation energy distributions inferred from RPO data were independent of sample mass (5–30 mg) and ramp rate (0.5–1.0°C/min), demonstrating that E distributions represent an intrinsic property of organic matter structure rather than experimental artifact. The E values for organic carbon span a broad range from ~30 kJ/mol (highly labile, easily oxidized components) to >150 kJ/mol (highly condensed, refractory components). 

The distribution of activation energies correlates with measured isotope values in predictable ways: low-E carbon (easily oxidized, likely young and labile) tends to have higher Δ¹⁴C values (younger radiocarbon ages), while high-E carbon (refractory, resistant to oxidation) often shows lower Δ¹⁴C values (older radiocarbon ages). The relationship between E and δ¹³C is more complex and depends on organic matter source and diagenetic pathway.

## Implications

The activation energy distribution provides a quantitative descriptor of organic carbon thermal reactivity that is independent of measurement conditions, enabling robust comparison across samples and studies. By coupling E distributions to isotope measurements, the E vs. δ¹³C and E vs. Δ¹⁴C relationships reveal compositional controls on organic matter source and residence time.

This method is broadly applicable to any serial oxidation technique, not just RPO. The approach enables direct mechanistic interpretation of how organic matter chemical composition (bond strength, functional group reactivity) determines both thermal decomposition kinetics and isotopic signatures. For paleoclimate and paleoenvironmental reconstruction, the E vs. isotope relationships provide quantitative constraints on the degree of diagenetic alteration, mineral protection, and thermal history experienced by ancient organic matter in sedimentary archives.

The regularized inverse method represents a significant methodological advance for extracting kinetic information from thermal decomposition data, with applications extending beyond geochemistry to materials science and combustion analysis.
