# Riverine carbon export in the arid to semiarid Wuding River catchment on the Chinese Loess Plateau

## Overall Scientific Topic

Riverine export of terrestrially-derived carbon represents a key component of the global carbon cycle, yet quantification of carbon dynamics in arid and semiarid river systems remains limited. This study examines the quantity, age, and fate of both dissolved and particulate carbon exported from the Wuding River on the Chinese Loess Plateau, evaluating how dam construction and land management affect carbon cycling and export.

## Methods

Water samples were collected along the Wuding River catchment to measure dissolved organic carbon (DOC), dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), and particulate organic carbon (POC) concentrations. Radiocarbon (Δ¹⁴C) measurements of CO₂ degassed from the water surface were used to determine the age of respired carbon. Bulk carbon concentrations, seasonal discharge patterns, and carbon burial in sediments behind check dams were quantified. Major ions and lithologic data were analyzed to determine the sources of DIC.

## Results

Export of DOC and DIC exhibited pronounced spatial and temporal variability along the river course. While DOC showed relatively stable concentrations independent of discharge, DIC concentrations were significantly higher in the loess-derived subcatchment due to carbonate dissolution. Radiocarbon analysis revealed that degassed CO₂ was 810–1890 years old, indicating substantial mobilization and release of ancient carbon previously stored in soil horizons. Carbon burial through sediment storage in reservoirs behind check dams accounted for 42% of total riverine carbon export on an annual basis.

## Implications

Check dams and other human modifications fundamentally alter carbon dynamics in arid river systems by increasing carbon burial while releasing ancient soil carbon. Riverine carbon export in such systems accounts for a significant fraction of the net ecosystem production, highlighting the importance of arid and semiarid rivers in both carbon cycling and climate interactions at regional scales.
