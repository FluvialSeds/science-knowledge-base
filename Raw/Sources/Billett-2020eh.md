# An assessment of chamber ¹⁴C methodologies for sampling aquatic CO₂ evasion

## Overall Scientific Topic

The development of methods to directly measure the radiocarbon age of dissolved and evaded aquatic carbon dioxide has enhanced understanding of carbon transport and cycling in the soil–water–atmosphere system. This study evaluates whether different floating chamber methodological approaches influence the isotopic (stable carbon and radiocarbon) composition of measured CO₂ samples from peatland streams.

## Methods

Four different floating chamber approaches were evaluated and compared for their effects on the stable and radiocarbon composition of evaded carbon dioxide. Chamber conditions, CO₂ concentrations, sampling times, and recovery procedures were varied according to different methodological approaches. Samples were analyzed for ¹⁴C age and δ¹³C composition to assess reproducibility and potential artifacts introduced by the different chamber configurations.

## Results

Chamber conditions varied considerably with the different methodologies, with maximum chamber CO₂ concentrations ranging from approximately 400–6,300 ppm during sampling. Despite these varying chamber conditions, no significant differences were observed in the ¹⁴C age of evasion (range: 1,276–1,364 years before present) with any of the methodological approaches in chambers where atmospheric CO₂ had been excluded. This result confirms that the floating chamber methodologies are both robust and widely applicable.

## Implications

The findings support continued use of floating chamber methods for direct measurement of evaded CO₂ isotopic composition. While chambers do produce different physical conditions (as indicated by varying CO₂ concentrations), these differences do not significantly alter the isotopic signature of the collected samples. This methodological consistency is important for comparing results across different studies and environments, and enables confident interpretation of evaded CO₂ as reflecting the true isotopic composition of carbon lost to the atmosphere.
