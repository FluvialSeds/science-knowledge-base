# Multiple plant-wax compounds record differential sources and ecosystem structure in large river catchments


## Overall Scientific Topic

Plant-wax lipids (organic compounds produced by plants) are used as biomarkers to reconstruct terrestrial ecosystem structure and vegetation composition from river sediments and paleoclimate archives. Different compound classes—n-alkanes, n-alcohols, and n-alkanoic acids—have distinct molecular distributions and stable carbon isotope compositions (δ¹³C) that reflect different vegetation types (C₃ vs C₄ plants) and ecosystem characteristics. Understanding how these biomarkers vary seasonally and inter-annually in modern river systems is crucial for properly interpreting sedimentary records of past climate and ecosystem changes. This study examines temporal variability in plant-wax biomarkers from the Congo River to determine which compound classes best represent local vs. catchment-integrated ecosystem signals.

## Methods

A 34-month time series of suspended sediments was collected from the Congo River outflow. Three plant-wax lipid compound classes (n-alkanes, n-alcohols, and n-alkanoic acids) were analyzed for concentration and δ¹³C composition using gas chromatography coupled to isotope ratio mass spectrometry (GC-IRMS). Individual compound homologues (varying chain lengths from C₂₅-C₃₅ for n-alkanes and C₂₆-C₃₆ for n-alcohols and n-alkanoic acids) were measured to determine molecular distributions. River discharge data were used to assess correlations between sediment composition and seasonal runoff patterns.

## Results

The three plant-wax lipid classes showed fundamentally different patterns of variability and response to discharge. n-Alkanes (C₂₅-C₃₅) exhibited a mixture of C₃ and C₄ vegetation sources with high δ¹³C variability across chain lengths (8.1 ± 0.7‰) but weak correlations between individual homologues. In contrast, n-alcohols (C₂₆-C₃₆) and n-alkanoic acids (C₂₆-C₃₆) showed strong positive correlations between homologues (r = 0.70–0.99) and more depleted, less variable δ¹³C values (2.6 ± 0.6‰ and 2.0 ± 1.1‰, respectively). Importantly, all individual plant-wax lipids showed minimal temporal δ¹³C variability (<0.9‰) across the 34-month period, indicating that stable carbon isotopes are not sensitive tracers for seasonal-to-inter-annual changes in plant-wax source in the Congo basin. However, carbon-normalized concentrations and relative abundances of n-alcohols and n-alkanoic acids responded rapidly to seasonal discharge changes (indicating recent local sources), while n-alkanes showed no correlation with discharge and maintained low, stable abundances (5–16%), indicating they represent a catchment-integrated signal.

## Implications

This study demonstrates that analyzing multiple plant-wax lipid classes and chain lengths provides complementary information for distinguishing local vs. distal (catchment-integrated) ecosystem signals in large river systems. n-Alkanes, with their stable temporal behavior and weak discharge response, appear better suited for reconstructing long-term catchment-scale vegetation changes and paleoclimate signals in sedimentary archives. n-Alcohols and n-alkanoic acids are more sensitive to short-term hydrological changes and local ecosystem inputs. These findings have important implications for paleoclimate and paleovegetation reconstructions using plant-wax biomarkers: researchers should carefully consider which compound classes are most appropriate for their specific research questions, as different lipid classes integrate ecosystem information over different spatial and temporal scales. This work provides a framework for more robust interpretation of plant-wax biomarker records in both modern and ancient sediments.
