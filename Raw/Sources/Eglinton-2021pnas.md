# Climate control on terrestrial biospheric carbon turnover

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study investigates how climate controls the ages and turnover rates of organic carbon exported from terrestrial river basins. Terrestrial vegetation and soils store three times more carbon than the atmosphere, yet large uncertainties persist regarding how climate change will perturb these carbon reservoirs. By measuring radiocarbon ages of plant-derived biomarker tracers (leaf-wax fatty acids and lignin phenols) from 36 globally distributed rivers, the researchers establish that climate parameters—particularly mean annual temperature and precipitation—exert primary controls on biospheric carbon turnover times within watersheds. Linking riverine biomarker ages to basin-scale soil carbon cycling reveals how climate influences both the remobilization and long-term stability of terrestrial carbon pools across diverse ecosystems.

## Methods

The study compiled previously published ¹⁴C age measurements (n = 95) and generated additional measurements (n = 28) on 36 fluvial systems representing diverse watersheds and collectively accounting for ~42%, 29%, and 20% of global riverine water, sediment, and POC discharge. Water samples were collected from multiple sites spanning a range in glacial and permafrost coverage and filtered through precombusted glass fiber filters. Two classes of plant-derived biomarkers were analyzed: n-alkanoic acids (fatty acids) and lignin-derived phenols from sediment samples. Radiocarbon analysis was performed on isolated compounds using accelerator mass spectrometry. Multivariate linear regression (OLS MLR) was used to identify key climatic (temperature, precipitation), geomorphic (slope, erosion rate), and anthropogenic parameters governing biomarker ¹⁴C ages, with spatial values weighted by an e-folding distance factor decaying exponentially with flow distance. The dataset was screened to remove anthropogenic contamination and to examine only terrestrial vascular plant biomarkers.

## Results

Biomarker ¹⁴C ages ranged widely across the global sample set, from above modern (reflecting bomb ¹⁴C incorporation) to more than 10,000 ¹⁴C years for fatty acids and up to 5,000 ¹⁴C years for lignin. Strong negative relationships existed between both fatty acid and lignin phenol ¹⁴C ages and mean annual temperature and precipitation. Riverine biospheric-carbon ages scaled proportionally with basin-wide soil carbon turnover times and soil ¹⁴C ages, indicating that organic carbon cycling within soils is a primary control on exported biomarker ages. The findings reveal a broad distribution of soil OC reactivities. Rivers in colder, drier regions exported older biomarkers, while rivers in warmer, wetter regions exported younger biomarkers.

## Implications

The discovery that riverine biomarker ages scale with soil OC turnover times establishes that riverine biomarkers can constrain the sensitivity of carbon dynamics to environmental controls on broad spatial scales. This relationship enables extraction of paleo-climate information from fluvially-dominated sedimentary sequences to infer past variations in soil OC turnover in response to climate and anthropogenic perturbations. Future monitoring of riverine organic carbon composition may help detect climate-change-induced perturbations of soil OC turnover and stocks. These findings suggest that the ubiquitous occurrence of long-lived soil OC pools renders soil carbon globally vulnerable to perturbations from future temperature and precipitation increases, with direct implications for understanding the response of terrestrial carbon reservoirs to ongoing climate change.
