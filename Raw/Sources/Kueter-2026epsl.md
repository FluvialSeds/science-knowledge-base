# Precise kinetic parameters for thermal resetting of clumped isotope signatures in biogenic and abiogenic calcites

## Overall Scientific Topic

Clumped isotope paleothermometry—the measurement of bonds between heavy stable isotopes (¹³C-¹⁸O pairs) in carbonate minerals—provides powerful tools for reconstructing ancient temperatures. However, the precision and accuracy of clumped isotope paleothermometry depend critically on understanding how thermal resetting during burial alters the original isotopic signature. This study quantifies the kinetics of thermal resetting in both biogenic (belemnite) and abiogenic (optical) calcites through carefully designed laboratory heating experiments. Results reveal material-specific kinetic parameters that control the rate of clumped isotope reordering at geological temperatures, with important implications for interpreting clumped isotope paleothermometry in naturally buried samples. The study demonstrates that accurate reconstruction of kinetic parameters requires precise experimental design, particularly timing of sample collection during high-temperature and low-temperature portions of heating experiments.

## Methods

Annealing experiments were conducted on belemnite rostral calcite and natural optical calcite materials over a range of temperatures (500–1,000°C) and heating durations. Samples were removed at multiple time intervals during heating and analyzed for Δ47 (the excess abundance of ¹³C-¹⁸O bonds relative to stochastic distribution). The degree of isotope reordering was tracked as a function of time and temperature. Kinetic parameters (activation energy E, frequency factor v₀, and the distribution of activation energies σE) were determined by fitting reaction progress data to first-order kinetic models. Numerical experiments assessed how sampling timing affects accuracy and precision of reconstructed kinetic parameters.

## Results

Kinetic parameters differ substantially between biogenic belemnite calcite and abiogenic optical calcite, revealing material-specific controls on thermal resetting rates. Activation energies ranged from ~167–290 kJ mol⁻¹ depending on carbonate material type, with frequency factors ranging over 10+ orders of magnitude. Numerical modeling revealed that accurate kinetic parameter recovery depends critically on capturing both short-duration, high-temperature experiments (before substantial reordering occurs) and long-duration, low-temperature experiments (to define the low-temperature asymptote). The study demonstrates that suboptimal sampling timing can introduce biases in calculated kinetic parameters up to ±35 kJ mol⁻¹ in activation energy.

## Implications

Material-specific kinetic parameters enable more accurate interpretation of clumped isotope records from naturally buried carbonates by accounting for thermal resetting during burial. The kinetic framework allows quantitative prediction of how burial temperature and duration alter clumped isotope signatures, enabling correction of paleothermometric signals in rocks that have experienced complex thermal histories. The results emphasize the importance of careful experimental design in kinetic studies and provide a template for determining accurate thermal history corrections in other paleoceanographic and paleoclimate proxies sensitive to thermal alteration.
