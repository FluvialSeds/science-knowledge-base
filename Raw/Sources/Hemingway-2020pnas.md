# Triple oxygen isotope insight into terrestrial pyrite oxidation

**Journal:** Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, Volume 117, Issue 14, Pages 7650–7657 (April 7, 2020)

## Overall Scientific Topic

The mass-independent minor oxygen isotope compositions (Δ¹⁷O) of atmospheric O₂ and CO₂ are primarily regulated by their relative partial pressures (pO₂/pCO₂). Pyrite oxidation during chemical weathering on land consumes O₂ and generates sulfate that is carried to the ocean by rivers. The Δ¹⁷O values of marine sulfate deposits have been proposed as a proxy to quantitatively track ancient atmospheric conditions. However, a mechanistic understanding of pyrite oxidation—including oxygen sources—in weathering environments remains poorly understood. This study uses triple oxygen isotope measurements from modern rivers in the Himalaya to constrain the oxygen sources during pyrite oxidation and assess the utility of sulfate Δ¹⁷O as a paleoatmospheric proxy.

## Methods

Water and sulfate samples were collected from modern rivers transecting the Annapurna Himalaya, Nepal, spanning a range of elevations and bedrock lithologies. Sulfate source estimates were made using major and minor sulfur isotopes (δ³⁴S, δ¹⁸O) to distinguish between pyrite oxidation, microbial sulfate reduction, and other sulfur cycling pathways. Triple oxygen isotope measurements (Δ¹⁷O) were made on sulfate samples to track oxygen sources incorporated during oxidation reactions.

## Results

Sulfate in high-elevation headwaters is quantitatively sourced by pyrite oxidation based on stable isotope constraints. However, the measured Δ¹⁷O values imply no direct tropospheric O₂ incorporation during pyrite oxidation. Instead, results indicate incorporation of oxygen atoms from alternative, ¹⁷O-enriched sources such as reactive oxygen species (including ozone and hydrogen peroxide). Sulfate Δ¹⁷O values decrease significantly when moving into warm, low-elevation tributaries draining the same bedrock lithology, reflecting overprinting of the pyrite oxidation-derived Δ¹⁷O anomaly by microbial sulfate reduction and reoxidation.

## Implications

The geologic application of sulfate Δ¹⁷O as a proxy for past pO₂/pCO₂ must account for: (1) alternative oxygen sources during pyrite oxidation, particularly reactive oxygen species rather than direct molecular O₂ incorporation, and (2) secondary overprinting by microbial sulfur cycling in warm, low-elevation environments. These findings refine the interpretation of sulfate Δ¹⁷O records in ancient sediments and improve constraints on paleo-atmospheric oxygen levels.
