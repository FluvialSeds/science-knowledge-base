# Multi-molecular ¹⁴C evidence for mineral control on terrestrial carbon storage and export

## Overall Scientific Topic

Most studies of terrestrial organic carbon cycling via compound-specific radiocarbon analysis focus on higher plant biomarkers (plant leaf-wax n-alkanoic acids, n-alkanes, lignin-derived phenols). However, soils constitute the largest continental carbon pool, and soil organic matter (rather than litter) is the dominant source of terrestrial biospheric carbon exported by rivers. This study measures radiocarbon ages of glycerol dialkyl glycerol tetraethers (GDGTs)—microbial membrane lipids produced by bacteria and archaea and abundant in soils—and compares GDGT ¹⁴C signatures to those of higher plant biomarkers and pyrogenic carbon markers across eight global river systems. Results indicate that GDGTs reflect soil carbon turnover and that systematic radiocarbon offsets between biomarker classes reveal different mobilization pathways and stabilization mechanisms, with particular evidence that mineral association controls GDGT preservation.

## Methods

Eight riverine sediment samples were selected from diverse climatic and geomorphic settings: Amazon, Gaoping, Cagayan, Koshi, Pearl, Earn, Dul, and Arctic Red rivers plus a Danube floodplain deposit. Lipid extraction used dispersed-guided extraction (CEM EDGE) with dichloromethane:methanol solvent mixtures. GDGTs were isolated by silica column chromatography followed by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) using hydrophilic interaction chromatography columns to separate isoprenoid GDGTs (isoGDGTs produced by archaea) and branched GDGTs (brGDGTs produced by bacteria). GDGT fractions were tested for purity by re-analysis on HPLC-MS, then measured for radiocarbon content using elemental analyzer-accelerator mass spectrometry (EA-AMS). Compound-specific radiocarbon data for other biomarkers (fatty acids, lignin phenols, n-alkanes, benzene polycarboxylic acids) were compiled from the literature for samples from the same catchments or nearby sites.

Catchment properties (geomorphic variables including floodplain extent, elevation, slope; climatic factors including mean annual temperature, precipitation, seasonal variability; and environmental controls including anthropogenic disturbance, net primary productivity, and topsoil radiocarbon age) were obtained from prior studies. Spearman rank-order correlations were computed to assess relationships between biomarker radiocarbon ages and catchment properties.

## Results

GDGT radiocarbon ages (Δ¹⁴C values ranging from −106‰ to −737‰ for isoGDGTs and −109‰ to −631‰ for brGDGTs) varied systematically with catchment properties. isoGDGTs and brGDGTs within samples showed similar ¹⁴C values, suggesting common preservation mechanisms. Most samples exhibited GDGT ¹⁴C ages older than higher plant biomarkers (lignin and fatty acids), indicating that microbial lipids experience extended soil residence times before fluvial export. Exceptions were the Amazon and Cagayan rivers, where GDGTs were relatively young.

GDGT ¹⁴C values showed statistically significant correlations with soil radiocarbon age and topsoil radiocarbon age (p < 0.05), similar in strength and direction to correlations observed for plant-derived lignin and fatty acids. Both GDGT and higher plant biomarker classes correlated strongly with basin properties including mean annual temperature (negative correlation), mean annual precipitation, net primary productivity, and ecosystem/soil turnover times. Long-chain fatty acids and lignin showed stronger correlations with elevation and slope compared to GDGTs, while GDGTs showed unique patterns with certain catchment variables. Branched and isoprenoid GDGTs showed offsetting patterns relative to pyrogenic carbon markers (benzene polycarboxylic acids), suggesting distinct transport and preservation pathways.

## Implications

GDGTs serve as diagnostic tracers of soil-derived organic carbon because their radiocarbon ages reflect intermittent soil storage before riverine export. Systematic ¹⁴C offsets among biomarker classes reflect different mobilization pathways: GDGTs (microbial lipids) experience longer soil residence than plant waxes, suggesting greater integration into mineral-protected soil pools. This evidence strongly supports mineral association as the dominant mechanism controlling organic carbon preservation in soils.

The correlation of GDGT radiocarbon ages with topsoil (0–30 cm) radiocarbon age indicates that these compounds reflect active soil cycling zones rather than deep, ancient soil carbon. For paleoenvironmental applications, GDGT-based radiocarbon measurements provide quantitative constraints on soil carbon turnover and erosional export, complementing plant biomarker records. The offset between GDGT and plant biomarker ¹⁴C ages can be used to partition how much exported organic carbon derives from soil versus fresh vegetation, improving paleoclimate reconstructions.

The findings underscore that mineral stabilization, not just selective preservation of recalcitrant compounds, drives the long residence times observed for terrestrial organic matter in soil systems and during fluvial export to oceans.
