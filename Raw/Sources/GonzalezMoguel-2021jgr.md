# Radiocarbon data reveal contrasting sources for carbon fractions in thermokarst lakes and rivers of eastern Canada (Nunavik, Quebec)

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study uses radiocarbon measurements to determine carbon sources for dissolved and particulate organic carbon, inorganic carbon, and methane in thermokarst lakes and rivers in the sporadic permafrost region of northern Quebec. Results demonstrate that soil type and seasons strongly influence carbon age and source, with peatland systems containing significant permafrost-derived carbon while mineral soil systems are dominated by contemporary carbon.

## Methods

Five thermokarst lakes (three palsa-associated and two lithalsa-associated) and three rivers were sampled in northern Quebec during summer 2018-2019 and winter 2019. Measurements of ¹⁴C content, δ¹³C, and concentration of ebullition and dissolved CH4, dissolved CO2, DOC, POC, and surficial sediments were conducted. Samples included ice-free season (August) and ice-covered season (February) measurements for palsa lakes, and summer-only sampling for non-peatland rivers with one peatland river sampled in winter. A ¹⁴C end-member mixing model was applied to estimate relative contributions of potential carbon sources to different aquatic carbon pools.

## Results

Modern carbon (fixed after 1950 CE) was the dominant source of DOC, DIC, and dissolved CH4 in non-peatland systems, while POC and sediment carbon were predominantly fixed in the last millennia. In peatland systems, modern and permafrost carbon were important sources of DOC, DIC, and dissolved CH4, but POC and ebullition CH4 were dominated by millennial-old carbon. In winter, ¹⁴C age of DOC, DIC, and POC in peatland lakes increased, but dissolved CH4 age did not change seasonally. Peatland lakes showed clearly older carbon sources for ebullition CH4 relative to dissolved CH4, but this pattern was absent in non-peatland lakes.

## Implications

These results indicate that while permafrost carbon is incorporated into aquatic organic carbon pools in peatland systems, modern carbon from recent productivity is the largest source of CH4 and CO2 emissions. The contrasting seasonal patterns between dissolved and ebullitive gases have implications for accurately constraining the permafrost carbon feedback in the context of climate warming and for understanding how different thermal regimes and vegetation types influence carbon mobilization pathways in Arctic and Subarctic systems.
