# A contemporary microbially maintained subglacial ferrous "ocean"

## Overall Scientific Topic

Subglacial environments beneath Antarctic glaciers contain isolated liquid water bodies with unique biogeochemistry and active microbial communities despite extreme conditions and lack of contemporary photosynthesis. This study examines the biogeochemical cycles and microbial metabolism in the subglacial brine beneath Taylor Glacier, revealing how microorganisms can maintain active life in carbon-starved, anoxic aquatic systems beneath thick ice.

## Methods

Water and sediment samples were collected from a series of subarctic and Arctic lakes in Alaska and Antarctica. Dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and particulate organic carbon (POC) were analyzed for both radiocarbon (Δ¹⁴C) and stable carbon isotope (δ¹³C) composition. Lake sediment cores were analyzed for total organic carbon (TOC) and compound-specific radiocarbon to assess long-term carbon cycling patterns. Physical properties including ice thickness and water column stratification were measured to relate to carbon isotope patterns.

## Results

Δ¹⁴C signatures in Arctic lake water and sediments showed high variability related to lake geometry and ice cover duration. Lakes with longer ice-free seasons showed more modern DOC and DIC, while permanently ice-covered lakes showed more depleted carbon reflecting contributions from ancient sources. Sediment cores showed that organic matter buried in Arctic lake sediments is predominantly modern despite atmospheric mixing being limited by ice cover, indicating efficient processing and transformation of organic matter during burial.

## Implications

Carbon cycling in Arctic and subarctic lakes is fundamentally different from temperate lakes due to the unique physical constraints imposed by prolonged ice cover and short open-water seasons. These findings have important implications for understanding how climate change will affect carbon storage and cycling in polar aquatic ecosystems.
