# Age and source of different forms of carbon released from boreal peatland streams during spring snowmelt in E. Finland

## Overall Scientific Topic

The spring snowmelt period dominates the hydrological year in boreal regions and results in significant annual losses of aquatic carbon. This study uses δ¹³C and ¹⁴C measurements to investigate the source and age of different forms of aquatic carbon (DOC, POC, and evasion CO₂) released from contrasting forested peatland catchments during the spring snowmelt period in boreal eastern Finland.

## Methods

Isotopic analysis was conducted on two contrasting (undrained versus drained) forested peatland catchments during the end of winter snowmelt period. Multiple carbon species were analyzed for their stable and radiocarbon isotope signatures, including dissolved organic carbon, particulate organic carbon, and CO₂ lost by evasion. Samples were collected during pre-flood, flood, and post-flood periods to capture the dynamic changes during snowmelt.

## Results

The δ¹³CVPDB values of DOC (range -28.3 to -28.8‰) were generally more ¹³C-depleted than evasion CO₂ (range -22.7 to -31.5‰) and showed no clear differences between the pre-flood, flood, and post-flood periods. Both DOC and CO₂ contained evidence of bomb-¹⁴C (>100% modern), indicating substantial quantities of carbon fixed since the mid-1950s. However, DOC was ¹⁴C-enriched compared to evasion CO₂, with ¹⁴C concentrations suggesting DOC was approximately 5–6 years younger than CO₂. POC was significantly ¹⁴C-depleted with conventional radiocarbon ages of 805–1135 BP.

## Implications

Contrary to other studies, the isotopic compositions of DOC and evasion CO₂ were very similar, suggesting a predominantly single carbon source (decomposition of soil organic matter) during the snowmelt period. No evidence suggests release of old (pre-bomb) carbon at the end of winter, though the drained site was associated with more ¹⁴C-depleted and ¹³C-enriched evasion CO₂, suggesting closer linkage to atmospheric CO₂. Results demonstrate strong connectivity between carbon cycling in the soil–plant–water system of boreal peatlands.
