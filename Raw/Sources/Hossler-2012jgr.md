# Estimation of riverine carbon and organic matter source contributions using time-based isotope mixing models

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study applies novel time-based isotope mixing models to quantify variable sources of riverine carbon across six northeastern U.S. rivers. Rather than assuming static source signatures, the time-varying mixing model (TVMM) allows both isotopic signatures and source contributions to vary temporally, revealing that seasonal changes in relative source contributions from algal material, soil organic matter, and atmospheric exchange drive observed variation in particulate organic carbon, dissolved organic carbon, and dissolved inorganic carbon.

## Methods

Time-based mixing models were developed: a time-averaged mixing model (TAMM) varying only source isotopic signatures and a time-varying mixing model (TVMM) allowing both isotopic signatures and source contributions to vary with time. Natural abundance δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C isotopes of POC, DOC, and DIC were measured for six rivers (Connecticut, Hudson, Delaware, Susquehanna, Potomac, and Roanoke) draining to the Middle Atlantic Bight, collected on seven different occasions over a year. Six potential POC and DOC sources were considered: C3 plant material, C4 plant material, algal material, slow-turnover soil organic carbon, passive-turnover soil organic carbon, and fossil organic carbon. Four sources were evaluated for DIC: atmospheric exchange, carbonate dissolution, POC remineralization, and DOC remineralization.

## Results

Based on TVMM results, drivers of variation in riverine carbon sources included stream discharge for POC, stream discharge and water temperature for DOC, and water temperature and vegetation phenology for DIC. Major sources included C3 plant material, algal material, and slow-turnover soil organic matter accounting for 50-100% of POC; C3 plant material and slow-turnover soil organic matter accounting for 60-100% of DOC; and atmospheric exchange accounting for 40-60% of DIC. Seasonal change in relative contributions from these sources dominated observed variation in all three carbon pools.

## Implications

The TVMM represents a novel approach for identifying component contributions under realistic non-static conditions, with potential application across diverse biogeochemical studies. By accounting for temporal variation in both source isotopic signatures and contributions, this modeling approach provides more accurate and realistic estimates of carbon source dynamics in riverine systems, enabling better understanding of how climate, hydrology, and ecosystem changes affect terrestrial carbon export to coastal waters.
