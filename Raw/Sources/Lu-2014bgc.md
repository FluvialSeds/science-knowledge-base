# Effects of land use on sources and ages of inorganic and organic carbon in temperate headwater streams

## Overall Scientific Topic

Human land use substantially alters terrestrial landscapes and affects aquatic carbon cycling through multiple pathways. This study examines how different land-use types (forest, pasture, cropland, and urban) affect the sources, concentrations, and ages of all three major aquatic carbon pools—dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and particulate organic carbon (POC)—in temperate headwater streams of the Chesapeake Bay region.

## Methods

Eight first-order headwater streams draining watersheds dominated by different land uses were sampled at baseflow conditions six times over one year. Water samples were analyzed for DIC, DOC, and POC concentrations, and for their stable isotope (δ¹³C) and radiocarbon (Δ¹⁴C) signatures. Excitation-emission matrix fluorescence with parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC) was used to characterize dissolved organic matter composition. Watershed land use was quantified using aerial photo analysis.

## Results

Forested streams exported predominantly young, ¹⁴C-enriched DIC originating from respiration of recent organic matter, while urbanized streams were influenced more by sedimentary carbonate weathering, producing older and more depleted DIC. Aged DOM (Δ¹⁴C = -248 to -202‰) and POM (Δ¹⁴C = -90 to -88‰) were observed exclusively in urbanized streams, with apparent ages of 1,811–2,284 years BP for DOM and 669–887 years BP for POM. These aged carbon pools likely resulted from autotrophic fixation of aged DIC from sedimentary shell dissolution and possibly watershed export of fossil fuel carbon.

## Implications

This study demonstrates that human land use can significantly mobilize geologically aged inorganic carbon and enable its participation in contemporary carbon cycling, with important consequences for understanding aquatic and terrestrial carbon budgets in human-modified landscapes.
