# Controls on short-term dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr variations in large rivers: evidence from the Ganga–Brahmaputra

## Overall Scientific Topic

The Ganga and Brahmaputra rivers deliver disproportionately large fluxes of radiogenic strontium (high ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) to the ocean, substantially influencing the marine strontium isotope record. Dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr compositions in these river systems show extreme temporal variability, with week-to-week changes as large as 0.0134 ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr units—the largest fluctuations observed in any major river globally. This study presents high-resolution (weekly during monsoon, monthly during non-monsoon) dissolved and suspended particulate Sr time-series data from 2010-2011, combined with a hydro-geochemical mixing model to understand controls on short-term strontium isotope variability. Results demonstrate that dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr variations are largely driven by spatio-temporal rainfall variability across the watersheds, with in-stream interactions with radiogenic sediments during transport further contributing to dissolved isotope variability.

## Methods

Dissolved and depth-specific suspended particulate strontium concentrations and isotope ratios (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) were measured from the Ganga and Brahmaputra rivers with weekly resolution during monsoon season and monthly resolution during non-monsoon season (2010-2011). A hydro-geochemical mixing model was developed using literature-derived Sr concentration and ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr values of major tributaries combined with 5-day integrated rainfall data as a proxy for discharge data. Depth-specific suspended particulate sampling enabled assessment of in-stream interactions between dissolved and particulate strontium at different water column depths. The model incorporates the isotopic composition of major tributaries and investigates the relationships between rainfall patterns and observed dissolved strontium isotope variations.

## Results

Dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr compositions of both the Ganga and Brahmaputra showed substantial short-term variability with week-to-week changes reaching 0.0134 ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr units. Strong correlations existed between dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr and suspended particulate ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr at all sampled depths, suggesting active in-stream interactions between the dissolved and particulate Sr pools. The hydro-geochemical mixing model indicates that short-term dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr variability is primarily driven by spatio-temporal rainfall variability across the Ganga and Brahmaputra watersheds. Depth-specific profiles revealed consistent dissolved-particulate Sr isotope relationships throughout the water column in both studied rivers, corroborating observations from other large rivers globally.

## Implications

The high-resolution strontium isotope time-series data demonstrate that short-term riverine Sr isotope variability is sensitive to rainfall distribution patterns and monsoon dynamics in the Himalayan watersheds. The strong correlation between dissolved and particulate strontium isotope compositions throughout the water column indicates that in-stream weathering and sediment-water interactions are important secondary controls on riverine Sr isotope composition beyond primary source weathering. These findings have implications for interpreting marine strontium isotope records, particularly during periods of monsoon variability or climate change affecting precipitation patterns. The hydrogeochemical mixing model approach provides a framework for predicting how changes in watershed rainfall and hydrology will affect the composition of strontium exported to the ocean.
