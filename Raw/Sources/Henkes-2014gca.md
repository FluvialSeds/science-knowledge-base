# Temperature limits for preservation of primary calcite clumped isotope paleotemperatures


## Overall Scientific Topic

Solid-state reordering of C–O bonds in the calcite crystal lattice can alter the clumped isotope (Δ₄₇) composition of paleothermometers such as fossil brachiopod shells without causing significant changes in shell microstructure, trace element concentrations, or other conventional preservation metrics used to evaluate sample quality. This disconnect creates a critical problem: samples that appear well-preserved by standard criteria may have experienced C–O bond reordering that destroys or overprints primary paleoclimate signals. This study establishes quantitative constraints on the temperature and time domains where primary clumped isotope compositions are preserved in calcite, enabling researchers to distinguish genuine paleoclimate information from thermally altered signals.

## Methods

Preservation of fossil brachiopods was characterized using plane-polarized light microscopy and cathodoluminescence (CL) to identify microstructural integrity and burial alteration. Laboratory heating experiments were conducted on calcite samples from Paleozoic brachiopod shells, heating them at different temperatures and durations to directly observe rates of ¹³C–¹⁸O bond reordering. Arrhenius parameters (activation energy and frequency factor) were determined from the heating experiment results. An alternative kinetic model accounting for non-first-order reaction progress during initial heating was developed and compared with simplified first-order approximation models. Laboratory-derived kinetic parameters were tested against natural samples from sedimentary basins with independently constrained burial temperature histories.

## Results

Laboratory heating experiments yielded Arrhenius parameters indistinguishable from those previously determined for optical calcite with similar trace element compositions. A non-first-order kinetic model provided a better fit to heating data than the simplified first-order model during the initial several hundred minutes of experiments, but both models predicted similarly accurate reaction progress for the temperatures and timescales relevant to sedimentary basins. Paleozoic brachiopod shells from five sedimentary basins in the United States and Russia showed clumped isotope paleotemperatures ranging from 35 to 165°C, with elevated temperatures indicating varying degrees of alteration despite apparently good preservation by conventional microstructural criteria. Laboratory-predicted clumped isotope values, based on basin burial temperature histories, showed general correspondence (within error) with observed paleothermometer values.

## Implications

Temperature–time diagrams were developed showing domains where primary clumped isotope compositions will be preserved, partially reordered, or fully reordered in calcite. These diagrams enable prediction of preservation fidelity for fossil carbonate paleothermometers in specific burial scenarios. The study concludes that calcite samples dwelling at ≤100°C for 10⁶–10⁸ year timescales should not experience significant solid-state C–O bond reordering. This framework allows researchers to assess whether observed clumped isotope paleothermatures reflect primary paleoenvironmental conditions or have been thermally altered by burial, thereby improving confidence in paleoclimate reconstructions from fossil carbonate archives.
