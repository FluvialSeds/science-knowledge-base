# Abundant pre-industrial carbon detected in Canadian Arctic headwaters: implications for the permafrost carbon feedback

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study measures the ¹⁴C content of aquatic CO2 and dissolved organic carbon in Arctic headwater systems to assess mobilization of old permafrost carbon. Results demonstrate that pre-industrial (pre-1750) aged carbon comprised 15-40% of total aquatic carbon fluxes during the 2014 snow-free season, indicating significant destabilization of ancient soil carbon in response to active layer deepening.

## Methods

Samples were collected from six lakes, four streams, and four ponds in the western Canadian Arctic permafrost zone during the snow-free season of 2014. Age distribution analysis of aquatic carbon was performed using ¹⁴C measurements alongside dissolved organic carbon analysis. The study site is located in continuous permafrost with organic-rich soils up to 50 cm deep containing maximum bulk organic carbon ages of 2200-6200 years B.P. Repeated sampling tracked changes in carbon age as the active layer deepened during the sampling season.

## Results

Age of mobilized aquatic carbon increased significantly during the 2014 snow-free season as the active layer deepened. DOC age increased from 101-228 years before sampling date (120-125% increase), while CO2 age increased from 92-151 years (59-63% increase). Pre-industrial aged carbon (assimilated prior to AD 1750) comprised 15-40% of total aquatic carbon fluxes, demonstrating the prevalence of old carbon in Arctic headwaters. The increase in old carbon was more pronounced in DOC than in CO2.

## Implications

These findings provide direct evidence for mobilization of carbon sequestered prior to 1950 in Arctic systems in response to permafrost thaw and active layer deepening. Although the presence of old carbon does not necessarily indicate a net positive permafrost carbon feedback, these baseline data are essential for future assessment of permafrost carbon release under continued warming scenarios and for understanding climate-carbon feedbacks in the Arctic.
