# Permafrost Hydrology Drives the Assimilation of Old Carbon by Stream Food Webs in the Arctic

## Overall Scientific Topic

Permafrost thaw in the Arctic is mobilizing old carbon from soils to aquatic ecosystems and the atmosphere. However, little is known about how aquatic food webs assimilate this old carbon released from permafrost and how it affects ecosystem function. This study quantifies carbon assimilation by aquatic biota across 12 Arctic streams with varying permafrost hydrology, from ice-poor bedrock to ice-rich yedoma (Siberian deep ice), using carbon isotope tracing techniques.

## Methods

Carbon isotopes (δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C) were used to quantify carbon assimilation by stream biota across 12 streams in Arctic Alaska with watersheds ranging from ice-poor bedrock to ice-rich terrain. Isotopic measurements were made on carbon sources (dissolved organic carbon [DOC], dissolved inorganic carbon [DIC], and soil carbon) and stream biota (benthic biofilm, macroinvertebrates, and fish species: Arctic Grayling and Dolly Varden). Mixing model analysis was used to estimate relative contributions of different carbon sources to consumers.

## Results

Old carbon was assimilated by stream biota at multiple trophic levels, with Δ¹⁴C ages ranging from 5,255 to 265 years BP in benthic biofilm, 4,490 yr BP to modern in macroinvertebrates, and 3,195 yr BP to modern in fish. Mixing model results indicated DOC and DIC contribute to benthic biofilm composition, with DOC originating primarily from old terrestrial carbon sources including deep peat horizons (39–47%; 530 yr BP) and near-surface permafrost (12–19%; 5,490 yr BP). Analyses suggest that as the contribution of old carbon to fish increases, fish growth and nutritional status decline.

## Implications

As warming increases thaw of permafrost, increased delivery of old DOC to streams may further alter food web dynamics in Arctic watersheds. These findings highlight the complex linkages between permafrost hydrology, carbon mobilization, and aquatic ecosystem function in regions sensitive to climate change.
