# Temporal and spatial variations of freshwater reservoir ages in the Loire River watershed

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study maps spatial and temporal variations of the freshwater reservoir effect (FRE) in the Loire River and its tributaries using carbon isotope analysis. The research demonstrates that the geological substrate is the primary control on ¹⁴C reservoir ages, with carbonate regions showing much older apparent ages than crystalline regions. These findings improve understanding of radiocarbon correction factors needed for accurate dating of archaeological and paleoclimate records.

## Methods

Spatial and temporal ¹³C and ¹⁴C analyses of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) were conducted at 20 sampling sites in the Loire River basin during four sampling campaigns in 2004, 2005, 2013, and 2014. Sites were selected to represent diverse geological settings, soil types, and land use patterns. Samples were collected from bridges at 0-0.5 m depth. Analysis included chlorophyll-a, alkalinity, and physico-chemical parameters to assess environmental factors affecting ¹⁴C activity.

## Results

Radiocarbon FRE values ranged from 135 to 2,251 years ± 30 years depending on location, with large spatial variability objectively correlated to DIC contents and alkalinity. The geological substrate was the dominant factor controlling ¹⁴C FRE, far more influential than river flow discharge. Deeper investigations revealed that chemical weathering of ¹⁴C-free carbonates and silicates was the primary mechanism driving FRE variations across the watershed.

## Implications

These results provide essential data for correcting ¹⁴C dates in archaeological and paleoclimate studies conducted in carbonate regions. Understanding the spatial distribution and temporal behavior of FRE variations in river systems is critical for accurate radiocarbon chronologies and for predicting how changes in hydrology and geology affect FRE-related dating uncertainties.
