# Downstream evolution of the ¹⁴C signature of organic carbon in the Pearl River (south China)

## Overall Scientific Topic

Radiocarbon signatures of riverine organic matter undergo systematic changes from headwaters to mouth due to in-stream processing, mineral weathering, and mixing of terrestrial and aquatic sources. Understanding these downstream changes is critical for constraining terrestrial carbon budgets and interpreting radiocarbon ages of organic matter in estuarine and marine environments. This study traces the evolution of ¹⁴C signatures of dissolved and particulate organic carbon along the entire gradient of the Pearl River.

## Methods

Water samples for dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and particulate organic carbon (POC) were collected at multiple locations along the Pearl River main stem and tributaries during both dry season and flood season. Radiocarbon (Δ¹⁴C) and stable carbon isotope (δ¹³C) measurements were made on both DOC and POC fractions. Compound-specific radiocarbon analysis was performed on selected samples to determine ages of specific organic compounds. Size-fractionation of organic matter was used to assess changes in molecular composition downstream.

## Results

Downstream changes in Δ¹⁴C signatures revealed complex mixing of old (geologically-derived) and young (biogenic) carbon sources. Near the river mouth, POC showed highly depleted Δ¹⁴C signatures (-100 to -200‰), suggesting strong contribution from carbonate-derived DIC incorporated by aquatic photosynthesis. DOC showed more variable signatures ranging from modern to moderately depleted, with seasonal variations related to discharge-driven transport patterns. Compound-specific analysis indicated that specific biomarkers retained their source signatures despite downstream transport.

## Implications

The downstream evolution of ¹⁴C signatures reflects the combined effects of carbonate weathering, aquatic primary production, and selective preservation of different organic compounds. These results improve our understanding of how old carbon is mobilized and exported in large river systems and provide a framework for interpreting ¹⁴C data in marine and estuarine sediments.
