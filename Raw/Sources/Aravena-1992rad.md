# Evaluating dissolved inorganic carbon cycling in a forested lake watershed using carbon isotopes

## Overall Scientific Topic

Dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) is the main acid buffer in forested lake watersheds in Canada, particularly in acid-sensitive regions. Understanding the production and cycling of DIC through carbon isotope analysis is critical for predicting anthropogenic influences such as acid rain and climate modifications on carbon cycling and fluxes in northern temperate forest watersheds.

## Methods

Carbon isotopes (¹³C, ¹⁴C) were used to evaluate the production and cycling of DIC in an acid-sensitive lake watershed of the Precambrian Shield at Harp Lake, Ontario. Soil CO₂, groundwater, and stream DIC were characterized chemically and isotopically. Soil CO₂ samples were collected monthly using nested metal probes penetrating the first 100 cm of soil. Groundwater samples were collected from wells near recharge areas, in the middle of the basin, and in discharge areas. Stream water was sampled at gauging weirs. CO₂ concentrations were measured by gas chromatography and isotopic compositions by mass spectrometry and tandem accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS).

## Results

Soil CO₂ concentration profiles show three distinct seasonal patterns: an increase to maximum concentration (1.2%) at mid-summer, a decrease from July to December reaching minimum (0.3%), and a slight increase from 0.3%-0.5% between December and March. δ¹³C values of soil CO₂ ranged from about -22‰ during summer to -25‰ during winter, reflecting seasonal changes in production and diffusion processes. Carbon isotopic composition of groundwater and stream DIC clearly indicates that weathering of silicates by soil CO₂ is the main source of DIC in these watersheds. ¹⁴C data show that in addition to recent groundwater, an older groundwater component with depleted ¹⁴C activity is present in the bedrock. The carbon isotope pattern in groundwater also implies that contributions to the groundwater occur during late winter/early spring in addition to the main springtime recharge events.

## Implications

The isotopic data demonstrate that soil CO₂ production, diffusion, and hydrological pathways collectively control DIC cycling in forested watersheds. The presence of older groundwater components suggests connections to deep bedrock reservoirs. These processes are fundamental to understanding the acid neutralization capacity of acid-sensitive lake watersheds and predicting responses to future environmental changes.
