# Glacier meltwater and monsoon precipitation drive Upper Ganges Basin dissolved organic matter composition


## Overall Scientific Topic

Mountain glaciers store dissolved organic carbon (DOC) that is exported to river networks and subsequently respired to CO₂. Despite this potential importance for the global carbon cycle, the seasonal variability and downstream transport of glacier-derived DOC in mountainous river basins remains poorly understood. This study examines dissolved organic matter (DOM) composition across 22 nested, glaciated catchments (1.4–81.8% glacier cover) in the Upper Ganges Basin, Western Himalaya during the 2014 Indian summer monsoon (ISM), revealing how glacier melt and monsoon precipitation drive DOM composition and bioavailability.

## Methods

Water was collected from multiple sites spanning a wide range in glacial coverage (10.3 to 81.8% of catchment area) during the Indian summer monsoon. Samples were immediately filtered through precombusted (450°C, 4 h) 0.45 μm glass fiber filters using acid-leached filtration towers to prevent contamination. DOM composition was determined at the molecular level using Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR-MS) to characterize compound classes. Incubation experiments assessed the bioavailable fraction of dissolved organic carbon (BDOC) by measuring respiration in dark bottles.

## Results

Aliphatic and peptide-like compounds were abundant in glacier-fed headwaters but were progressively replaced by soil-derived phenolic, polyphenolic, and condensed aromatic material as DOC concentrations increased moving downstream. Across the basin, DOC concentrations and soil-derived compound class contributions decreased sharply from pre-monsoon to post-monsoon, indicating increased relative contribution of glacier-fed signals as the monsoon progresses. Glacier-derived DOC showed the highest bioavailability (BDOC fraction), while soil-derived DOM showed lower bioavailability. The study demonstrates a strong compositional control on DOC bioavailability, with glacier DOM being far more labile than soil-derived material.

## Implications

Short-term (coming decades) increases in glacier melt flux driven by climate warming will further bias exported DOM toward an aliphatic-rich, bioavailable signal, especially during the monsoon and post-monsoon seasons, increasing aquatic respiration and CO₂ emissions. In contrast, eventual decreases in glacier melt flux due to glacier mass loss will drive DOM composition toward a soil-like, aromatic-rich, less bioavailable composition, likely reducing aquatic respiration rates. These results demonstrate the tight coupling between glacier mass balance, monsoon precipitation, and the quality and quantity of organic carbon exported from mountain river systems.
