# Evidence for the respiration of ancient terrestrial organic C in northern temperate lakes and streams

## Overall Scientific Topic

Lakes and rivers process large quantities of organic carbon from surrounding terrestrial ecosystems, resulting in widespread CO₂ supersaturation and significant emissions to the atmosphere. However, the nature (age and source) of the terrestrial organic carbon respired in aquatic systems has remained largely unknown. This study provides the first direct measurements of the radiocarbon age of organic carbon respired by aquatic bacteria, revealing that a significant fraction of respired carbon is ancient (1,000–3,000 years old) rather than contemporary.

## Methods

Radiocarbon measurements were made directly on CO₂ respired by bacteria in short-term incubations of natural lake and river water from five temperate lakes and two streams in southern Quebec, Canada. The Δ¹⁴C signatures of respiratory CO₂ were measured using accelerator mass spectrometry. The study measured the age composition of organic matter being respired in these aquatic systems to understand carbon bioavailability and turnover times.

## Results

The Δ¹⁴C of respiratory CO₂ varied widely (from +94‰ to -172‰), indicating that aquatic bacteria consume organic carbon ranging from post-bomb ages to carbon over 1,000 years old. This demonstrates selective removal of an old, highly bioavailable terrestrial organic carbon pool despite the bulk organic carbon pool being relatively young. The data challenge the paradigm that age is a priori indicator of chemical recalcitrance, showing instead that old carbon can be highly bioavailable and preferentially consumed by aquatic bacteria.

## Implications

These findings provide fundamental evidence that old terrestrial carbon stored in soils can be mobilized, transported to aquatic systems, and respired by microorganisms, thus returning to the atmosphere carbon putatively considered permanently stored. This challenges current models of long-term carbon storage in terrestrial reservoirs and has important implications for understanding terrestrial-aquatic carbon linkages and the role of lakes and rivers in regional carbon cycling.
