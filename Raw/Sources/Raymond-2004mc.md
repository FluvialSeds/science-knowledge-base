# Controls on the variability of organic matter and dissolved inorganic carbon ages in northeast US rivers

## Overall Scientific Topic

Radiocarbon (¹⁴C) can provide unique source, turnover, and processing information for organic and inorganic carbon in aquatic ecosystems, yet few comprehensive surveys have been conducted on river systems that connect terrestrial and oceanic carbon cycles. This study examines the radiocarbon content and age variability of dissolved and particulate organic carbon (DOC and POC) and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) across seven northeast US rivers to understand the controls on riverine carbon ages and their sources.

## Methods

Water samples were collected from seven northeast US rivers (including the Hudson, Delaware, Potomac, James, York, Rappahannock, and Pamunkey rivers) at multiple sites and times. Radiocarbon measurements of bulk DOC, POC, and DIC fractions were conducted using accelerator mass spectrometry. Stable carbon isotope composition (δ¹³C) was also measured for all fractions. Results were compared across rivers and related to potential sources of carbon including wetlands, ancient sedimentary organic matter, and in-system processing.

## Results

The examined rivers showed large ranges in Δ¹⁴C for DOC, POC, and DIC, indicating both ancient and fully modern post-bomb radiocarbon content. DOC ¹⁴C ages ranged from modern to several thousand years BP, while POC showed similarly variable ages. DIC ages varied depending on whether the carbon originated from carbonate weathering versus atmospheric/biological sources. Large spatial and temporal variability was observed across the geographically contiguous set of rivers, suggesting different local sources and processing pathways for carbon in each system.

## Implications

The large variability in riverine carbon ages across northeast US rivers demonstrates that local sources (including wetlands, ancient uplifted sediments, and in-stream processing) have significant impacts on the ages of carbon exported from rivers to the coastal ocean. These findings highlight the need for multiple ¹⁴C measurements across river systems to fully characterize carbon dynamics and constrain regional carbon budgets.
