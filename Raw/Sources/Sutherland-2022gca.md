# The influence of reactive oxygen species on respiration isotope effects

## Overall Scientific Topic

Triple-oxygen isotope (¹⁷O/¹⁶O, ¹⁸O/¹⁶O) measurements of dissolved oxygen are used globally to determine gross primary productivity (GPP) from the isotopic composition of marine dissolved O₂. The method relies on predictable isotope fractionation factors for three processes: photosynthetic oxygen production, microbial respiration, and gas exchange with the atmosphere. However, significant disagreement exists in the literature regarding the triple-oxygen isotope effect of respiration. Recent discoveries have shown that microorganisms ubiquitously produce extracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS)—such as superoxide radical (O₂⁻) and hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)—through enzymatic pathways, potentially accounting for ~20% of net oxygen utilization in the ocean. This study investigates whether ROS-mediated oxygen cycling affects the apparent triple-oxygen isotope fractionation factor for respiration, and whether consideration of ROS-cycling processes can reconcile previously conflicting respiration isotope effects reported in the literature.

## Methods

Triple-oxygen isotope fractionations were measured for three ROS-related enzymatic reactions: (1) superoxide dismutase, which catalyzes the disproportionation of superoxide radical (O₂⁻ + O₂⁻ + 2H⁺ → H₂O₂ + O₂); (2) catalase, which decomposes hydrogen peroxide (2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂); and (3) iron-mediated H₂O₂ degradation (Fenton-like reactions).

Kinetic isotope fractionations (λ values, where λ = Δ(ln¹⁸O)/Δ(ln¹⁷O)) were determined for each process by measuring the isotopic composition of product and substrate oxygen under controlled laboratory conditions.

An oxygen isotope flux model was developed incorporating ROS-mediated cycling pathways alongside conventional respiration and photosynthesis. The model explored how ROS production rates varying from 5% to 20% of total oxygen consumption would affect the apparent triple-oxygen isotope fractionation factor observed in the bulk system.

## Results

Superoxide dismutase (and the catalase-mediated H₂O₂ decomposition that follows) produced oxygen with a distinct triple-oxygen isotope fractionation signature. The combined effect of superoxide-to-peroxide conversion and subsequent decomposition creates an oxygen isotope effect that is different from the canonical respiration isotope effect.

Iron-mediated H₂O₂ degradation (Fenton-like reactions) showed non-canonical triple-oxygen isotope mass laws (λ ≠ 0.5), indicating that this process produces oxygen with unusual isotope signatures distinct from typical mass-dependent fractionation.

When ROS-mediated oxygen cycling was incorporated into a flux model of the marine oxygen cycle, the observed bulk oxygen isotope effect in the ocean could be reconciled with previously conflicting laboratory measurements of the respiration isotope effect. Models with ROS production rates of 10–20% of total oxygen consumption produced oxygen isotope effects consistent with field observations, while models without ROS cycling could not reproduce the observed isotope signatures.

The results indicated that superoxide-mediated oxygen loss (by reaction with organic substrates and other molecules) likely follows non-canonical triple-oxygen isotope mass laws (λ ≈ 0.51 ± 0.01), exerting significant leverage on bulk dissolved oxygen isotope compositions.

## Implications

ROS-mediated oxygen cycling must be incorporated into triple-oxygen isotope models used for gross oxygen production (GOP) estimation. The previously conflicting respiration isotope effects reported in different studies can be rationalized by considering that ROS production rates vary depending on microbial community composition, oxygen concentrations, and available organic carbon—factors that differ between laboratory cultures, coastal environments, and open ocean settings.

This discovery has significant consequences for paleoproductivity estimates based on triple-oxygen isotopes in ancient marine records. Paleoenvironmental conditions that altered ROS production rates (such as changes in oxygen concentrations, microbial community composition, or organic carbon availability) would also alter the apparent respiration isotope effect, affecting calculated GPP estimates.

For modern oceanographic applications, understanding ROS-mediated oxygen isotope fractionation is essential for accurate quantification of gross primary productivity and net metabolic state from dissolved oxygen isotope measurements. Future work should focus on measuring ROS production rates across different marine environments to better constrain the contribution of ROS-mediated oxygen cycling to bulk oxygen isotope systematics.

The work demonstrates that seemingly simple biogeochemical cycles (oxygen cycling) involve previously underappreciated intermediate reactions that have significant isotope effects and can substantially alter the apparent isotope signature of the overall process.
