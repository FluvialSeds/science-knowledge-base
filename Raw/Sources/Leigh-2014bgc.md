# Source and age of dissolved and gaseous carbon in a peatland–riparian–stream continuum: a dual isotope (¹⁴C and δ¹³C) analysis

## Overall Scientific Topic

Peatlands are globally important carbon stores, but the sources and ages of carbon exported from peatlands to streams remain poorly understood, particularly regarding the role of riparian zones. This study examines the isotopic composition of soil organic matter, dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and gaseous carbon (CO₂ and CH₄) in a peatland-riparian-stream transect to determine carbon sources and the relative contributions of different carbon pools to stream export.

## Methods

Soil cores and pore water samples were collected from both peatland and riparian zones at Auchencorth Moss, a temperate peatland in Scotland, during two sampling campaigns in spring and autumn 2012. Soil gases (CO₂ and CH₄) were collected from probes installed at different depths, and stream water samples were collected to measure dissolved gases and DOC. Both ¹⁴C (radiocarbon) and δ¹³C (stable carbon isotope) analyses were performed on all carbon fractions to determine their ages and sources. The study tested hypotheses about the relative importance of peatland vs. riparian zone as carbon sources to streams.

## Results

Peatland soils had a mean radiocarbon age of 551 ± 133 years BP, with ages increasing with depth. The riparian zone had an older mean radiocarbon age of 1,055 ± 107 years BP with no age/depth relationship, reflecting different depositional processes. Most soil decomposition products (CO₂, CH₄, and DOC) were predominantly modern (100% modern ¹⁴C values), indicating recent carbon fixation. However, gases evaded from the stream surface were considerably older (CH₄: 310–537 years BP; CO₂: 36 years BP to modern), showing a complex mix of soil organic matter and geogenic carbon sources. The peatland, not the riparian zone, appears to be the dominant source of carbon to stream water.

## Implications

This study demonstrates considerable vertical transport of modern carbon within peat profiles and highlights the importance of distinguishing between riparian and peatland contributions to aquatic carbon exports. The findings suggest that contemporary carbon cycling in peatland headwaters is dominated by recently fixed organic matter, with implications for understanding how peatland carbon storage may respond to environmental changes.
