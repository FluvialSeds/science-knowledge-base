# The known unknowns of petrogenic organic carbon in soils

## Overall Scientific Topic

This perspective paper calls attention to a critical but overlooked component of soil organic carbon: petrogenic organic carbon (OCpetro) derived from ancient (meta-)sedimentary bedrock such as shale. Current soil carbon models assume that organic carbon inputs are primarily biospheric (plant-derived), completely overlooking OCpetro contributions. This omission creates significant knowledge gaps about soil carbon stocks, turnover times, and the response of soil carbon to climate change and land management. The authors outline four key questions defining critical research needs at the bedrock–soil continuum to understand OCpetro transport, stability, and cycling—addressing how OCpetro inputs may prime microbial activity, influence carbon turnover calculations via radiocarbon, and respond to land-use changes.

## Methods

This is a perspective/commentary article rather than a traditional research study. The authors synthesize existing empirical work on OCpetro from diverse geoscience subdisciplines (palynology, organic petrology, geochemistry), reviewing how radiocarbon measurements and thermal methods (e.g., ramped pyrolysis) have enabled quantitative discrimination of OCpetro from biospheric OC. They compile evidence for OCpetro's presence and ecological significance across terrestrial and marine environments, and highlight methodological advances (isotopic, spectroscopic, thermal analysis) that allow differentiation of recently photosynthesized OC from ancient OCpetro. The paper then identifies four critical knowledge gaps through systematic questioning.

## Results

The analysis reveals that: (1) OCpetro is prevalent in surface environments globally, with transport from geosphere to pedosphere occurring via weathering and soil formation processes governed by soil production functions; (2) Controls on OCpetro assimilation by subsoil microbial communities remain poorly understood, though kerogen's condensed aromatic carbon structure likely limits biodegradability; (3) Soil priming effects—where fresh OC inputs trigger mineralization of resistant pools—may affect OCpetro stability but remain completely unstudied for this carbon fraction; and (4) Land-use and management impacts on OCpetro are essentially unquantified, despite evidence that soil erosion, vegetation colonization, and root penetration into bedrock may significantly alter OCpetro oxidation rates. The paper demonstrates that ignoring OCpetro results in overestimation of biospheric carbon stocks and turnover times, and misrepresents soil carbon dynamics in landscapes underlain by OC-bearing bedrock.

## Implications

Incorporating OCpetro into soil carbon models is essential for accurate carbon cycle accounting, especially given ongoing climate change and accelerating bedrock weathering in high-latitude and -altitude regions. The authors argue that conceptualizing the bedrock–soil continuum as a unified system, rather than discrete Earth System compartments, is critical for resolving OCpetro cycling processes. Cross-disciplinary collaboration between soil scientists, geochemists, and ecosystem ecologists is urgently needed to quantify OCpetro transport rates, microbial decomposition kinetics, priming effects, and response to land management. Better understanding OCpetro dynamics will improve soil carbon models, enable more accurate projections of soil carbon response to climate change, and help design effective land-management strategies for carbon sequestration and Net Zero emissions goals.
