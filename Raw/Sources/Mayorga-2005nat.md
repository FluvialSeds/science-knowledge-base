# Young organic matter as a source of carbon dioxide outgassing from Amazonian rivers

## Overall Scientific Topic

Amazonian rivers are supersaturated with carbon dioxide (CO₂) and outgas more than ten times the amount of carbon exported to the ocean in dissolved and particulate forms, making them a significant component of regional carbon budgets. However, the sources and turnover times of carbon fueling this CO₂ outgassing have been uncertain. This study uses dual carbon isotope analysis to demonstrate that respiration of young, recently-fixed organic matter (less than five years old) is the dominant source of excess CO₂ that drives outgassing in medium to large Amazonian rivers.

## Methods

An extensive survey of carbon isotope composition (¹³C and ¹⁴C) of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) and three size-fractions of organic carbon was conducted across the Amazonian river system from the Andes to the mainstem. Sites were categorized topographically by drainage area elevation. Both stable (δ¹³C) and radiocarbon (Δ¹⁴C) isotopes were measured on DIC, dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and suspended particulate organic carbon fractions (fine and coarse POC). Temporal evolution of atmospheric ¹⁴C-CO₂ was used to constrain carbon turnover times.

## Results

CO₂ in nearly all lowland rivers had Δ¹⁴C signatures consistent with modern atmospheric values, indicating CO₂ derived from respiration of organic carbon fixed within approximately 2–7 years. This represents rapid turnover of photosynthetically sequestered atmospheric CO₂. While bulk organic carbon fractions transported by Amazonian rivers range from tens to thousands of years in age, the CO₂ outgassed from these rivers originates from a small, rapidly-cycling pool of labile organic carbon. This young carbon pool is disproportionately composed of river-corridor and floodplain C₄ grasses, which may be intrinsically more biodegradable than other organic matter sources.

## Implications

These findings demonstrate that a small, rapidly cycling pool of young organic carbon is responsible for the large CO₂ evasion fluxes from Amazonian rivers. This paradigm has important implications for understanding carbon cycling in humid tropical regions and suggests that deforestation in the Amazon leads to immediate changes in riverine carbon sources available for respiration and outgassing.
