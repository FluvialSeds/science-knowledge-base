# The experimental flow to the Colorado River delta: Effects on carbon mobilization in a dry watercourse

## Overall Scientific Topic

The Colorado River below the U.S.-Mexican border is mostly a dry watercourse due to upstream water extraction. In 2014, the dry reaches were flooded for the first time since 1999 as part of an experimental flow restoration. This event provided a unique opportunity to investigate carbon mobilization and greenhouse gas dynamics in a long dry watercourse following a pulse flow event.

## Methods

Water samples were collected at six sampling sites during the onset of flooding (23-29 March 2014), near peak flow (3 March to 2 April 2014), and during receding flow (17-18 April 2014). Analysis included measurements of dissolved organic carbon (DOC), dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), particulate organic carbon (POC), methane and CO₂ concentrations, and carbon isotopes (δ¹³C and ¹⁴C). Chemical biomarkers (lignin phenols) were analyzed to assess degradation history. Spectrofluorometric measurements characterized chromophoric dissolved organic matter. A simple laboratory leaching experiment was performed with dry riverbed sand to estimate dissolved organic matter release.

## Results

Post-flood differences were observed in the degree of decay, age, and concentration of DOC, as well as dissolved CH₄ and CO₂ concentrations. The flooded waterway was a limited source of CH₄ and CO₂ to the atmosphere during the event. DOC age increased with time of flooding. Changes in radiocarbon age of DOC throughout the flooding event suggest that organic matter stored for long periods (e.g., millennial timescales) was mobilized by the flooding event along with CO₂. The organic matter residing in the dry riverbed had a signature reflective of degraded vascular plant material and microbial biomass.

## Implications

The interplay between storage and mobilization of carbon and greenhouse gases in arid and semiarid regions is potentially sensitive to changing climate conditions, particularly hydrologic variability. As human appropriation of water resources continues to increase, the episodic drying and rewetting of once natural riverbeds and deltas may fundamentally alter the processing and storage of carbon in such systems. These findings highlight the need to better quantify carbon cycling dynamics in temporarily dry watercourses globally.
