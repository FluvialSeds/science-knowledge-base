# Lake water dissolved inorganic carbon dynamics revealed from monthly measurements of radiocarbon in the Fuji Five Lakes, Japan

## Overall Scientific Topic

Radiocarbon reservoir ages in lakes are notoriously difficult to constrain due to complex processes governing radiocarbon transfer from the atmosphere to lake waters. Yet accurate knowledge of these reservoir ages is essential for reliable radiocarbon dating of lake sediments used in paleoenvironmental reconstructions. This study presents the first systematic monthly monitoring of radiocarbon in dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) of multiple lakes to understand how local hydrological dynamics control radiocarbon reservoir effects.

## Methods

Lake water samples were collected monthly from June 2018 to April 2019 from the Fuji Five Lakes (Motosu, Shoji, Sai, Kawaguchi, and Yamanaka) in Japan. Groundwater samples from wells on both Mt. Fuji and northern mountain sides were also collected. River water samples were collected during high-flow periods. All samples were analyzed for Δ¹⁴C of DIC using accelerator mass spectrometry. Hydrochemical parameters and lake retention times were measured to understand the physical factors controlling radiocarbon signatures.

## Results

Significant seasonal variations in lake water DIC Δ¹⁴C were observed, with Lakes Motosu and Kawaguchi showing lower (older) Δ¹⁴C values in summer and higher (younger) values in winter, indicating seasonal changes in groundwater input. Lakes Sai and Yamanaka showed less distinct seasonal patterns. Groundwater samples showed highly variable Δ¹⁴C values depending on location, with depleted values characteristic of old groundwater. Mixing model analysis suggests groundwater contributes 20% of DIC in Lake Motosu, 50% in Lake Kawaguchi, and over 80% in Lakes Sai and Yamanaka.

## Implications

These results demonstrate that seasonal variations in lake water Δ¹⁴C are driven primarily by changes in groundwater contribution rather than retention time. Lake water DIC radiocarbon, particularly from spring to summer when aquatic primary production is high, can be used to estimate and correct radiocarbon ages of lake sediments. This approach provides a tool for improving the accuracy of radiocarbon dating in paleoenvironmental studies and understanding lake carbon cycling dynamics.
