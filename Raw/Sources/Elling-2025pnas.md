# A novel quinone biosynthetic pathway illuminates the evolution of aerobic metabolism

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study characterizes a previously unknown high-potential respiratory quinone (methyl-plastoquinone, mPQ) unique to bacteria of the phylum Nitrospirota and demonstrates that it shares an ancient common evolutionary origin with the two previously known high-redox potential quinones—plastoquinone (PQ) from Cyanobacteriota and ubiquinone (UQ) from Pseudomonadota. The discovery reveals that the evolution of aerobic metabolism and high-potential electron transport chains significantly predates Earth's surface oxygenation during the Great Oxygenation Event (~2.3 Ga). By tracking the phylogenetic distribution and biosynthetic mechanisms of all three HPQ types, this work provides new insights into how aerobic respiration evolved and spread through early bacterial lineages.

## Methods

Researchers analyzed lipid extracts from anaerobic and aerobic Nitrospirota species using high-performance liquid chromatography coupled to high-resolution tandem mass spectrometry. Mass spectrometric characterization and structural analysis including stable isotope labeling experiments and NMR spectroscopy confirmed the structure of mPQ as a trimethyl-benzoquinone headgroup connected to an isoprenoid tail. Bioinformatic analysis of Nitrospirota genomes identified putative quinone biosynthesis gene clusters. Comparative genomics examined the distribution of hpnP (hopanoid methyltransferase) and related methyltransferase genes across marine and terrestrial bacterial lineages. Phylogenetic analysis was performed on HPQ biosynthetic pathway genes to determine evolutionary relationships among Nitrospirota, Cyanobacteriota, and Pseudomonadota.

## Results

All eight studied aerobic Nitrospirota species contained mPQ (varying in polyprenyl chain length and saturation by species), while anaerobic Nitrospirota contained only menaquinone (MK), a low-redox potential quinone. mPQ is structurally related to both UQ and PQ, sharing methylation at C2 with UQ and at C5/C6 with PQ. Isotope labeling experiments confirmed that all three methyl groups of mPQ are derived from methionine via SAM-dependent methyltransferases, mechanistically similar to UQ and PQ biosynthesis. Phylogenetic analysis of HPQ biosynthetic pathways revealed a single evolutionary origin predating the emergence of Nitrospirota, Cyanobacteriota, and Pseudomonadota. An ancestral HPQ pathway evolved ≥3.4 billion years ago and was laterally transferred to these three phyla ~2.5–3.2 Ga.

## Implications

The identification of mPQ and its ancient evolutionary origin demonstrate that the three modern HPQ types all evolved from a common ancestor that existed well before atmospheric oxygen accumulation. This evidence indicates that Cyanobacteriota and Pseudomonadota (and likely Nitrospirota) were ancestrally aerobic organisms capable of using high-potential quinones for efficient aerobic respiration, predating the Great Oxygenation Event by potentially 1+ billion years. The evolution of HPQs to reduce oxidative stress in aerobic conditions may have driven early diversification of aerobic metabolism in anoxic or weakly oxygenated niches. This work changes our understanding of when and how aerobic metabolism evolved and suggests that oxygen-utilizing metabolisms emerged and diversified in microbial lineages long before atmospheric oxygenation, with important implications for early Earth biogeochemistry.
