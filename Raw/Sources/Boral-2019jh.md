# Stable water isotope modeling reveals spatio-temporal variability of glacier meltwater contributions to Ganges River headwaters

## Overall Scientific Topic

Himalayan headwater rivers are sourced from glacier meltwater and Indian summer monsoon (ISM) precipitation, both strongly influenced by ongoing climate change. This study uses stable water isotopes (δ¹⁸O and δD) and electrical conductivity measured across three seasons over three years (2014-2016) in the Upper Ganges Basin to determine water source contributions. A novel isotope mixing model combined with optimum multiparameter analysis (OMPA) reveals large seasonal variability in water source contributions. Contrary to previous model predictions, glacier meltwater contributions were highest during ISM and post-ISM seasons rather than pre-ISM months. The results indicate rain-induced glacier melting during monsoon rains can produce 3-11% of total discharge at the glacier snout, demonstrating complex seasonal dynamics that must be considered when predicting hydrologic responses to climate change in this region.

## Methods

Water samples were collected from 17 sites spanning the Ganges River Basin during pre-ISM (April-June), ISM (July-September), and post-ISM (October-December) periods over three years (2014-2016). Stable oxygen and hydrogen isotope compositions were measured by isotope ratio infrared spectroscopy (IRIS) at the Department of Earth Sciences, Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur. Temperature-compensated electrical conductivity was measured using a handheld multi-parameter sonde. Groundwater samples were collected from hand-pumped tube wells, and glacier ice samples were collected from glacier margins. Water source apportionment was performed using two complementary approaches: (1) a dual-tracer (δ¹⁸O and deuterium excess) three-component mixing model accounting for glacier meltwater, precipitation, and groundwater contributions, and (2) optimum multiparameter analysis (OMPA) using δ¹⁸O, deuterium excess, and electrical conductivity. Both methods use Monte Carlo iterations (10,000 runs) to quantify uncertainties from natural variability in end-member compositions.

## Results

Glacier meltwater fractional contributions varied substantially over space and time. Spatially-averaged glacier meltwater fractions across the entire Ganges headwater basin were 25 ± 17% (pre-ISM), 46 ± 16% (ISM), and 41 ± 20% (post-ISM). Near the glacier snout, glacier meltwater contributions were highest during post-ISM (73 ± 12%) and lowest during pre-ISM (51 ± 4%), with contributions decreasing downstream as precipitation and groundwater contributions increased. Results from both the δ¹⁸O-deuterium excess mixing model and OMPA showed good agreement, validating the robustness of both approaches. Energy balance calculations demonstrate that heat released by freezing rainfall during ISM months could generate rain-induced glacier melting amounting to 3-11% of total discharge at the glacier snout. Groundwater contributions reached more than 50% of total discharge during post-ISM months, substantially higher than previous estimates.

## Implications

This study demonstrates that glacier meltwater contributions to Himalayan rivers exhibit complex seasonal dynamics driven by multiple mechanisms: protracted release of glacier meltwater from previous melting, rain-induced glacier melting triggered by ISM rainfall, and seasonal variability in snow cover and drainage network efficiency. The finding that glacier meltwater proportions are highest during warm ISM and post-ISM seasons (contrary to conventional expectations of peak contributions during hot pre-ISM months) has important implications for understanding hydrologic responses to climate change. The role of rain-induced glacier melting represents an under-appreciated mechanism that should be considered in hydrological models predicting future water availability in South Asian river systems dependent on Himalayan glaciers. These results underscore the value of ground-based isotopic observations for constraining water source contributions and improving process-based understanding of glacier-fed river systems.
