# The triple oxygen isotope composition of marine sulfate and 130 million years of microbial control

## Overall Scientific Topic

This study examines the triple oxygen isotope composition (Δ¹⁷O) of marine sulfate over the last 130 million years to reconstruct changes in atmospheric oxygen levels and primary productivity. Marine barite minerals preserve a high-resolution record of sulfate oxygen and sulfur isotope ratios, which reflect the balance between terrestrial weathering, microbial sulfate reduction, and sedimentary export. The research demonstrates how microbial metabolic processes have controlled the geochemical composition of seawater sulfate across deep time, linking oxygen isotope records to changes in gross primary productivity and atmospheric pO₂/pCO₂.

## Methods

The triple oxygen isotope composition (Δ¹⁷O) of sulfate minerals is used to track atmospheric pO₂/pCO₂ and gross primary productivity (GPP) in the geologic past. This proxy is based on the assumption that between 10% and 25% of oxygen atoms in sulfate produced from pyrite weathering originate from atmospheric O₂. Tropospheric O₂ contains a large negative ¹⁷O anomaly (Δ¹⁷O < 0) that reflects a balance of stratospheric exchange reactions, where pO₂/pCO₂ sets the absolute magnitude of this effect, and dilution by gross primary production at Earth’s surface. The study compiled high-resolution records of δ¹⁸O and Δ¹⁷O in marine barite minerals over the last 130 million years, analyzing the covariance of these isotope ratios with δ³⁴S records to constrain the relative importance of different processes controlling marine sulfate composition.

## Results

The stable isotope composition of seawater sulfate records the biogeochemical cycling of sulfur and oxygen over geological timescales. Since 130 million years ago, marine barite (BaSO₄) minerals most faithfully preserve this isotopic record. The sulfur isotope record (δ³⁴S) of marine barite has been interpreted as reflecting a balance between terrestrial weathering, microbial sulfate reduction, and sedimentary export from the ocean, with recent suggestions indicating an outsized control from sulfur injected through large igneous province emplacement. In parallel, the marine barite oxygen isotope record (δ¹⁸O) largely reflects changes in microbial recycling, linking gross metabolic activity to environmental features such as the locus of organic matter oxidation, sea level, and nutrient availability. The triple oxygen isotope data (Δ¹⁷O) reveals the covariance between oxygen and sulfur isotope ratios, constraining the relative contributions of atmospheric versus microbial processes to marine sulfate composition over the past 130 million years.

## Implications

The covariance between marine sulfate δ¹⁸O and δ³⁴S over the past 130 million years reveals that the composition of the marine sulfate reservoir is under strict control by microbial processes, particularly sulfate reduction. The apparent lack of a direct atmospheric O₂ signal in the triple oxygen isotope record indicates that biogeochemical cycling dominates over passive atmospheric influence, suggesting that changes in microbial metabolism and primary productivity have been the primary drivers of sulfate isotope variations throughout the Cretaceous and Cenozoic. These findings demonstrate the fundamental importance of microbial processes in regulating the chemical composition of the oceans across geological timescales.
