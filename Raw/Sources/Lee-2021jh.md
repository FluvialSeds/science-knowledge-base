# Loads and ages of carbon from the five largest rivers in South Korea under Asian monsoon climates

## Overall Scientific Topic

Rivers are important conduits for carbon transport from terrestrial ecosystems to the ocean. Understanding the loads and ages of carbon (both dissolved inorganic and organic) in Asian rivers is critical for constraining global carbon budgets, yet these regions remain understudied compared to large tropical rivers. This study characterizes the amounts, sources, and radiocarbon ages of dissolved and particulate carbon exported by South Korea's five largest rivers under monsoonal climate conditions.

## Methods

Water samples were collected from the five largest rivers in South Korea (Han, Nakdong, Geum, Yeongsan, and Seomjin) at monthly intervals over one year. The study measured dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and particulate organic carbon (POC) concentrations using standard analytical techniques. Carbon isotope analyses included both stable isotopes (δ¹³C) and radiocarbon (Δ¹⁴C) measurements to determine carbon ages and sources. Seasonal variation in carbon export was evaluated in the context of monsoon-driven discharge patterns typical of Asian river systems.

## Results

All five South Korean rivers showed highly variable carbon loads and ages depending on seasonal discharge conditions and specific river characteristics. DIC concentrations ranged widely among rivers, reflecting different weathering intensities in their watersheds. DOC and POC were predominantly modern (less than 50 years old) in most samples, consistent with recent terrestrial primary production. However, some samples showed radiocarbon-depleted carbon ages ranging from several hundred to over 1,000 years, indicating contribution from older soil organic matter and fossil sources. The five rivers collectively transport significant quantities of young organic carbon to the coastal ocean, particularly during high-flow monsoon periods.

## Implications

The study demonstrates that Asian monsoonal rivers export a mixture of young and old carbon to coastal seas. These results have important implications for understanding how land-use changes and climate variability in Asian watersheds affect global carbon cycling and coastal productivity.
