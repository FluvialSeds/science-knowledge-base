# Hydrothermal dolomitisation of a deep-water bioherm isolated in a non-dolomitised intraplatform basin within the Norian Dolomia Principale

## Overall Scientific Topic

Dolomite (CaMg(CO₃)₂) is an abundant carbonate mineral in ancient sedimentary rocks, yet the mechanisms controlling its formation remain incompletely understood. This study documents hydrothermal dolomitisation of a deep-water bioherm (coral-sponge limestone mound) within the Dolomia Principale formation (Norian age, ~228–207 million years ago). The bioherm is surrounded by non-dolomitised intraplatform basin sediments, indicating that dolomitisation was localized to the bioherm structure. Petrographic, geochemical, and fluid inclusion evidence demonstrates that dolomitisation occurred through fluid-rock interaction driven by hydrothermal circulation, with dolomite precipitating in response to elevated temperature and Mg/Ca ratios. The study reveals how structural features (organic mounds, fracture networks) and fluid circulation patterns control the spatial and temporal distribution of dolomite in the geological record.

## Methods

Samples were collected from dolomitised bioherm and surrounding non-dolomitised intraplatform basin sediments of the Dolomia Principale formation. Petrographic analysis via transmitted and reflected light microscopy was used to characterize mineralogy, textures, and diagenetic features. Elemental and isotopic compositions were measured by electron microprobe analysis and stable isotope mass spectrometry to determine Mg/Ca ratios, trace element compositions, and δ¹³C and δ¹⁸O signatures. Fluid inclusion microthermometry provided temperature and composition constraints on dolomitizing fluids. Scanning electron microscopy with energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (SEM-EDS) characterized dolomite crystal morphologies and compositions.

## Results

Petrographic observations reveal two distinct dolomite generations: an early phase formed within the bioherm during burial, and a later phase associated with fracture networks and recrystallization. Elemental and isotopic data indicate that dolomite precipitated from warm, Mg-rich brines distinct from ambient seawater, consistent with hydrothermal circulation. Fluid inclusion homogenization temperatures of 60–120°C are consistent with moderately deep burial and hydrothermal alteration. The dolomitisation is strictly localized to the bioherm structure and associated fracture networks; surrounding basin sediments remained calcitic despite similar burial depths. The differential dolomitisation reflects local fluid circulation patterns enhanced by the high permeability and structural relief of the bioherm.

## Implications

Hydrothermal dolomitisation controlled by fluid circulation through permeable carbonate structures explains the spatial heterogeneity of dolomite in ancient carbonates. The bioherm-specific dolomitisation indicates that organic mounds and other high-relief structures focus fluid flow and create pathways for dolomitizing fluid circulation. These findings provide a mechanistic framework for understanding dolomite distribution in basin-scale carbonate successions and for interpreting dolomite as a paleofluid indicator. The results emphasize the importance of structural features and fluid circulation in controlling diagenetic mineralogy in carbonate rocks.
