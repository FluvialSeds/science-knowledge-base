# Glacial and Subglacial Carbon Cycling

## Overview

Glaciers and subglacial environments represent distinct hydrogeochemical settings where carbon cycling is fundamentally different from non-glaciated systems. Beneath glaciers, chemical weathering occurs under high water pressure, temperature gradients, and in contact with freshly exposed bedrock, producing unique signatures of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC). Glacial discharge carries this subglacially-produced carbon to downstream rivers and eventually to the ocean, where it influences coastal biogeochemistry and paleoceanographic proxies. Understanding glacial carbon cycling is essential for predicting how the global cryosphere's response to climate warming will alter carbon export, and for interpreting paleoceanographic records from glaciated regions during past ice sheet expansions and contractions.

## Subglacial Chemical Weathering and DIC Production

[[SubglacialInorganicCarbonChemistry|Subglacial environments produce dissolved inorganic carbon through carbonic acid weathering of bedrock under ice sheets and glaciers]]. The mechanics of subglacial carbon production differ from open-system weathering: water in contact with exposed bedrock becomes saturated with CO₂ from respiration and organic matter oxidation, producing carbonic acid that dissolves silicate and carbonate minerals. The high water pressure beneath glaciers facilitates mineral dissolution and can mobilize carbon that would not be accessible in lower-pressure environments. Additionally, subglacial discharge occurs with elevated temperatures relative to surface water, affecting both the kinetics of chemical weathering and the equilibrium speciation of inorganic carbon (DIC vs. CO₂ vs. HCO₃⁻ vs. CO₃²⁻).

The radiocarbon age of subglacial DIC reflects a complex mixture of carbon sources: some DIC is produced recently from atmospheric CO₂ (modern radiocarbon age), while other DIC derives from oxidation of organic matter or mobilization of old groundwater reservoirs (potentially depleted in ¹⁴C). In many glacier systems, the DIC exported in glacial discharge carries intermediate radiocarbon ages (hundreds to thousands of years), indicating mixing between young weathering-derived carbon and old groundwater contributions.

## Glacial Discharge and Temporal Variation

The magnitude and composition of glacial discharge varies dramatically with season and ice melt intensity:

**Summer ablation season**: Peak discharge carries maximum subglacial DIC export to downstream rivers, driven by surface melt and acceleration of subglacial water flow. The DIC concentration and radiocarbon age can vary through the melt season as different subglacial water sources become activated and as the efficiency of carbon-water contact varies.

**Winter baseflow**: Reduced discharge reflects diminished surface melt and increased contribution of groundwater and stored subglacial water. The DIC composition may shift toward more groundwater-influenced signatures with older radiocarbon ages.

**Event-driven discharge variation**: Outburst floods and sudden drainage of subglacial lakes produce pulses of discharge with distinct DIC signatures, occasionally carrying carbon that was stored and aged within subglacial systems.

This discharge-dependent variation creates a direct linkage between climate (controlling melt intensity) and the magnitude and isotopic composition of carbon exported from glaciated watersheds.

## Glacier-Fed River Carbon Dynamics

[[GlacierDerivedOrganicCarbon|Glacier-derived organic carbon]] supplements the DIC produced by subglacial weathering, contributing both fresh biospheric carbon (from recent vegetation and soil material entrained in ice) and potentially petrogenic carbon from ancient organic matter in exhumed bedrock. Glacial discharge therefore carries a complex mixture of inorganic and organic carbon with diverse radiocarbon ages, making glacier-fed rivers important vectors for transporting carbon and carbon-age signals to downstream systems and the ocean.

The coupling between glacial discharge and carbon export has profound implications:

- **Downstream river chemistry**: Glacial inputs modify the DIC concentration, isotopic composition, and radiocarbon age of river water downstream of glacier confluences, with effects propagating to estuaries and coastal zones.

- **Food web carbon sources**: Aquatic organisms in glacier-fed rivers consume carbon derived from subglacial weathering and glacial discharge, acquiring isotopic signatures that reflect glacial carbon sources. This [[AquaticFoodWebIntegrationAncientCarbon|food web integration of glacial carbon]] influences the isotopic composition of organisms and their predators.

- **Ocean carbon export**: In high-latitude regions with extensive glaciation (Greenland, Antarctica, coastal Alaska), glacial discharge is a major source of freshwater and DIC to coastal oceans, influencing coastal biogeochemistry and nutrient cycling. The radiocarbon age of glacially-derived DIC becomes an isotopic tracer of glacial contribution to coastal carbon budgets.

## Paleoceanographic Implications

The radiocarbon age of DIC exported from glaciated regions during past climate changes reflects the balance between subglacial weathering (producing young DIC) and mobilization of old groundwater and aged carbon sources. During glacial intervals with extensive ice sheets:

- **Expanded subglacial weathering area**: Larger ice sheets expand the area of subglacial weathering and increase the potential export of subglacially-produced DIC to the ocean.

- **Altered discharge regimes**: Changes in glacial discharge patterns (magnitude, seasonality, duration) alter the amount and isotopic composition of carbon exported.

- **Coupling to ocean radiocarbon**: The radiocarbon age of DIC exported to the ocean influences the ¹⁴C composition of dissolved inorganic carbon in coastal and potentially open-ocean water masses, creating a direct link between glacial extent and oceanographic radiocarbon signatures preserved in marine sediment cores.

Recognition of glacial carbon cycling as a distinct pathway expands our understanding of how ice sheet dynamics influence global carbon cycling and paleoceanographic proxies, particularly in records spanning deglaciations and periods of rapid ice sheet change.

## Analytical Approaches

Multiple complementary techniques reveal different aspects of glacial carbon cycling:

- [[RadiocarbonMethodsAquaticCO2Analysis|Direct radiocarbon measurement of evaded CO₂]] from glacier-fed streams captures the radiocarbon signature of carbon mobilized by subglacial weathering

- [[SubglacialInorganicCarbonChemistry|Dissolved inorganic carbon concentration and composition]] reflects the intensity of subglacial weathering and the water residence time in contact with bedrock

- [[StrontiumIsotopeVariabilityRivers|Strontium isotope ratios]] of glacial discharge trace the mixing between carbonate and silicate weathering, revealing the relative importance of different mineral dissolution pathways beneath glaciers

- Discharge-dependent sampling captures temporal variation in glacial carbon export linked to seasonal melt patterns and ice dynamics
