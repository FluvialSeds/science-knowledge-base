# Biogeochemical Nitrogen Cycling in Ancient Oceans

## Overview

The global [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycle]] is fundamental to ocean productivity and carbon sequestration, yet its expression and intensity have varied dramatically across Earth history in response to climate, ocean circulation, and the evolution of microbial metabolic pathways. This topic explores how nitrogen cycling processes operate in modern and ancient oceans, the microbial communities that drive nitrogen transformations, the biomarker signatures that preserve evidence of these processes in sediments, and how paleoceanographic records reveal the coupling between nitrogen cycle perturbations and large-scale ocean reorganizations.

## Core Processes

Three microbial processes form the backbone of the marine nitrogen cycle, each with distinct biogeochemical signatures and sensitivity to environmental conditions:

**Nitrification**: [[Nitrification|Aerobic oxidation of ammonia to nitrate]] by specialized bacteria (particularly *Nitrobacter*) in oxygen-rich waters. This process produces distinctive [[MethylhopanoidBiosynthesisNitrification|methylated hopanoid biomarkers]], particularly 2-methylhopanoids when vitamin B₁₂ is available. The abundance of these biomarkers in sediments indicates past nitrification activity and provides a window into nitrogen cycling intensity in paleoceanographic settings.

**Nitrogen Fixation**: [[NitrogenFixation|Biological conversion of atmospheric N₂ to bioavailable ammonia]] by diazotrophic cyanobacteria and archaea. This process is particularly important in nitrogen-limited environments and can create positive feedbacks where enhanced nitrogen limitation drives greater nitrogen fixation, potentially sustaining high productivity in otherwise oligotrophic waters.

**Anaerobic Ammonia Oxidation (Anammox)**: [[Anammox|Direct oxidation of ammonia by nitrite]] under anoxic conditions, producing nitrogen gas and removing fixed nitrogen from the ocean. This process produces characteristic [[BacteriohopanepolyolBiomarkers|35-aminobacteriohopanepolyol biomarkers]] and is particularly important in oxygen-minimum zones where it drives nitrogen loss and can reinforce nitrogen limitation.

## Paleoceanographic Applications

Paleoceanographic records reveal that during certain intervals—most notably during [[SapropelNitrogenCycleCoupling|Mediterranean sapropel deposition]]—the nitrogen cycle became tightly coupled to other aspects of ocean biogeochemistry. [[OceanicAnoxicEvents|Oceanic anoxic events (OAEs)]] and [[DeepWaterAnoxia|deep water anoxia]] fundamentally alter the balance between these three processes, creating distinctive geochemical and biomarker signatures that can be read in the geological record.

The evidence from both [[Elling-2020pnas|Elling et al. 2020]] and [[Elling-2021epsl|Elling et al. 2021]] demonstrates that:

1. **Biomarkers directly record past microbial activity**: The abundance and isotopic composition of [[MethylhopanoidBiosynthesisNitrification|methylhopanoids]] and [[BacteriohopanepolyolBiomarkers|aminobacteriohopanepolyols]] in sediments reflect the past activity of nitrifiers and anammox bacteria
2. **Nitrogen cycle perturbations are feedbacks, not forcing**: Enhanced nitrogen fixation during sapropel events is a response to nitrogen limitation created by [[Anammox|anammox-driven nitrogen loss]], creating reinforcing cycles
3. **Oxygen-redox coupling is critical**: The expansion of oxygen-minimum zones drives changes in which nitrogen-cycle processes dominate, fundamentally altering how nitrogen is cycled through ancient oceans

## Nitrogen Cycle-Climate Coupling

The nitrogen cycle is intimately connected to climate and carbon cycling. Enhanced [[Nitrification|nitrogen fixation]] can amplify the effects of ocean stratification by supporting diazotrophic cyanobacteria that form mats and modify local hydrography. Conversely, the expansion of oxygen-minimum zones driven by climate-induced stratification can amplify [[Anammox|anammox activity]] and nitrogen loss. These feedbacks mean that climate perturbations can trigger rapid shifts in the nitrogen cycle, with implications for long-term carbon sequestration and atmospheric CO₂.

Understanding how the nitrogen cycle has responded to past climate changes provides a framework for predicting how future ocean deoxygenation—driven by climate warming—will alter nitrogen transformations and ocean productivity.
