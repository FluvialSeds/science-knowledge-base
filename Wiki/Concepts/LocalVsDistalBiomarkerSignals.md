# Local vs Distal Biomarker Signals in River Sediments

Different plant-wax lipid classes integrate ecosystem information over different spatial and temporal scales, creating distinct local vs. catchment-integrated (distal) signals in fluvial sediments.

## Local Signals (Discharge-Responsive)

n-Alcohols and n-alkanoic acids respond rapidly to seasonal changes in river discharge, indicating they are derived from recently entrained local sources. These compounds are sensitive tracers for:
- Short-term hydrological variability
- Local vegetation changes
- Seasonal ecosystem dynamics
- Immediate catchment input

Carbon-normalized concentrations and relative abundances vary dramatically (19–76% of total plant-wax lipids) in response to runoff changes, demonstrating their high sensitivity to discharge seasonality.

## Distal Signals (Discharge-Insensitive)

n-Alkanes show no correlation with discharge and maintain low, stable relative abundances (5–16%), indicating they represent a catchment-integrated signal with minimal response to seasonal variability. This makes them better suited for:
- Long-term paleoclimate reconstructions
- Paleovegetation changes over geological timescales
- Integrating signals across entire river basins
- Filtering out short-term noise

## Temporal Stability

All individual plant-wax lipids show minimal temporal δ¹³C variability (<0.9‰) across seasonal to inter-annual timescales. This means that δ¹³C values are **not sensitive tracers for temporal changes in plant-wax source** in large river systems on short timescales, but the **relative concentrations and distributions** of different lipid classes are highly responsive to discharge.

## Implications for Paleoclimate Reconstruction

- **n-Alkanes**: Better choice for inferring past ecosystem composition and vegetation types
- **n-Alcohols and n-Alkanoic Acids**: May reflect local hydrological conditions rather than regional climate/vegetation
- **Multiple lipid classes**: Needed together to resolve the full spatial and temporal complexity of paleoenvironmental signals
