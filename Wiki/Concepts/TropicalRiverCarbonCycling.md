# Tropical River Carbon Cycling

## Core Concept

Tropical rivers export substantial quantities of organic and inorganic carbon to coastal waters and the atmosphere, with carbon sources and cycling pathways varying across river systems depending on watershed geology, vegetation, hydrology, and land use. Understanding tropical river carbon cycling is essential for quantifying the role of these systems in global carbon budgets and predicting how climate and hydrological change affect carbon transport and transformation.

## Basin-Scale Carbon Distribution

Large tropical river systems integrate carbon from diverse source areas spanning lowland floodplains, upland watersheds, and montane regions with differing geology and vegetation. The Tana River in East Africa and rivers in Papua New Guinea illustrate the complexity of tropical basin carbon cycling, with carbon distributed across particulate organic carbon (POC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) fractions. The proportion of carbon in each fraction varies longitudinally from headwaters to delta as a function of sediment transport, riverine productivity, and biogeochemical processing.

## Radiocarbon Characteristics

Tropical river carbon exhibits variable radiocarbon ages reflecting mixed sources and transformation pathways. Particulate organic carbon in tropical rivers often contains substantial proportions of aged carbon reflecting reworking of older terrestrial organic matter and geogenic sources. The ¹⁴C age of different carbon fractions (POC, DOC, DIC) and the spatial variability in ¹⁴C signatures provides diagnostic information about carbon source contributions, weathering regime, and the degree of biogeochemical alteration occurring within river systems.

## Effects of Sediment Transport and Resuspension

In tropical rivers characterized by high sediment loads, the suspension and resuspension of fine-grained sediments significantly influences carbon cycling. Sediment resuspension can increase water column turbidity, suppress aquatic productivity, and affect the composition and age of riverine carbon. Large seasonal variations in discharge and sediment transport create temporal dynamics in carbon distributions and cycling rates.

## Headwaters to Delta Continuum

Carbon cycling in tropical rivers exhibits systematic changes from headwaters to delta regions. Headwater streams dominated by erosional processes may export carbon with distinctive aged signatures from specific geologic formations. Moving downstream, floodplain inundation, macrophyte productivity, and microbial decomposition modify carbon distributions and ages. Near river mouths, estuarine mixing with seawater and coastal productivity further transform riverine carbon signatures.
