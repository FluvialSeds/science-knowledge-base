# Petrogenic Carbon Oxidation

## Core Concept

[[PetrogenicOrganicCarbon|Petrogenic organic carbon]] oxidation is the oxidative weathering of ancient organic matter locked in bedrock that is released during mountain erosion and soil formation. This process represents a significant source of atmospheric CO₂ that can partially or fully counteract CO₂ drawdown achieved through silicate weathering and burial of biospheric organic carbon.

## Process and Flux

In rapidly eroding mountain systems like Taiwan's Central Range (3–6 mm/year erosion rates), approximately 67 ± 11% of the petrogenic organic carbon present in exhumed bedrock is oxidized during hillslope soil formation. This oxidation produces CO₂ emissions of 6.1 to 18.6 metric tons of carbon per square kilometer per year—a substantial atmospheric release.

## Microbial Mediation

The remineralization of petrogenic organic carbon is microbially mediated, as shown through the molecular and isotopic evolution of organic carbon in soil profiles. Microbial communities oxidize this ancient carbon as they process newly exposed bedrock material, converting stable carbon compounds into dissolved and gaseous CO₂.

## Tectonic-Atmospheric Coupling

A critical finding is the strong coupling between erosion rate and petrogenic carbon oxidation—higher erosion drives greater CO₂ emissions. This relationship directly links tectonic activity (which drives erosion) to atmospheric CO₂ sources, suggesting that tectonically active mountain regions may act as net sources of CO₂ to the atmosphere rather than net sinks.

## Implications for Carbon Cycling

This work fundamentally changes our understanding of the global carbon cycle in rapidly eroding mountain systems. The oxidation of petrogenic carbon must be explicitly considered when:

- Modeling long-term climate regulation
- Assessing the net carbon balance of mountain-building regions
- Understanding how tectonic activity influences atmospheric CO₂ composition over geological timescales
