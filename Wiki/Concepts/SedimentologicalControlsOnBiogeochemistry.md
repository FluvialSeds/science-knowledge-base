# Sedimentological Controls on Long-Term Biogeochemical Cycles

## Core Concept

The geological and sedimentological characteristics of marine environments—particularly the area of flooded continental shelves and the flux of organic carbon and reactive iron to sediments—exert primary control on rates of biogeochemical processes that regulate atmospheric composition. Flooded shelf areas create extended oxygen-poor environments where anaerobic respiration dominates, enabling sulfate reduction and pyrite burial. Changes in flooded shelf area driven by sea-level variation and continental configuration thus directly modulate the rates of long-term biogeochemical cycles (particularly sulfur cycling and oxygen burial). This mechanism provides a quantitative framework linking tectonics, climate, and sea-level change to planetary atmospheric composition on million-to-billion-year timescales.

## Flooded Shelf Area as a Primary Control

**Definition:**
Flooded shelf area refers to the extent of shallow continental shelves that are submerged beneath seawater, creating broad expanses of sedimentary environments receiving organic carbon from coastal productivity.

**Why Shelves Matter:**
- High organic carbon delivery from coastal and upwelling productivity
- Shallow water column restricts oxygen penetration
- Poor water column mixing enables development of oxygen-minimum zones
- Sediment-water interface is often anoxic despite oxic surface waters
- Ideal conditions for anaerobic sulfate reduction and iron sulfide formation

**Quantitative Relationship:**
The diagenetic model shows that flooded shelf area is the dominant variable predicting:
- Pyrite formation rates across modern oceans
- Sulfur isotopic composition of buried pyrite
- Net sulfur burial flux
- By extension, long-term atmospheric oxygen production

## Organic Carbon and Reactive Iron as Secondary Controls

While flooded shelf area is primary, the model incorporates secondary controls that modulate biogeochemical rates:

**Organic Carbon Flux:**
- Higher productivity delivers more labile organic carbon to sediments
- Increased organic carbon fuels sulfate reduction (by providing electrons)
- At constant shelf area, higher productivity increases sulfate reduction rates and pyrite burial

**Reactive Iron Availability:**
- Dissolved iron (Fe²⁺) and iron oxides in sediments are required for pyrite formation
- Without sufficient iron, sulfide precipitates as FeS instead of FeS₂ (pyrite)
- Reactive iron comes from mineral weathering and delivery by rivers (iron oxide), diagenetic reduction of iron oxides, and hydrothermal inputs
- Limited reactive iron constrains pyrite burial rates despite abundant sulfate and organic carbon

**Sedimentation Rate:**
- Rapid sedimentation buries organic matter quickly, reducing water column oxidation
- Slow sedimentation exposes organic carbon to longer water column degradation
- Affects the timing and efficiency of anaerobic respiration in sediments

## The Phanerozoic Perspective: Sea Level and Atmospheric Oxygen

**Stratigraphic Record:**
Throughout the Phanerozoic (last 541 million years), flooded shelf area has varied dramatically:
- High sea level intervals (e.g., Cretaceous, Early Paleozoic) expanded shelf area and promoted pyrite burial
- Low sea level intervals (e.g., late Paleozoic glaciation, current interglacial) reduced shelf area and limited pyrite burial
- Model predictions of pyrite burial flux track with independent sea-level records

**Oxygen Implications:**
- Periods of expanded flooded shelves → increased pyrite burial → net oxygen accumulation → rising atmospheric pO₂
- Periods of contracted shelves → reduced pyrite burial → slower oxygen production
- Long-term atmospheric oxygen curves should correlate with sea-level records—a testable prediction

**Tectonics and Configuration:**
- Continental breakup and rifting alter continental shelf width and area
- Orogenic collisions create wide shelves in some regions and reduce them in others
- Mantle convection controls long-term sea level through mid-ocean ridge volume and spreading rates
- Thus, mantle dynamics → tectonics → shelf area → sulfur cycling → atmospheric composition

## Modern Anomaly: Why Burial Exceeds Oxidation Today

The diagenetic model reveals that modern pyrite burial (7.0 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹) exceeds terrestrial pyrite oxidation (~1–2 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹). This excess occurs because:

**Flooded Shelf Area is Expanded:**
- Current sea level is relatively high (interglacial)
- Extensive continental shelves are submerged globally
- Shelf area and sediment organic carbon flux favor high pyrite burial

**Reactive Iron is Abundant:**
- Tropical and subtropical weathering delivers iron oxides to coastal seas
- Coastal upwelling brings iron-rich subsurface waters to productive zones
- Diagenetic iron oxide reduction in sediments provides additional reactive iron

**Consequence:**
Earth's atmosphere is currently in net oxygen accumulation. This is not in steady state—atmospheric pO₂ is very slowly rising (on geological timescales). This excess production provides a buffer against future oxygen loss and indicates the biosphere is "producing more oxygen than can be consumed."

## Applications to Understanding Past and Future Changes

**Paleozoic Oxygenation:**
- The Cambrian-Ordovician expansion of animal phyla may have increased particle sinking efficiency
- Combined with high sea level in Early Paleozoic, expanded shelf area promoted pyrite burial
- Result: Rising atmospheric oxygen levels supporting aerobic life

**Mesozoic-Cenozoic Variability:**
- Sea-level fluctuations modulated sulfur cycling and oxygen production
- Regional tectonic events altered continental shelf configuration and productivity
- Paleoceanographic records of isotope composition reflect changes in pyrite burial rates

**Anthropogenic Future:**
- Warming-induced sea level rise will expand shelf area → increase pyrite burial → potential for accelerated oxygen production (on geological timescales)
- But on decadal-to-century timescales, ocean deoxygenation (caused by reduced mixing and temperature effects) reduces anoxic sediment area and may decrease pyrite burial
- The balance between these competing effects determines whether oxygen production accelerates or decelerates
