# Fatty Acid and Lignin Phenol Radiocarbon Biomarkers

## Core Concept

Fatty acids and lignin phenols are two of the most important organic biomarkers for paleoenvironmental reconstruction because they are ubiquitous, derive from specific biological sources, and can be isolated and radiocarbon-dated at the compound level. [[RadiocarbonOrganicMatter|Radiocarbon]] measurement of individual fatty acids (n-C₁₆, n-C₁₈) and lignin-derived phenols provides compound-specific organic matter ages that reveal: (1) how different components of soil and fluvial organic matter age at different rates; (2) whether carbon derives from recent photosynthesis or ancient geological sources; and (3) how terrestrial carbon export varies with climate and environmental conditions.

## Fatty Acids as Biomarkers

**Biological sources and ubiquity:**
Fatty acids (long-chain carboxylic acids with 8–30+ carbons) are fundamental components of all cellular membranes and lipid storage compounds. In terrestrial systems, fatty acids are synthesized by:

- **Plants**: Constitutive membrane lipids and plant wax components (abundant in leaves, cuticles)
- **Soil microorganisms**: Bacterial and fungal cell membranes (primary sources of C₁₆ and C₁₈ branched fatty acids)
- **Animals**: Body lipids and dietary reserves

**Common fatty acid biomarkers:**
- **n-C₁₆ (palmitic acid)** (C₁₆:₀): Universal in membranes; relatively labile
- **n-C₁₈ (stearic acid)** (C₁₈:₀): Abundant in plant waxes; intermediate lability
- **Branched iso- and anteiso-C₁₅**: Diagnostic for bacterial sources
- **Odd-chain lengths (C₁₅, C₁₇, C₁₉)**: Often enriched in soil microbial biomass

**Radiocarbon behavior:**
Fatty acids are relatively labile and degraded relatively rapidly by microbial β-oxidation. Consequently, radiocarbon ages of fatty acids typically reflect carbon cycling on decadal-to-centennial timescales:

- **In young, active soils**: Fatty acid Δ¹⁴C ≈ 0 to −100‰ (modern-to-young radiocarbon)
- **In old, weathered soils**: Fatty acid Δ¹⁴C ≈ −100 to −300‰ (showing tens to hundreds of years residence time)
- **In very old systems or petrogenic contributions**: Fatty acids can show older ages if from ancient sources

**River suspended sediment patterns:**
In large rivers, fatty acid radiocarbon ages respond to discharge-driven changes in sediment source:
- **High discharge**: Fresh hillslope erosion provides young fatty acids (Δ¹⁴C ≈ 0‰)
- **Low discharge**: Floodplain and deep groundwater contributions provide older fatty acids

## Lignin Phenols as Biomarkers

**Biological source and structure:**
Lignin is a highly polymeric, three-dimensional cross-linked biopolymer that comprises 10–30% of plant dry biomass. It is synthesized exclusively by vascular plants and serves as the structural backbone linking cellulose microfibrils in plant cell walls. Unlike fatty acids (produced by all organisms), lignin phenols are diagnostic for terrestrial plant sources and rare or absent in marine organic matter.

**Lignin phenol compositions:**
Lignin is composed of three primary monomeric precursors (coumaryl, coniferyl, and sinapyl alcohols), which after condensation and oxidation generate characteristic phenolic compounds:

- **Vanillyl phenols** (V: vanillin, vanillic acid, acetovanillone): Derived primarily from gymnosperms (softwoods)
- **Syringyl phenols** (S: syringaldehyde, syringic acid): Derived primarily from angiosperms (hardwoods)
- **Cinnamyl phenols** (C): Characteristic of grasses and non-woody plants

The ratio of V:S can thus distinguish gymnosperm vs. angiosperm sources, and the C contribution identifies grass-rich vegetation.

**Radiocarbon behavior:**
Lignin is much more resistant to microbial degradation than fatty acids because of its three-dimensional polymeric structure and chemical complexity. Consequently, lignin phenol radiocarbon ages typically reflect longer residence times than fatty acid ages in the same sample:

- **In soils**: Lignin Δ¹⁴C ≈ −200 to −500‰ (centuries to millennia residence time)
- **In river suspended sediment**: Lignin can be several hundred to 1,000+ years older than fatty acids from the same sample
- **In marine sediments**: Lignin radiocarbon ages constrain the time lag between terrestrial carbon production and ocean burial

**Interpretation as landscape-averaged ages:**
Because lignin is abundant and long-lived in soils, its radiocarbon age reflects an integrated, landscape-averaged residence time of carbon in the source region. This makes lignin radiocarbon ages particularly valuable for constraining paleoclimate (since residence time is climate-dependent).

## Multi-Biomarker Approach to Source and Age Inference

**Compound-specific radiocarbon patterns:**
When multiple biomarkers are radiocarbon-dated from the same sample, their relative ages reveal mixing relationships and decomposition selectivity:

**Simple case: Two-source mixing**
If a sample contains a mixture of young and old carbon, biomarkers may show bimodal age distributions:
- Young, labile biomarkers (fatty acids): Dominated by recent carbon
- Old, refractory biomarkers (lignin): Dominated by ancient carbon

This pattern indicates mixing between recently fixed and pre-aged sources.

**Climate inference:**
The offset between fatty acid and lignin radiocarbon ages in river suspended sediment scales with climate:
- **Warm climate**: Small offset (fatty acids and lignin both young) due to rapid decomposition
- **Cold climate**: Large offset (fatty acids young, lignin very old) due to selective preservation of refractory compounds

This relationship enables paleoclimate inference from ancient biomarker radiocarbon records.

## Applications and Case Studies

**Amazon River:**
Systematic analysis of fatty acid and lignin phenol radiocarbon ages across the Amazon river system shows:
- Modern fatty acids (Δ¹⁴C ≈ 0‰) indicating fresh vegetation supply
- Pre-aged lignin (Δ¹⁴C ≈ −200 to −400‰) indicating soil carbon ages of 2,000–4,000 years
- Systematic age decrease downstream, reflecting loss of labile fatty acids during transport and selective preservation of refractory lignin

**Arctic Rivers and Permafrost Carbon:**
Radiocarbon analysis of fatty acids and lignin in Arctic rivers reveals:
- Seasonal variation in biomarker ages (young in summer, old in spring when permafrost thaw occurs)
- Multi-thousand-year-old carbon mobilized during rapid permafrost thaw

## Limitations and Considerations

**Biosynthetic fractionation:**
There is minor isotope fractionation during synthesis of fatty acids and lignin that could theoretically bias radiocarbon ages by a few percent. However, studies show this effect is negligible (<5 years).

**Degradation versus aging:**
An increase in radiocarbon age during transport could reflect either time elapsed in storage (true aging) or preferential loss of young components through degradation. Integrating with other tracers (stable isotopes, specific biomarker abundance ratios) helps distinguish.

**Contamination:**
Both fatty acids and lignin phenols can be contaminated by modern sources during extraction and analysis. Careful procedural blank analysis is essential.
