# Carbon Cycle in Mountain Systems: CO₂ Sources and Sinks

## Core Concept

Mountain belts and rapidly eroding orogens are major regulators of atmospheric CO₂ and climate on geological timescales (million-year timeframes). They both consume and release atmospheric carbon through competing processes.

## CO₂ Drawdown (Sinks)

Mountains act as CO₂ sinks through:

1. **Silicate weathering**: [[ChemicalWeatheringDissolvedInorganicCarbon|Weathering of silicate minerals consumes atmospheric CO₂]] through the reaction of CO₂ with feldspar and other rock-forming minerals. This process increases with erosion rate. Both carbonic acid (from atmospheric CO₂) and sulfuric acid (from pyrite oxidation) drive weathering reactions that produce dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in riverine systems.

2. **Biospheric organic carbon burial**: Erosion exports organic carbon produced by living organisms to the ocean, where it is buried in sediments. This removes CO₂ from the atmosphere on geological timescales.

Both processes increase with erosion rate, making steep, rapidly eroding mountains important for global CO₂ drawdown.

## CO₂ Release (Sources)

Mountains also release CO₂ through:

1. **Oxidative weathering of sulfide minerals**: Oxidation of sulfides (coupled with carbonate dissolution) releases CO₂.

2. **Petrogenic organic carbon oxidation**: Oxidation of ancient, lithospheric organic carbon exhumed from bedrock releases CO₂ directly to the atmosphere.

## The Coupling: Erosion Drives Both Processes

Critically, erosion rate controls **both CO₂ consumption and CO₂ release**:
- Higher erosion → more silicate weathering and organic carbon burial (drawdown)
- Higher erosion → more petrogenic carbon oxidation (release)

In rapidly eroding systems like Taiwan's Central Range, petrogenic carbon oxidation (6.1–18.6 metric tons C/km²/year) is substantial enough to **partially or fully counteract** the CO₂ drawdown from silicate weathering and organic carbon burial.

## Implications for Climate and Tectonics

This tight coupling between erosion rate and both CO₂ drawdown and release means:
- **Tectonics drives erosion** → which affects atmospheric CO₂
- **Mountains are not simple CO₂ sinks** — they are complex systems with competing processes
- **Mountain building can be a net CO₂ source** in rapidly eroding regions, potentially having **negative feedback on long-term climate**
- Understanding the carbon cycle requires considering all processes: weathering, burial, AND [[MicrobialOxidationOrganicCarbon|oxidation of petrogenic carbon]]

This framework connects tectonics directly to climate regulation on geological timescales.
