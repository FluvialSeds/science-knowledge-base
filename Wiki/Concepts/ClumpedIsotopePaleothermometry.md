# Clumped Isotope Paleothermometry

Clumped isotope thermometry measures the extent to which heavy stable isotopes (¹³C and ¹⁸O) bond with each other in carbonate minerals, a phenomenon that depends on temperature during mineral formation. Because the degree of heavy-isotope clustering increases at lower temperatures, clumped isotope compositions directly record the temperature at which carbonate minerals crystallized. This approach provides a powerful paleothermometer for reconstructing past climate and thermal conditions from sedimentary and authigenic carbonate archives.

## Theoretical Basis

**Heavy isotope clustering:**
In carbonate minerals (CaCO₃), carbon can be present as both ¹²C and ¹³C, while oxygen can be present as ¹⁶O, ¹⁷O, and ¹⁸O. At equilibrium, heavy isotopes prefer to bond together (¹³C-¹⁸O bonds) rather than with light isotopes. This preference—called "clumping"—is thermodynamically favored and increases as temperature decreases.

**The Δ₄₇ parameter:**
Clumped isotope composition is reported as Δ₄₇, which quantifies the excess abundance of ¹³C-¹⁸O bonds relative to statistical (random) distribution. Higher Δ₄₇ values indicate lower formation temperatures, while lower Δ₄₇ values indicate higher temperatures.

**Temperature sensitivity:**
The relationship between Δ₄₇ and temperature follows well-characterized theoretical predictions:
- At 25°C (modern seawater): Δ₄₇ ≈ 0.70‰
- At 100°C: Δ₄₇ ≈ 0.40‰
- At 1000°C: Δ₄₇ ≈ 0.00‰

## Bond Reordering and Post-Depositional Alteration

**Solid-state reordering:**
After carbonate minerals form, heavy-isotope bonds can be broken and reformed (reordered) at elevated temperatures during burial, heating, or weathering. This reordering tends to increase temperature toward equilibrium values for the higher temperature, destroying or weakening the original paleoclimate signal.

**Activation energy model:**
Bond reordering can be described using kinetic models that account for:
- **Activation energies** (typically 30–60 kcal/mol): The energy barrier for bond breaking and reformation, varying with mineral type and crystal structure
- **Temperature**: Higher temperatures accelerate reordering
- **Time**: Longer exposure to elevated temperatures allows more reordering
- **Structural disorder**: More disordered crystal structures have lower activation energies and reorder more readily

**Implications for paleoclimate:**
A disordered kinetic model allows quantitative prediction of how original paleoclimate signals are modified by post-burial heating. For a carbonate sample with known thermal history, the model predicts how much of the original Δ₄₇ signal has been reset, enabling assessment of whether measured values preserve primary paleoclimate information or have been partially altered.

## Applications in Paleoclimate Reconstruction

**Sedimentary carbonates:**
Clumped isotope paleothermometry has been applied to:
- Marine sedimentary carbonates (foraminifera, bulk sediment) to reconstruct ocean temperatures
- Lacustrine carbonates to reconstruct paleoclimate in continental settings
- Paleosol carbonates to infer past soil temperatures and precipitation
- Diagenetic cements to assess burial history and thermal alteration

**Advantages over traditional paleothermometry:**
- **Absolute temperature**: Unlike δ¹⁸O paleothermometry, clumped isotopes do not require assumptions about the isotopic composition of ancient water
- **High sensitivity**: Small changes in temperature produce measurable changes in Δ₄₇
- **Multiple mineral phases**: Can be applied to various carbonate minerals and lithologies

**Limitations and considerations:**
- Carbonate samples must not have undergone extensive post-depositional reordering
- Interpretation requires knowledge of thermal history or independent constraints on temperature
- Kinetic models must be validated for specific mineral phases and conditions
- Sample selection is critical—pristine, unaltered material is essential

## Kinetic Framework for Interpreting Real Samples

**Predicting reordering:**
The disordered kinetic model enables researchers to:
1. Estimate activation energy distributions for a given carbonate phase
2. Predict Δ₄₇ modification for a known time-temperature path
3. Evaluate whether measured Δ₄₇ values reflect primary paleoclimate information
4. Optimize sample selection and analytical approaches

**Assessing paleoclimate fidelity:**
By comparing predicted Δ₄₇ values (after post-burial reordering) with observed values, researchers can determine whether a sample has preserved primary paleoclimate information or has been thermally reset. This assessment is critical for building robust paleoclimate records.
