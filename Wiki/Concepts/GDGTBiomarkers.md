# GDGT Biomarkers as Environmental Proxies

Glycerol dialkyl glycerol tetraethers (GDGTs) are lipid biomarkers produced by bacteria and archaea that preserve environmental information in sediments. Two major classes—branched (brGDGTs) and isoprenoid (isoGDGTs)—serve as proxies for paleoenvironmental conditions and organic matter provenance in marine and riverine systems.

## Branched GDGTs (brGDGTs)

Branched GDGTs are produced primarily by soil bacteria and are transported to aquatic environments through terrestrial runoff. Their molecular composition records environmental conditions in soils:

**Key Indices:**
- **MBT' (Methylation of Branched Tetraethers)**: Co-varies with temperature in soil systems
- **CBT (Cyclization of Branched Tetraethers)**: Co-varies with soil pH

These indices have been calibrated globally and are used to reconstruct paleoclimate and paleovegetation. In fluvial systems, brGDGTs serve as tracers for terrestrial input and catchment environmental conditions.

## Isoprenoid GDGTs (isoGDGTs)

Isoprenoid GDGTs are produced by marine archaea and some bacteria. Their relative abundance and distribution reflect:
- Water column temperature and depth
- Redox conditions
- Microbial productivity

In rivers, isoGDGTs can indicate marine influence and in-stream microbial processes, complementing other biomarker signals.

## Applications in Riverine Systems

In large river systems like the Congo River, GDGT distributions provide:
- **Provenance constraints**: brGDGTs track terrestrial vs. marine organic matter
- **Temporal variability**: GDGT composition responds to seasonal discharge and catchment hydrology
- **Multiple source discrimination**: Complementary to other biomarkers (plant-wax lipids, radiocarbon) for partitioning POM sources

The molecular composition of both brGDGTs and isoGDGTs allows distinction between organic matter derived from headwater sources versus material produced or modified during transit through lowland floodplains and swamps.
