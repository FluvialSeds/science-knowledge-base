# Pyrite Burial and Long-Term Atmospheric Oxygen Evolution

## Core Concept

Pyrite (FeS₂) formation and burial in marine sediments fundamentally regulates atmospheric oxygen levels through coupling to the sulfur cycle. When pyrite is buried in sediments, the sulfur is sequestered in a reduced form and cannot be oxidatively weathered on land. This has a critical consequence: atmospheric oxygen is not consumed in weathering the buried pyrite, and thus becomes enriched. Conversely, oxidative weathering of exposed pyrite on the continents consumes atmospheric oxygen. The balance between these two processes—anaerobic bacterial sulfate reduction and pyrite burial in sediments versus terrestrial oxidative weathering—determines whether Earth's atmosphere is accumulating or losing oxygen on geological timescales. This study demonstrates that the modern global pyrite burial flux substantially exceeds terrestrial pyrite oxidation, indicating Earth's atmosphere is currently in a state of **net oxygen accumulation driven by sedimentary sulfur burial**—a finding with profound implications for understanding long-term atmospheric composition and planetary habitability.

## The Sulfur Cycle and Atmospheric Oxygen

**Oxidative Weathering of Pyrite (consumes O₂):**
- Exposed pyrite on continents reacts with atmospheric O₂
- Chemical reaction: 2FeS₂ + 7O₂ + 2H₂O → 2FeSO₄ + 2H₂SO₄
- Weathering consumes atmospheric oxygen, lowering pO₂
- Sulfate (SO₄²⁻) produced is transported to the ocean

**Anaerobic Bacterial Sulfate Reduction and Pyrite Burial (produces O₂):**
- In anoxic marine sediments, bacteria reduce sulfate to sulfide using organic carbon as an electron donor
- Sulfide precipitates with iron to form pyrite (FeS₂)
- Pyrite burial sequesters sulfur in a reduced form, preventing future oxidative weathering
- Net effect: For every mole of pyrite buried, one mole of oxygen is "saved" (not consumed in future weathering)
- Equivalently, the organic carbon that would have been oxidized in weathering is instead buried, with net oxygen production

**Steady-State Assumption:**
Classical geochemical models assumed that over geological timescales, pyrite burial and oxidation were in steady state—equal rates. If this were true, Earth's atmospheric oxygen should be constant (or changing only slowly in response to organic carbon burial).

## The Modern Anomaly: Pyrite Burial > Oxidation

A non-dimensional diagenetic model was developed to predict pyrite formation rates and sulfur isotopic compositions (δ³⁴S) using globally-interpolated boundary conditions:

**Model Inputs:**
- Organic carbon flux and reactivity
- Sulfate availability
- Sedimentation rates
- Reactive iron delivery
- Flooded shelf area

**Key Result:**
The global pyrite burial flux (7.0 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹) substantially exceeds terrestrial pyrite oxidation estimates (~1–2 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹). This means:
- The sulfur cycle is **not in steady state**
- More sulfur is being buried in sediments than is being oxidatively weathered
- Net atmospheric oxygen accumulation is occurring at rates of ~1–3 × 10¹² mol O₂ yr⁻¹

**Sensitivity and Uncertainty:**
- Pyrite burial flux range: 2.5 × 10¹² to 19.0 × 10¹² mol S yr⁻¹ (reflects uncertainties in reactive iron availability and sedimentation rates)
- Weighted-average δ³⁴S of buried pyrite: −4‰ (range: −8 to +3‰)
- Model validation: Predictions agree with observations from 216 sediment cores across diverse oceanic environments

## Controls on Pyrite Burial: Flooded Shelf Area

The diagenetic model identifies **flooded shelf area as the primary control** on modern pyrite burial. This makes mechanistic sense:

**Flooded Shelves as Oxygen-Poor Environments:**
- Shallow continental shelves receive high organic carbon flux (from coastal productivity)
- Limited water column mixing creates oxygen-poor (or anoxic) conditions at the sediment-water interface
- High organic carbon + low oxygen = ideal conditions for sulfate reduction and pyrite formation

**Phanerozoic Implications:**
- Throughout the last 541 million years, flooded shelf area varies with sea level and continental configuration
- Periods of high sea level and extensive flooded shelves (e.g., Cretaceous, Early Paleozoic) should show enhanced pyrite burial and net oxygen accumulation
- Periods of low sea level (e.g., late Paleozoic glaciation) should show reduced pyrite burial and slower oxygen accumulation or even oxygen loss

This framework provides a mechanistic explanation for paleoceanographic records of oxygen variation throughout Earth's history.

## Implications for Atmospheric Oxygen Evolution

**Current State:**
Earth's atmosphere is currently in a state of net oxygen accumulation. This has consequences:
- Atmospheric pO₂ is slowly increasing (on geological timescales)
- The biosphere is in a net reducing state (more reduced carbon burial than oxidative weathering)
- Sedimentological conditions controlling organic carbon and reactive iron delivery are critical determinants of planetary atmospheric composition

**Climate-Carbon Cycle Feedback:**
- Warming-induced changes in coastal productivity and anoxia could alter pyrite burial rates
- Ocean deoxygenation could expand anoxic sediment environments, increasing pyrite burial
- Changes in sea level and continental configuration (on million-year timescales) alter flooded shelf area and pyrite burial capacity
- These processes represent slow but powerful feedbacks linking tectonics, climate, and atmospheric composition

**Long-Term Habitability:**
- The excess pyrite burial indicates that Earth's biosphere is currently producing more oxygen-rich organic carbon than weathering can consume
- This "surplus" oxygen production is critical for maintaining high atmospheric pO₂
- If pyrite burial were to dramatically decrease (e.g., from ocean-wide anoxia preventing sulfate reduction), atmospheric oxygen could decline, with profound consequences for aerobic life

## Sulfur Isotope Constraints

The δ³⁴S composition of buried pyrite provides an independent check on pyrite formation rates and mechanisms:
- Microbial sulfate reduction produces large fractionations (Δ³⁴S = +40 to +70‰)
- The δ³⁴S of buried pyrite (−4‰ weighted average) reflects mixing of sulfate reduction products with minor contributions from other sulfur pathways
- Deviations from predictions in specific regions reveal local variations in sulfate reduction intensity and pyrite formation mechanisms
