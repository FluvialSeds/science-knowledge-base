# Glacier-Derived Organic Carbon

## Core Concept

Mountain glaciers store and release dissolved organic matter (DOM) with distinct chemical and isotopic properties that reflect subglacial weathering, microbial activity, and organic matter preservation at depth. Glacier-derived DOM is enriched in aliphatic and peptide-like compounds—highly bioavailable forms that are rapidly respired when exported to river networks, contributing significantly to aquatic CO₂ emissions in glacier-fed systems.

## Glacier DOM Characteristics

**Compositional signature:**
Glacier-derived DOM is dominated by:
- Aliphatic compounds (saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons)
- Peptide-like compounds (proteinaceous material)
- Simple, labile organic molecules

This bioavailable composition contrasts sharply with soil-derived DOM, which is enriched in recalcitrant phenolic and condensed aromatic compounds.

**Isotopic signature:**
- Modern or near-modern radiocarbon ages (Δ¹⁴C close to 0‰)
- Reflects recent organic production or shallow soil inputs in glaciated headwaters
- Distinct from pre-aged, soil-derived DOM (Δ¹⁴C << 0‰)

**Bioavailability:**
Glacier DOM shows the highest fraction of bioavailable dissolved organic carbon (BDOC) among different sources, meaning a large fraction is rapidly respired by aquatic microorganisms upon export to rivers.

## Controls on DOM Export

**Glacier melt discharge:**
DOM concentrations in glacier-fed rivers vary seasonally with meltwater flux:
- **High discharge (monsoon/warm season):** High glacier melt dilutes DOM but exports large total loads due to increased water volume
- **Low discharge (winter/dry season):** DOM concentrations increase but total export decreases

The relative contribution of glacier-derived DOM to total river DOM depends on glacier coverage (% glaciated catchment area) and meltwater contribution to total discharge.

## Spatial Variability in Glacier-Fed Basins

In the Upper Ganges Basin (Western Himalaya), DOM composition changes dramatically downstream as glacier-derived inputs are mixed with soil and vegetation-derived DOM:

**Headwaters (>50% glacier coverage):**
- Aliphatic and peptide-like compounds dominant
- High bioavailability
- Modern radiocarbon ages

**Downstream reaches (lower glacier coverage):**
- Progressive increase in soil-derived phenolic and condensed aromatic compounds
- Lower bioavailability
- Older radiocarbon ages from soil organic matter
- Soils contribute progressively more DOM as the river integrates tributaries draining non-glaciated areas

## Seasonal Variability

Monsoon-driven precipitation and glacier melt create strong seasonal signals:

**Pre-monsoon season:**
- Declining glacier melt but high snowmelt
- Aliphatic-rich, bioavailable glacier DOM still prominent
- Begins mixing with soil-leaching signal as precipitation increases

**Monsoon season:**
- Peak glacier discharge from snowmelt accumulation
- Aliphatic and peptide-rich DOM maximum
- High total DOC export despite some dilution

**Post-monsoon season:**
- Declining discharge transitions toward baseflow
- Shift toward soil-derived, aromatic-rich, less bioavailable DOM
- This shift reflects reduced glacier input and increasing importance of soil leaching

## Implications for Carbon Cycling

**Respiration and CO₂ emissions:**
The high bioavailability of glacier-derived DOM means that its export to rivers drives significant heterotrophic respiration and aquatic CO₂ emissions. In glacier-fed rivers, a large fraction of exported DOM is respired within the fluvial network before reaching the coast.

**Climate change vulnerability:**
Glacier-derived DOM export is highly sensitive to climate-driven changes in glacier mass balance:

- **Short-term (decades):** Increased glacier melt will increase export of bioavailable DOM, increasing aquatic respiration rates and CO₂ emissions
- **Long-term (centuries):** Glacier mass loss and reduced meltwater will decrease bioavailable DOM export, potentially reducing aquatic respiration, but shifting toward soil-derived, less reactive DOM
