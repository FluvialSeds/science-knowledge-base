# Chemical Weathering and Dissolved Inorganic Carbon

## Core Concept

Chemical weathering of silicate and carbonate rocks is a fundamental process generating dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in rivers and groundwaters. Different weathering pathways (sulfuric acid, carbonic acid, organic acid pathways) produce DIC with distinctive chemical compositions and radiocarbon signatures, providing diagnostic indicators of weathering regime, geochemistry, and long-term carbon cycle controls on atmospheric CO₂ concentrations.

## Weathering Pathways and DIC Sources

Dissolved inorganic carbon produced by rock weathering originates from three potential sources: (1) atmospheric CO₂ dissolved in rainwater and soil water; (2) soil CO₂ from root respiration and organic matter decomposition; (3) geogenic carbonate minerals in the weathered rock. Different weathering pathways preferentially mobilize these carbon sources. Carbonic acid weathering of silicates and carbonates primarily uses atmospheric and soil CO₂. Sulfuric acid weathering, derived from pyrite oxidation, can weather both carbonates and silicates, producing DIC enriched in geogenic carbon. Organic acid weathering, mediated by organic matter in soils and sediments, combines soil and atmospheric carbon sources.

## Pyrite-Derived Sulfuric Acid Weathering

In rapidly eroding mountain belts with significant pyrite content, sulfuric acid-mediated weathering represents a major pathway for rock dissolution and DIC production. Pyrite oxidation generates sulfuric acid that can weather both carbonate minerals (producing CO₂ release) and silicate minerals (consuming CO₂ on geological timescales). The balance between CO₂ production (from carbonate weathering) and CO₂ consumption (from silicate weathering) determines whether rapidly weathering orogens act as net CO₂ sources or sinks. Radiocarbon analysis of DIC reveals the contribution of geogenic carbon from carbonate minerals, distinguishing it from atmospheric and biogenic sources.

## Radiocarbon Signatures as Weathering Indicators

Radiocarbon measurements of dissolved inorganic carbon provide powerful constraints on weathering regimes. Waters characterized by very old ¹⁴C-DIC (e.g., <50% modern) indicate substantial contributions from geogenic carbonates lacking radiocarbon, typical of carbonate-weathering dominated systems. More modern ¹⁴C-DIC (closer to atmospheric levels) suggests dominance of atmospheric and soil CO₂ sources in weathering reactions. The ¹⁴C signature of DIC thus serves as a tracer of weathering pathway importance and helps quantify the partitioning between different carbon sources contributing to riverine DIC.

## Global Carbon Cycle Implications

Over geological timescales, chemical weathering and carbonate burial represent dominant processes governing atmospheric CO₂ concentrations. Silicate weathering consumes atmospheric CO₂, providing a long-term negative feedback on climate change. Carbonate weathering releases CO₂ to the atmosphere, but this CO₂ can be resequestered when carbonate-derived DIC is buried in marine sediments. The net effect of weathering on atmospheric CO₂ depends on the relative rates of silicate versus carbonate weathering and the burial efficiency of weathering products. Rapid erosion in mountains can accelerate both processes, influencing carbon cycle dynamics over millennial to million-year timescales.
