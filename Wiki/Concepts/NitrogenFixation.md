# Nitrogen Fixation

## Core Concept

 is the biological conversion of atmospheric nitrogen gas (N₂) into bioavailable ammonia (NH₃), making atmospheric nitrogen accessible to other organisms. This process is catalyzed exclusively by specialized microorganisms (bacteria and archaea) that possess the nitrogenase enzyme complex.  is the primary input of new bioavailable nitrogen to most ecosystems and is essential for primary productivity in nitrogen-limited environments.

## The Nitrogenase Reaction

The nitrogenase enzyme catalyzes the reduction of N₂ to ammonia:

N₂ + 8H⁺ + 8e⁻ → 2NH₃ + H₂

This reaction is energetically expensive, requiring 16 ATP molecules per N₂ molecule fixed. Nitrogenase also produces hydrogen gas (H₂) as a byproduct and is irreversibly inhibited by oxygen, requiring either anaerobic conditions or special protective mechanisms in aerobic organisms.

## Diazotrophic Communities

Organisms capable of nitrogen fixation (diazotrophs) include:

**Cyanobacteria**: Photosynthetic bacteria that fix nitrogen while conducting photosynthesis
- Filamentous cyanobacteria with specialized heterocysts that protect nitrogenase from oxygen
- Unicellular cyanobacteria using oxygen-protective mechanisms

**Heterotrophic bacteria and archaea**: Fix nitrogen while consuming organic matter
- Free-living soil and aquatic bacteria (e.g., *Azotobacter*)
- Symbiotic bacteria in plant root nodules (e.g., *Rhizobium*)

**Archaeal methanogens and other archaea**: Some thermophilic and marine archaea fix nitrogen

In marine systems, cyanobacteria (particularly *Trichodesmium*) are responsible for most open-ocean , while in terrestrial systems, symbiotic and free-living bacteria dominate.

## Environmental Triggers

 Nitrogen fixation rates increase when:
- **Nitrogen is limiting**: Low bioavailable nitrogen (ammonia and nitrate) stimulates nitrogen fixers
- **Phosphorus is available**: Phosphorus often co-limits nitrogen fixation rates
- **Iron is accessible**: Iron is required for nitrogenase function; iron limitation suppresses fixation
- **Oxygen levels are suitable**: Varies by organism; some fix nitrogen under anoxia, others require protective mechanisms

## Paleoceanographic Significance

In paleoceanographic records, nitrogen fixation is inferred from:
- **Nitrogen isotope ratios**: Nitrogen-fixing organisms deplete ¹⁵N, leaving distinctive δ¹⁵N signatures
- **Biomarker distributions**: Cyanobacterial and diazotrophic lipid biomarkers indicate fixation activity
- **Stoichiometry of organic matter**: The ratio of carbon to nitrogen (C:N) reflects whether nitrogen fixation has supplemented other nitrogen sources

Enhanced  during past anoxic events (like [[SapropelNitrogenCycleCoupling|Mediterranean sapropel formation]]) suggests nutrient limitation and stratification-driven coupling between nitrogen fixation and water column anoxia.

## Modern and Paleoceanographic Implications

 Nitrogen fixation is sensitive to climate and oceanographic changes:
- Warming oceans favor some nitrogen fixers over others
- Changes in iron delivery (e.g., from dust or upwelling) directly alter fixation rates
- Anoxia expansion can suppress oxygen-sensitive fixers while favoring anaerobic diazotrophs
- Nitrogen fixation provides a negative feedback to nitrogen loss: if denitrification and [[Anammox|anammox]] remove too much nitrogen, nitrogen limitation stimulates fixation, replenishing the pool

Understanding how past  responded to climate and oceanographic changes helps predict how marine productivity will respond to future environmental changes.
