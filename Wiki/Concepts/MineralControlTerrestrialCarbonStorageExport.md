# Mineral control on terrestrial carbon storage and export

## Core concept

Terrestrial soil carbon cycling is fundamentally controlled by mineral-soil interactions. Compound-specific radiocarbon analysis of multiple biomarker classes in global river sediments reveals that radiocarbon ages of soil-derived microbial lipids (glycerol dialkyl glycerol tetraethers, GDGTs) are consistently older than higher plant biomarkers (lignin phenols, fatty acids), indicating that microbial organic matter experiences extended residence in soils before riverine export. This differential aging pattern is explained by the association of microbial lipids with mineral phases during soil formation—a process of mineral-mediated stabilization that extends organic matter residence time and fundamentally controls how much terrestrial carbon is stored, how long it persists, and what signals it carries to the ocean.

## GDGT radiocarbon ages as soil carbon tracers

Glycerol dialkyl glycerol tetraethers are bacterial and archaeal membrane lipids ubiquitously produced in soils and exported by rivers. Unlike higher plant biomarkers that are produced at the surface and buried with minimal intermediate processing, GDGTs undergo significant soil residence before export. Analysis of GDGT ¹⁴C ages across eight global river systems reveals:

- **GDGT ¹⁴C ages typically older than plant biomarkers**: In most systems (except Amazon and Cagayan rivers), GDGT ¹⁴C values are significantly depleted (older) compared to corresponding lignin and fatty acid ages, indicating 100s to 1000s of years of additional soil storage
- **Correlation with soil radiocarbon age**: GDGT ¹⁴C values show statistically significant correlation with topsoil (0–30 cm) and soil ¹⁴C age, confirming that GDGTs reflect active soil carbon pools
- **Systematic offset between biomarker classes**: The consistent offset between GDGT and plant biomarker radiocarbon ages indicates different mobilization pathways through soil systems

## Mineral protection as the preservation mechanism

The longer soil residence time reflected in GDGT ¹⁴C ages is not due to inherent recalcitrance of microbial lipids (which are structurally simpler than some plant-derived compounds) but rather to their **association with mineral phases**. Multiple lines of evidence support mineral-controlled stabilization:

1. **Size requirements and extraction protocols**: GDGTs are successfully extracted from mineral-bound soil fractions using aggressive extraction procedures, indicating strong association with minerals

2. **Comparison across biomarker classes**: Biomarkers that preferentially associate with mineral phases (GDGTs, which are microbial and thus intimately mixed with soil minerals) show older ¹⁴C ages than plant waxes that are deposited as discrete plant litter at the soil surface and incorporated more gradually into mineral associations

3. **Correlation structure with catchment properties**: GDGT ¹⁴C values correlate strongly with soil organic carbon content and topsoil ¹⁴C age—exactly what would be expected if GDGTs reflect mineral-stabilized soil carbon pools that turn over on millennial timescales

## Export patterns reflect mineral-mediated partitioning

Rivers export organic carbon derived from multiple sources (fresh plant litter, soil organic matter, petrogenic carbon) that have different residence times and mineral associations. The radiocarbon age offset between GDGTs (soil-derived, mineral-stabilized) and higher plant biomarkers (litter-derived, more labile) means that:

- **Young exported carbon** (low ¹⁴C depletion) primarily reflects recent plant productivity and short-term litter cycling
- **Older exported carbon** (high ¹⁴C depletion) reflects mineral-stabilized soil carbon mobilized by erosion
- **The ratio of young to old carbon** encodes information about the balance between litter production and soil carbon mobilization in the catchment

In systems with high erosion rates, mineral-stabilized soil carbon comprises a larger fraction of exported organic matter. In systems with low erosion, litter-derived carbon dominates. This partitioning can be quantified using radiocarbon age offsets between biomarker classes.

## Implications for terrestrial carbon budgets

Mineral-mediated stabilization fundamentally alters how much carbon is stored in soils and how long it persists:

- **Carbon storage capacity**: Mineral surface area and clay mineral abundance directly control how much organic carbon can be stabilized in soils. Soils in high-erosion mountain systems continuously expose fresh mineral surfaces via weathering, providing additional mineral-binding sites and increasing carbon storage capacity
- **Residence time control**: The association with minerals extends carbon residence time from 10s-100s of years (litter-derived, labile carbon) to 1000s-10,000s of years (mineral-stabilized carbon)
- **Climate sensitivity**: Climate-driven changes in erosion rates, precipitation, or vegetation productivity alter the balance between mineral surface exposure and organic matter inputs, directly affecting soil carbon storage
- **Paleoclimate applications**: The ¹⁴C age offset between biomarker classes reflects soil carbon turnover rates controlled by climate; this offset changes predictably with temperature and precipitation changes, enabling paleoclimate reconstruction from compound-specific radiocarbon data
