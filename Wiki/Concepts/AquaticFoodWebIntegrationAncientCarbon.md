# Aquatic Food Web Integration of Ancient Carbon

## Core Concept

Ancient organic carbon derived from subsurface reservoirs (groundwater, permafrost, deep peat) can be integrated into contemporary aquatic food webs through microbial pathways and trophic transfer, resulting in aquatic consumers—including fish, invertebrates, and zooplankton—that bioaccumulate apparent radiocarbon ages of centuries to thousands of years. This integration of old carbon into living organisms has profound implications for ecosystem function, radiocarbon dating of archaeological materials, and understanding climate-driven changes in aquatic carbon cycling and productivity.

## Bioaccumulation Pathways

Ancient carbon enters aquatic food webs through multiple overlapping pathways:

**Microbial mineralization and biosynthesis:**
- Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) from subsurface sources is metabolized by bacteria and other microorganisms
- A fraction of the consumed carbon is incorporated into microbial biomass rather than fully respired to CO₂
- Microbial biomass retains the radiocarbon age of consumed substrate, creating a "aged" microbial carbon pool

**Bacterial and algal consumption:**
- Algae and other primary producers consume dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) from respiring microbes and geogenic sources
- Biofilms and algae that feed on microbial communities bioaccumulate the radiocarbon signature of their substrates
- Primary producer ages reflect weighted average of all DIC sources, including aged components

**Trophic transfer:**
- Zooplankton consume bacteria and algae, incorporating microbial and phytoplankton biomass into their tissues
- Fish and macroinvertebrates consume these intermediate trophic levels, further bioaccumulating aged carbon
- Trophic transfer does not fractionate radiocarbon (unlike stable isotopes), so food web animals retain the radiocarbon signature of their diet

## Extreme Ancient Carbon Integration

In groundwater-fed aquatic systems (Great Artesian Basin, Australia):
- **Water column:** Δ¹⁴C indicates 99.58% old carbon, with integrated age ~11,000 years
- **Algae:** 82% derived from old groundwater carbon, retaining substantial radiocarbon depletion
- **Fish:** Despite lower fractional contribution (52%), fish reaching 11,000-year apparent ages represent the oldest-known living vertebrates based on radiocarbon
- **Food chain implication:** Ancient carbon sources are preferentially bioaccumulated despite not being the dominant carbon pool, suggesting either (1) selective utilization of specific bioavailable compounds or (2) selective retention of old carbon in consumer biomass

## Arctic Food Web Ancient Carbon Integration

In Arctic streams receiving permafrost-derived carbon:
- **Biofilms:** Δ¹⁴C ages of 5,255–265 yr BP reflect direct consumption of permafrost DOC
- **Macroinvertebrates:** Ages of 4,490 yr BP to modern show mixing of ancient and modern carbon sources through feeding
- **Fish:** Apparent ages of 3,195 yr BP to modern, integrating both ancient permafrost carbon and modern in-stream production
- **Functional consequence:** Higher old carbon assimilation correlates inversely with fish growth rate and condition index, indicating ecosystem-level impacts of ancient carbon integration

The food web age gradient (declining from biofilms through macroinvertebrates to fish) likely reflects: (1) differential feeding on aged versus modern carbon sources at different trophic levels, (2) selective growth on modern versus aged carbon pools, and (3) consumer metabolic routing of old versus new carbon into different tissue compartments.

## Implications for Radiocarbon Dating

Ancient carbon integration into aquatic food webs has profound implications for archaeological radiocarbon dating:

**Reservoir effect complications:**
- Fish bones used for dating in groundwater-fed systems can yield apparent ages 5,000–11,000 years "too old" due to consumption of ancient groundwater carbon
- Arctic fauna consuming permafrost carbon can yield apparent ages 2,000–5,000 years "too old"
- Standard freshwater reservoir effect corrections may be insufficient if multiple ancient carbon sources are utilized

**Taxonomic variation:**
- Aquatic consumers will show systematically older apparent ages than terrestrial organisms from the same time period
- Within-food-web age gradients (biofilms oldest, fish younger) can create age inversions in stratigraphic sequences if not recognized

**Chronological implications:**
- Archaeological sites dependent on fish bone dating in regions with groundwater-fed or permafrost-affected aquatic systems may require substantial chronological revision
- Recognition of ancient carbon integration enables use of radiocarbon ages as tracers of subsurface carbon mobilization and food web structure

## Climate Change Trajectory

As climate warming accelerates permafrost thaw and alters groundwater dynamics:
- Ancient carbon mobilization to aquatic systems will increase substantially
- Food web ages will shift toward older apparent ages as more permafrost carbon is mobilized
- Ecosystem productivity may decline as consumers increase their reliance on aged carbon with potentially lower nutritional value
- Aquatic-based food webs in regions underlain by substantial permafrost or served by ancient aquifers will become increasingly "aged" in radiocarbon terms, creating complex chronological challenges for paleoclimate and archaeological records
