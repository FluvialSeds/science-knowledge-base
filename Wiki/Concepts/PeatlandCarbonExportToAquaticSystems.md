# Peatland Carbon Export to Aquatic Systems

## Core Concept

Peatlands represent globally significant carbon reservoirs, with substantial portions of stored carbon mobilized and exported to aquatic systems through multiple pathways. Export occurs as dissolved organic carbon (DOC), dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), and dissolved/gaseous CO₂, with different carbon fractions exhibiting distinct ages and biogeochemical reactivity. Understanding peatland-aquatic carbon linkages is critical for predicting how climate change and hydrological disturbance affect carbon cycling and atmospheric CO₂ concentrations.

## Multiple Carbon Export Pathways

Peatlands export carbon to aquatic systems via lateral (downstream) and vertical (atmospheric evasion) pathways. DOC is transported laterally through drainage streams, while CO₂ is lost to the atmosphere through both direct evasion and indirect pathways involving groundwater discharge. The relative importance of DOC versus CO₂ export varies with peatland hydrology, vegetation, and climate. Seasonal variations in water table position and discharge strongly influence the magnitude and composition of carbon export.

## Age and Source Heterogeneity

A defining characteristic of peatland carbon export is the marked age difference between DOC and CO₂ pools. In peatland systems, DOC is consistently young (modern to ~200 years BP), reflecting decomposition of recently fixed vegetation. In contrast, CO₂ lost by evasion is much older (geogenic and old peat-derived, ranging to ~1,450 years BP), indicating mobilization of deep peat carbon pools and geogenic DIC from carbonate weathering. This age contrast reflects different subsurface flow pathways and depth-dependent peat carbon reactivity.

## Subsurface Connectivity and Carbon Variability

Peatland pipes and other subsurface macropore flow pathways create direct connectivity between surface and deep peat layers, enabling transport of carbon with highly variable radiocarbon ages. The variable carbon age mobilized through pipes suggests mixing of water from different peat strata with distinct carbon residence times. Seasonal snowmelt and high discharge events mobilize carbon from different subsurface compartments, creating temporal variability in the age and source composition of exported carbon.

## Boreal Peatland Dynamics

In boreal regions, peatland carbon export is strongly influenced by seasonal hydrology. Spring snowmelt creates high discharge that mobilizes surface and near-surface peat carbon, while low flow periods may preferentially access deeper carbon pools. Antecedent moisture conditions influence both within-site and between-site differences in the radiocarbon age of exported carbon, suggesting that climate-driven changes in precipitation and water table depth will alter peatland carbon export characteristics.

## Climate Change Implications

Climate change-driven destabilization of peatlands through permafrost thaw, drainage, or shifting water tables may preferentially mobilize old carbon pools to aquatic systems and the atmosphere. If exported carbon is released as CO₂ rather than being buried or transported as DOC, this could amplify climate feedback mechanisms. Conversely, if enhanced peatland productivity increases recent carbon export, feedbacks may be attenuated. Quantifying both the magnitude and radiocarbon age of exported carbon is essential for predicting carbon cycle responses to future environmental change.
