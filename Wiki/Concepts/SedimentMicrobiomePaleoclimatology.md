# Sediment microbiome paleoclimatology

## Core concept

Microbial communities in marine sediments are shaped primarily by post-depositional diagenetic processes—the sequential depletion of energy-yielding electron acceptors (oxygen → nitrate → sulfate → CO₂) that controls which microbial metabolic pathways remain energetically favorable at different sediment depths. However, metagenomic analysis of sediments underlying the Arabian Sea oxygen minimum zone reveals that 5–15% of microbial taxa exhibit paleoenvironmental selection patterns that transcend post-burial diagenetic controls. These taxa cluster based on the **depositional conditions** that existed when sediments were first buried rather than their current depth-dependent diagenetic environment. The genomic potential for specific metabolic pathways (particularly denitrification) correlates with independent paleoclimate proxies indicating oxygen minimum zone conditions, independent of present-day pore water composition. This demonstrates that sedimentary microbiomes preserve a "paleomicrobiome" signal reflecting past oceanographic conditions, opening new avenues for paleoclimate reconstruction using ancient sedimentary DNA.

## Diagenetic versus paleoenvironmental microbial selection

**Post-burial diagenetic selection:**

The dominant control on sedimentary microbial communities is the sequence of electron acceptor depletion. As sediment is buried, oxygen is consumed, causing sulfate-reducing bacteria to dominate deeper zones where sulfate is still available. Below the sulfate-oxygen interface, methanogens prevail. This energetically-driven selection creates predictable vertical profiles of microbial taxa and is independent of paleoenvironmental history.

**Paleoenvironmental selection (novel finding):**

Surprisingly, analysis of sedimentary metagenomic DNA from the Arabian Sea reveals that certain microbial taxa show **depth-independent stratigraphic patterns** where:

- Taxa deposited during oxygen minimum zone (OMZ) conditions cluster together across multiple stratigraphic intervals, even though they are separated in the vertical sediment profile
- Taxa from intervening bioturbated intervals (deposited under higher-oxygen conditions) form a distinct microbial community
- The taxonomic composition correlates with **paleoclimate proxies** (such as authigenic mineral assemblages, biomarker indicators, or oxygen isotope records) that independently indicate depositional redox conditions

This indicates that paleoceanographic conditions during deposition **select for specific microbial taxa** that are adapted to those conditions and that this selection signal persists despite post-burial diagenesis that would theoretically homogenize communities based on electron acceptor availability.

## Paleoproxy signals from denitrification genes

The genomic potential for denitrification (genes for sequential reduction: NO₃⁻ → NO₂⁻ → NO → N₂O → N₂) is encoded in the metagenomic DNA. Analysis shows:

1. **Correlation with OMZ proxies**: Denitrification gene abundance correlates significantly with independent paleoclimate proxies indicating OMZ deposition, even after controlling for sediment depth
2. **Independence from modern pore water composition**: Modern pore water nitrate and nitrite concentrations do not predict denitrification gene abundance
3. **Conservation in fossil taxa**: The denitrification signal persists as a genomic signal despite millions of years of burial

This indicates that during periods of OMZ deposition, denitrifying bacteria were preferentially selected (because denitrification was an advantageous respiration pathway under those conditions). After burial, even though denitrifiers no longer have access to their preferred substrate (nitrate), their DNA persists, carrying a paleoenvironmental signature.

## Fermentation pathways and paleoceanographic adaptation

A key finding is that many denitrifiers encode **mixed fermentation pathways** (acid and Entner-Dourdoroff) alongside denitrification genes. This dual metabolic capability enables facultatively anaerobic bacteria to survive extended burial in anoxic sediments:

- **During deposition under OMZ conditions**: Denitrifiers have abundant NO₃⁻ and NO₂⁻, enabling denitrification as a highly favorable respiration pathway
- **After burial in anoxic sediments**: NO₃⁻ and NO₂⁻ are depleted, but fermentation pathways allow continued metabolism of available organic substrates
- **Genomic signature**: The presence of both denitrification genes and fermentation genes indicates adaptation to a transient environment (OMZ at deposition) followed by long-term persistence through fermentation

This fermentation-enabled survival explains how paleoceanographically selected microbial communities persist as genomic signals in deeply buried sediments.

## Paleoclimate applications

Sedimentary microbiomes can serve as paleoclimate proxies in several ways:

**1. Redox state reconstruction:**
In oxygen minimum zone systems, specific taxa (particularly denitrifiers) are enriched during OMZ deposition. The metagenomic footprint of these taxa provides independent evidence for paleoceanographic redox conditions, complementing traditional proxies like iron speciation or trace metals.

**2. Temporal resolution:**
Unlike single-point paleoclimate proxies (e.g., a foraminifera shell), metagenomic data from sequential sediment intervals provides **centennial-scale temporal resolution** of how microbial communities respond to climate oscillations (glacial-interglacial cycles). This fine-scale resolution reveals the timescale and sensitivity of paleoceanographic changes.

**3. Multiproxy integration:**
Microbiome paleoclimate signals can be integrated with traditional paleoclimate proxies (isotopes, biomarkers, foraminifera, trace metals) to improve reconstructions. Microbial signals represent a completely independent archive (genomic information vs. chemical/isotopic signals) and thus provide valuable cross-validation.

**4. Hidden biodiversity and metabolic potential:**
The genomic approach reveals microbial taxa and metabolic pathways that are otherwise invisible to paleoceanographers. Taxa with no morphological fossil record and no stable biomarkers can now be detected, providing insight into ancient microbial metabolism and evolution.

## Outstanding questions and limitations

1. **Spatial heterogeneity**: Do paleomicrobiome signals vary geographically, or is the paleoenvironmental signal universal? Are there taxa-specific patterns that could serve as regional paleoclimate fingerprints?

2. **DNA preservation timescales**: Metagenomic studies work on Quaternary sediments. How far back in Earth history can paleomicrobiome signals be preserved? Can ancient DNA be recovered from Cretaceous or even older sediments?

3. **Contamination and authenticity**: Sedimentary DNA is susceptible to contamination from modern environments. Rigorous authentication protocols (pyrosequencing, cloning, sequence verification) are needed to confirm paleomicrobiome signals are genuine.

4. **Metabolic inference accuracy**: Genomic potential (presence of genes) does not guarantee metabolic activity. How can activity states be inferred from ancient DNA? Are there phylogenetic or genomic signatures of metabolic state?

5. **Quantitative paleoclimate modeling**: Can paleomicrobiome data be quantitatively inverted to estimate paleoceanographic parameters (e.g., past oxygen concentrations, denitrification rates)?

## Integration with sedimentary biomarker records

Paleomicrobiome signals complement biomarker records:

- **Biomarkers** (lipids, sterols, hopanoids) are the **stable end-products** of microbial metabolism persisting as chemical compounds
- **Sedimentary DNA** encodes the **active metabolic pathways** of organisms during deposition

Together, these approaches provide complementary information:
- Biomarkers reveal which bacteria were present (taxonomic fingerprint)
- Genomic data reveals what metabolic capabilities those bacteria possessed
- Temporal patterns of both reveal how paleoceanographic conditions selected for specific adaptations
