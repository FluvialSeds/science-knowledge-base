# Humic Lakes Greenhouse Gas Cycling

## Core Concept

Humic tropical lakes in carbon-rich peatland and wetland complexes are globally significant sources of greenhouse gases (COтВВ, CHтВД, NтВВO) to the atmosphere. These lakes exhibit high concentrations of dissolved organic carbon and sustained supersaturation of all three major greenhouse gases relative to atmospheric equilibrium across all seasons and water depths. ## Greenhouse Gas Distributions and Sources

**Carbon dioxide (COтВВ):**
Carbon-isotope measurements (╬┤┬╣┬│C) reveal that COтВВ is primarily sourced from respiration of labile (bioavailable) organic carbon rather than refractory dissolved organic matter. COтВВ concentrations increase with water depth, reflecting both surface gas exchange and in situ biological production at depth. This age contrast indicates that deep, aged carbon pools mobilized from lake sediments and surrounding peatlands represent significant sources of atmospheric COтВВ from humic tropical lakes, with implications for understanding how climate-driven changes in water table elevation and peatland connectivity will affect the age and source composition of greenhouse gas emissions.

**Methane (CHтВД):**
Sedimentary methanogenesis is the dominant source of CHтВД in the water column. However, aerobic methanotrophyтАФoxidation of CHтВД by methanotrophic bacteria in oxic watersтАФremoves the majority of pelagic methane before outgassing. In well-stratified systems, up to 90% of methane produced in sediments can be reoxidized before reaching the atmosphere, substantially reducing net CHтВД emissions relative to sedimentary production rates.

**Nitrous oxide (NтВВO):**
Nitrogen-isotope signatures (╬┤┬╣тБ╡N, ╬┤┬╣тБ╕O, and position-specific measurements) demonstrate that NтВВO is predominantly formed through sedimentary denitrification. Despite high sedimentary NтВВO production rates, the majority (~97%) is subsequently reduced to NтВВ gas before diffusing upward and outgassingтАФa process mediated by microbial respiration in anoxic sediments.

## Environmental Controls on Greenhouse Gas Emissions

**Dissolved oxygen saturation:**
The concentration of dissolved oxygen in the water column fundamentally controls which metabolic pathways are active. Aerobic respiration, nitrification, and methanotrophy all require oxygen, whereas methanogenesis and denitrification occur only under anaerobic conditions. Variations in oxygen saturation across seasons and depths directly control the proportion of greenhouse gases that are oxidized (and removed) versus outgassed.

**Water depth:**
Deeper lakes allow for larger vertical gradients in oxygen concentration, creating thicker anoxic zones in sediments and bottom waters. This spatial heterogeneity enhances sedimentary greenhouse gas production and allows for greater in-water oxidation of CHтВД and NтВВO before outgassing.

**Seasonal hydrologic cycles:**
High-water, falling-water, and low-water seasons create contrasting physical and biogeochemical conditions. Water column mixing and stratification patterns, driven by seasonal changes in insolation and precipitation, control oxygen distribution and hence the balance between greenhouse gas production and consumption.

## Microbial Community and Process Controls

**Methanotrophic efficiency:**
The high proportion of sedimentary CHтВД that is reoxidized in the water column indicates that methanotrophic communities are metabolically active and efficient. Changes in oxygen concentration or methanotrophic community composition could significantly alter the proportion of methane that escapes to the atmosphere.

**Denitrification capacity:**
The near-quantitative reduction of NтВВO to NтВВ indicates that denitrifier communities efficiently reduce NтВВO in sedimentary environments. This metabolic efficiency is sensitive to substrate availability (NOтВГтБ╗ and organic carbon) and environmental conditions (temperature, pH, oxygen diffusion rates).

## Climate Change Implications

**Warming-driven perturbations:**
Rising temperatures will increase microbial metabolic rates, potentially enhancing greenhouse gas production in sediments and water columns. Simultaneously, if warming reduces anoxic sediment zones (through enhanced oxygen diffusion or decreased stratification), microbial oxidation of CHтВД and NтВВO could be compromised, leading to higher emissions.

**Water table and connectivity changes:**
Climate-driven changes in precipitation, evaporation, and water table elevation will alter the hydrological connectivity between peatlands and lakes. Such changes could modulate the supply of organic carbon and nitrate to lakes, with cascading effects on greenhouse gas cycling.

**Peatland carbon mobilization feedback:**
Disturbance of upstream peatlands and changes in water table elevation could accelerate the mobilization of aged peat carbon into lake systems, further enhancing COтВВ and CHтВД production and emissions.
