# Humic Lakes Greenhouse Gas Cycling

## Core Concept

Humic tropical lakes in carbon-rich peatland and wetland complexes are globally significant sources of greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) to the atmosphere. These lakes exhibit high concentrations of dissolved organic carbon and sustained supersaturation of all three major greenhouse gases relative to atmospheric equilibrium across all seasons and water depths. Isotope geochemistry reveals distinct sources for each gas—CO₂ from aerobic respiration of bioavailable organic carbon, CH₄ from sedimentary methanogenesis followed by aerobic methanotrophy, and N₂O from sedimentary denitrification—providing mechanistic insight into how climate change and anthropogenic disturbance could accelerate greenhouse gas emissions from these systems.

## Greenhouse Gas Distributions and Sources

**Carbon dioxide (CO₂):**
Carbon-isotope measurements (δ¹³C) reveal that CO₂ is primarily sourced from respiration of labile (bioavailable) organic carbon rather than refractory dissolved organic matter. CO₂ concentrations increase with water depth, reflecting both surface gas exchange and in situ biological production at depth.

**Methane (CH₄):**
Sedimentary methanogenesis is the dominant source of CH₄ in the water column. However, aerobic methanotrophy—oxidation of CH₄ by methanotrophic bacteria in oxic waters—removes the majority of pelagic methane before outgassing. In well-stratified systems, up to 90% of methane produced in sediments can be reoxidized before reaching the atmosphere, substantially reducing net CH₄ emissions relative to sedimentary production rates.

**Nitrous oxide (N₂O):**
Nitrogen-isotope signatures (δ¹⁵N, δ¹⁸O, and position-specific measurements) demonstrate that N₂O is predominantly formed through sedimentary denitrification. Despite high sedimentary N₂O production rates, the majority (~97%) is subsequently reduced to N₂ gas before diffusing upward and outgassing—a process mediated by microbial respiration in anoxic sediments.

## Environmental Controls on Greenhouse Gas Emissions

**Dissolved oxygen saturation:**
The concentration of dissolved oxygen in the water column fundamentally controls which metabolic pathways are active. Aerobic respiration, nitrification, and methanotrophy all require oxygen, whereas methanogenesis and denitrification occur only under anaerobic conditions. Variations in oxygen saturation across seasons and depths directly control the proportion of greenhouse gases that are oxidized (and removed) versus outgassed.

**Water depth:**
Deeper lakes allow for larger vertical gradients in oxygen concentration, creating thicker anoxic zones in sediments and bottom waters. This spatial heterogeneity enhances sedimentary greenhouse gas production and allows for greater in-water oxidation of CH₄ and N₂O before outgassing.

**Seasonal hydrologic cycles:**
High-water, falling-water, and low-water seasons create contrasting physical and biogeochemical conditions. Water column mixing and stratification patterns, driven by seasonal changes in insolation and precipitation, control oxygen distribution and hence the balance between greenhouse gas production and consumption.

## Emissions Estimates and Global Significance

Lake Mai Ndombe, Africa's largest humic lake, serves as a paradigmatic system. Annual emissions are estimated at:
- **CO₂**: 375 ± 32 Gg C yr⁻¹
- **CH₄**: 623 ± 136 Mg C yr⁻¹
- **N₂O**: 223 ± 20 Mg N yr⁻¹

These emissions reflect year-round supersaturation and high gas concentrations in the water column, demonstrating that humic tropical lakes represent persistent (not episodic) sources of greenhouse gases.

## Microbial Community and Process Controls

**Methanotrophic efficiency:**
The high proportion of sedimentary CH₄ that is reoxidized in the water column indicates that methanotrophic communities are metabolically active and efficient. Changes in oxygen concentration or methanotrophic community composition could significantly alter the proportion of methane that escapes to the atmosphere.

**Denitrification capacity:**
The near-quantitative reduction of N₂O to N₂ indicates that denitrifier communities efficiently reduce N₂O in sedimentary environments. This metabolic efficiency is sensitive to substrate availability (NO₃⁻ and organic carbon) and environmental conditions (temperature, pH, oxygen diffusion rates).

## Climate Change Implications

**Warming-driven perturbations:**
Rising temperatures will increase microbial metabolic rates, potentially enhancing greenhouse gas production in sediments and water columns. Simultaneously, if warming reduces anoxic sediment zones (through enhanced oxygen diffusion or decreased stratification), microbial oxidation of CH₄ and N₂O could be compromised, leading to higher emissions.

**Water table and connectivity changes:**
Climate-driven changes in precipitation, evaporation, and water table elevation will alter the hydrological connectivity between peatlands and lakes. Such changes could modulate the supply of organic carbon and nitrate to lakes, with cascading effects on greenhouse gas cycling.

**Peatland carbon mobilization feedback:**
Disturbance of upstream peatlands and changes in water table elevation could accelerate the mobilization of aged peat carbon into lake systems, further enhancing CO₂ and CH₄ production and emissions.
