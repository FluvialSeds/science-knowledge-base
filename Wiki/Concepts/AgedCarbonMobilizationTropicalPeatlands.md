# Aged Carbon Mobilization in Tropical Peatlands

## Core Concept

Large humic lakes in tropical peatland complexes serve as sites where ancient carbon accumulated in peat soils over millennia is rapidly mobilized, transported through aquatic systems, and outgassed to the atmosphere as CO₂ and CH₄. Radiocarbon analysis reveals that dissolved organic carbon and dissolved inorganic carbon in these lakes contain substantial contributions from millennially-aged carbon (hundreds to thousands of years old), demonstrating that peat-derived carbon pools accumulated over long timescales can be rapidly remobilized through changes in water table elevation and hydrological connectivity. This mechanism represents a significant positive feedback to climate change, as disturbance of peatlands and shifts in water tables could accelerate the release of ancient carbon stores to the atmosphere.

## Evidence for Aged Carbon in Lake Systems

**Radiocarbon signatures of aged carbon:**
Accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) radiocarbon dating of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) reveals radiocarbon ages spanning hundreds to thousands of years (Δ¹⁴C depleted relative to modern standards). This pre-aged carbon signature is diagnostic of carbon derived from peat soils, which accumulate carbon over millennia through slow decomposition and burial.

**Distinction from modern primary production:**
The aged radiocarbon signature clearly distinguishes peat-derived carbon from modern primary production (which would have near-zero radiocarbon age). This allows quantitative partitioning of lake dissolved carbon into modern and aged components through two-component mixing models.

**Spatial and temporal variations:**
Radiocarbon ages of lake dissolved carbon vary seasonally and spatially, reflecting changes in peatland water table elevation and hydrological connectivity. During low-discharge periods (dry seasons), lakes receive more aged carbon from deep peat pools, resulting in more depleted Δ¹⁴C values. High-discharge periods bring fresher vegetation-derived carbon from riparian zones, causing Δ¹⁴C values to shift toward modern values.

## Mechanistic Pathways for Aged Carbon Mobilization

**Peatland water table dynamics:**
In tropical peatlands, seasonal and interannual variations in precipitation control water table elevation. When water tables are high (wet season), water moves laterally through recently accumulated peat near the surface, carrying modern and semi-modern carbon. When water tables drop (dry season), water is drawn from deeper peat layers accumulated over centuries to millennia, mobilizing aged carbon into lakes and rivers.

**Hydrological connectivity:**
The degree of hydrological connectivity between peatlands and lakes determines the proportion of aged versus modern carbon entering lake systems. In densely connected systems with extensive peatland-lake networks (such as the Cuvette Congolaise in the Congo Basin), changes in connectivity rapidly modulate aged carbon supply.

**Groundwater discharge:**
Aged carbon in groundwater discharging to lakes represents a significant and persistent source, even during seasons when surface runoff is minimal. This groundwater pathway is particularly important in systems with deep water tables and extensive peat sequences.

## Implications for the Carbon Cycle

**Vulnerability of peatland carbon stores:**
The rapid mobilization of millennial-aged peat carbon demonstrates that carbon accumulated in peatlands over long timescales is not permanently sequestered. Climate change-driven perturbations to water tables, precipitation, and peatland vegetation could accelerate this mobilization and shift peatlands from net carbon sinks to substantial net carbon sources.

**Feedback loop mechanisms:**
A positive feedback loop is triggered when peatland disturbance → increased water table drawdown → enhanced aged carbon mobilization → increased lake CO₂ and CH₄ emissions → additional atmospheric warming → further peatland drying. This feedback could substantially amplify climate warming in tropical regions with extensive peatland systems.

**Timescale of carbon release:**
The aged radiocarbon ages indicate that carbon accumulated over centuries to millennia can be released to the atmosphere over decadal to centennial timescales if hydrological changes persist. This represents a dramatic acceleration of carbon cycling relative to the accumulation timescales.

**Regional climate impacts:**
Tropical peatland-lake systems, particularly in central Africa and Southeast Asia, could become major sources of atmospheric CO₂ and CH₄ if peatland water tables decline. Given the vast extent of tropical peatlands (estimated >400 Gt C), even small changes in mobilization rates could have globally significant climate impacts.

## Seasonal and Hydrological Controls

**Seasonal Δ¹⁴C variations:**
Radiocarbon composition of lake dissolved carbon shows predictable seasonal patterns linked to water table elevation and discharge regimes. This seasonal signature can be used as a tracer of hydrological changes in peatland-lake systems and as a predictor of carbon mobilization rates.

**Drought and flood responses:**
Extreme drought events that dramatically lower water tables could trigger pulses of aged carbon mobilization. Conversely, extreme flooding events that raise water tables and increase connectivity could cause rapid shifts in Δ¹⁴C composition as different peat layers are activated.

## Management and Conservation Implications

**Peatland protection priority:**
The vulnerability of peatland carbon stores to rapid mobilization and atmospheric release argues for urgent protection and restoration of tropical peatlands. Even short-term water table perturbations could trigger long-term carbon loss.

**Hydrological management:**
Restoration of natural water table regimes through dam removal, wetland restoration, and precipitation management could slow aged carbon mobilization and reduce lake-based greenhouse gas emissions.
