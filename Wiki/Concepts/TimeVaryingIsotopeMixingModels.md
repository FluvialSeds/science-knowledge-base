# Time Varying Isotope Mixing Models

## Core Concept

Time-varying mixing models (TVMM) allow both isotopic signatures and source contributions to vary temporally, improving over time-averaged models (TAMM) for estimating riverine carbon sources. Applications across six northeastern U.S. rivers demonstrate that discharge, temperature, and vegetation phenology are primary drivers of seasonal variation in particulate organic carbon (POC), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) sources.

## Model Comparison: TVMM vs TAMM

**Time-averaged mixing model (TAMM):**
- Assumes static source isotopic signatures
- Only source contributions vary with time
- Useful baseline for comparison
- Oversimplifies complex biogeochemical dynamics

**Time-varying mixing model (TVMM):**
- Allows both isotopic signatures AND source contributions to vary temporally
- Accounts for seasonal changes in source end-member values
- Provides more realistic estimates of carbon dynamics
- Better captures complex non-static conditions

## Drivers of Temporal Variation

**Particulate organic carbon (POC):**
- Primary driver: Stream discharge
- High-flow periods mobilize different carbon sources than low-flow periods
- Seasonal peaks in different algal and plant material inputs

**Dissolved organic carbon (DOC):**
- Primary drivers: Stream discharge and water temperature
- Temperature affects decomposition rates and plant productivity
- Discharge controls hydrologic transport pathways

**Dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC):**
- Primary drivers: Water temperature and vegetation phenology
- Temperature affects carbonate equilibria and microbial respiration
- Vegetation phenology controls organic matter production and decomposition

## Six-River Application (Northeastern U.S.)

**Study rivers:**
- Connecticut, Hudson, Delaware, Susquehanna, Potomac, and Roanoke
- Drainage to Middle Atlantic Bight

**Six potential POC/DOC sources:**
- C₃ plant material
- C₄ plant material
- Algal material
- Slow-turnover soil organic carbon
- Passive-turnover soil organic carbon
- Fossil organic carbon

**Four DIC sources:**
- Atmospheric exchange
- Carbonate dissolution
- POC remineralization
- DOC remineralization

## Major Source Contributions

**POC sourcing:**
- C₃ plant material: 50–100%
- Algal material: significant seasonal contributor
- Slow-turnover soil organic matter: 50–100%
- Fossil carbon: minimal

**DOC sourcing:**
- C₃ plant material: 60–100%
- Slow-turnover soil organic matter: 60–100%
- Passive-turnover carbon: variable

**DIC sourcing:**
- Atmospheric exchange: 40–60%
- Remineralization of organic matter: remainder

## Key Finding: Seasonal Variation Dominates

Seasonal change in relative contributions from these sources dominated observed variation in all three carbon pools. This indicates that:
- Static source models significantly underestimate complexity
- Hydrologic and thermal conditions are primary controls on carbon sources
- TVMM provides more accurate representation of actual carbon dynamics

## Applications Across Biogeochemical Systems

The TVMM represents a novel approach with potential application across diverse biogeochemical studies:
- **Improved accuracy:** Better representation of non-static conditions
- **Climate sensitivity:** Enables prediction of how climate changes affect carbon export
- **Coastal impacts:** Provides realistic estimates of terrestrial carbon delivery to coastal waters
- **Management applications:** Helps constrain carbon budgets under variable conditions
