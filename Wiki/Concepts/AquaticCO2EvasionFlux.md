# Aquatic CO₂ Evasion Flux

## Core Concept

Aquatic ecosystems act as significant sources of atmospheric CO₂ through gas evasion from surface waters, with flux rates and carbon sources varying substantially based on water chemistry, flow conditions, and watershed characteristics. Understanding the magnitude, spatial heterogeneity, and isotopic composition of evaded CO₂ is essential for quantifying carbon cycling in soil-water-atmosphere continua.

## Measurement Approaches

CO₂ evasion from surface waters can be quantified through direct and indirect methods. Direct approaches use floating chambers with CO₂ capture systems (zeolite molecular sieves or gas chromatography) to collect evaded gases in situ. Indirect approaches calculate evasion from dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) concentrations, water-atmosphere CO₂ partial pressure gradients, and gas transfer coefficients. Comparison of direct and indirect methods reveals systematic differences that depend on chamber design, equilibration time, and environmental conditions. Methodological validation is critical for ensuring consistency across research programs and enabling synthesis of flux data from different field studies.

## Spatial and Temporal Variability

Evasion rates vary substantially with stream characteristics. Rates are lowest in deep, slow-flowing sections and highest in areas with rapid water movement and high dissolved CO₂ concentrations. Seasonal variations in evasion rates reflect changes in water temperature, pH, discharge, and the relative proportions of different carbon sources contributing to the dissolved CO₂ pool. Within peatland systems, evasion can vary from ~14 to ~475 μg CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹ depending on local hydrological and chemical conditions.

## Sources of Evaded CO₂

The carbon sources contributing to aquatic CO₂ evasion depend on watershed characteristics and hydrological pathways. In peatlands and other carbon-rich landscapes, evaded CO₂ contains substantial proportions of older carbon mobilized from soil organic matter and geogenic sources. In forest ecosystems, evaded CO₂ may be dominated by recent photosynthetic products, representing a rapid return pathway for currently fixed carbon. The radiocarbon age and stable isotope composition of evaded CO₂ therefore provides diagnostic information about carbon source contributions and the relative importance of different carbon pools to aquatic gas exchange.

## Quantifying Atmospheric Feedback

The magnitude of CO₂ evasion from aquatic systems contributes significantly to annual carbon budgets in coupled terrestrial-aquatic systems. Shifts in evasion rates resulting from climate change, hydrological alteration, or changes in watershed carbon processing can substantially alter net carbon export to the atmosphere. Direct measurements of evaded CO₂ isotopic composition enable separation of geogenic versus biogenic carbon sources and assessment of how disturbances to watershed carbon pools affect the age and source composition of aquatic CO₂ emissions.
