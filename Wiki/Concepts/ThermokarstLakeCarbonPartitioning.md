# Thermokarst Lake Carbon Partitioning

## Core Concept

Thermokarst lakes in sporadic permafrost regions exhibit contrasting carbon sources: peatland systems contain significant permafrost-derived carbon (millennial-old) in particulate organic carbon (POC) and ebullition CH₄, while mineral soil systems are dominated by contemporary carbon. Seasonal variation affects DOC and DIC ages more than dissolved CH₄, with peatland and non-peatland systems showing distinct patterns of carbon mobilization.

## Peatland vs Mineral Soil Systems

**Peatland thermokarst lakes:**
- DOC: Mix of modern and permafrost-derived carbon
- DIC: Mix of modern and permafrost-derived carbon
- Dissolved CH₄: Significant modern carbon contribution
- POC: Predominantly millennial-old carbon
- Ebullition CH₄: Dominated by very old (millennial) carbon

**Mineral soil systems:**
- DOC: Predominantly modern carbon (fixed after 1950)
- DIC: Predominantly modern carbon
- Dissolved CH₄: Predominantly modern carbon
- POC: Predominantly fixed in last millennium
- No distinct pattern for ebullition CH₄

## Seasonal Variation Patterns

**Winter vs summer differences:**
- In winter: ¹⁴C age of DOC, DIC, and POC in peatland lakes increases compared to summer
- In winter: Dissolved CH₄ age does not change seasonally
- This pattern absent in non-peatland lakes

**Interpretation:**
- Winter conditions alter water mixing and carbon mobilization pathways
- Different carbon pools respond differently to seasonal forcing
- Deep, older carbon becomes more accessible during winter mixing

## Differential Gas Dynamics

**Peatland lakes show:**
- Older carbon sources for ebullition CH₄ relative to dissolved CH₄
- Distinct pathways for methane production and transport

**Non-peatland lakes:**
- No such pattern observed
- More uniform carbon ages across different gas types

## Implications for Permafrost Carbon Feedback

**Modern carbon dominates emissions despite old carbon presence:**
- While permafrost carbon is incorporated into aquatic organic carbon pools
- Modern carbon from recent productivity is the largest source of CH₄ and CO₂ emissions
- Suggests current warming may not immediately trigger large old-carbon emissions

**Seasonal patterns important for predictions:**
- Contrasting seasonal patterns between dissolved and ebullitive gases
- Implications for accurately constraining permafrost carbon feedback
- Important for understanding how different thermal regimes and vegetation types influence carbon mobilization

**Climate scenarios:**
- Understanding current carbon pathways essential for predicting responses to continued warming
- Differential responses of peatland vs mineral systems important for regional extrapolation
- Arctic and Subarctic systems show complex carbon cycling dynamics
