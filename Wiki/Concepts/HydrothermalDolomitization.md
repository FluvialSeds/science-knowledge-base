# Hydrothermal Dolomitization

## Core Concept

Dolomite (CaMg(CO₃)₂) is an abundant carbonate mineral in ancient sedimentary rocks, yet the mechanisms controlling its formation in natural systems remain incompletely understood. Hydrothermal dolomitization—precipitation of dolomite through elevated-temperature, fluid-rock interactions driven by circulating hot brines—represents an important pathway for dolomite formation, particularly in carbonate systems with permeable structures that focus fluid flow. Evidence from petrography, elemental and isotopic geochemistry, fluid inclusions, and crystal morphology demonstrates that hydrothermal dolomitization is typically localized to high-permeability features (organic mounds, fracture networks, cavity systems) that serve as pathways for thermal brine circulation. These patterns reveal how structural and stratigraphic architecture controls the spatial and temporal distribution of dolomite in carbonate successions.

## Petrographic Evidence for Hydrothermal Dolomite

**Dolomite mineralogy and textures:**
Hydrothermal dolomite displays characteristic petrographic features:

- **Two or more generations of dolomite**: Early-formed dolomite crystals associated with burial and diagenesis, followed by later dolomite phases in fractures and along grain boundaries
- **Dolomite crystal morphologies**: Euhedral to subhedral crystals indicating formation in open-space environments (fractures, cavities) rather than replacement textures
- **Crosscutting relationships**: Later dolomite-bearing fracture networks that cut through earlier carbonate minerals, indicating post-compaction, pressure-solution-driven fluid flow

**Pore space characteristics:**
Original depositional porosity and permeability controls where dolomitization occurs:

- **High-permeability zones**: Bioherm structures (coral-sponge mounds), reef complexes, and granular sediments that maintain elevated porosity during burial
- **Low-permeability zones**: Surrounding basin sediments composed of fine-grained muds and marls that become isolated from dolomitizing fluid flow

This permeability heterogeneity explains spatial patterns of patchy or localized dolomitization.

## Fluid Inclusion Evidence for Thermal Conditions

**Homogenization temperature measurements:**
Fluid inclusion microthermometry on dolomite crystals provides direct evidence for the temperature of dolomite precipitation:

- **Moderate temperatures**: Homogenization temperatures typically range from 60–120°C, far exceeding modern depositional temperatures
- **Correlation with burial depth**: These temperatures are consistent with moderate burial depths (1–3 km for typical geothermal gradients), indicating that dolomitization occurred during burial rather than surface diagenesis

**Fluid composition from inclusion analysis:**
Fluid inclusion analysis reveals:

- **Elevated salinity**: Brines with salinity exceeding modern seawater (>35 ppt), consistent with evaporitic or diagenetically concentrated fluids
- **Elevated Mg/Ca ratios**: The dissolved Mg/Ca ratio of dolomitizing fluids exceeded that of modern seawater, favoring dolomite precipitation
- **Radiogenic isotope signatures**: Fluid compositions potentially reflecting mixing between connate (formation) waters and external fluids

## Isotopic Constraints on Dolomitization

**Carbon and oxygen stable isotopes:**
δ¹³C and δ¹⁸O compositions of dolomite reveal:

- **Depleted δ¹³C values**: Reflecting either incorporation of oxidized organic carbon from the surrounding rocks or loss of carbon to the fluid phase during dolomite precipitation
- **Elevated δ¹⁸O values**: Reflecting precipitation at elevated temperatures, as dolomite δ¹⁸O decreases systematically with increasing temperature
- **Non-seawater isotopic compositions**: Indicating that dolomitizing fluids were not simple modified seawater, but rather had experienced significant water-rock interaction

**Strontium isotope evidence:**
The ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr composition of dolomite may differ from host carbonates, potentially indicating:

- Fluid incorporation of radiogenic strontium from feldspar dissolution
- Mixing of seawater-derived fluids with continental or basement-derived fluids

## Mechanisms of Hydrothermal Dolomitization

**Thermally-driven fluid flow and density gradients:**
The density structure of fluids at elevated temperatures drives circulation:

- **Depth-driven flow**: Hot, less-dense fluids at depth rise along permeable pathways (fractures, grain boundaries)
- **Lateral flow**: Fluids cool laterally through less-permeable country rock, precipitating minerals as they cool
- **Return flow**: Cooled fluids sink back to depth through alternate pathways, creating convection cells

This thermally-driven circulation can persist over geological timescales if heat sources (magmatism, deep burial, fault-zone friction) sustain temperature gradients.

**Dolomite precipitation from cooling brines:**
As fluids cool from elevated temperatures, the solubility of dolomite decreases, promoting precipitation. Additionally, cooling reduces the solubility of calcite, but more steeply than dolomite, creating conditions favorable for dolomite precipitation relative to calcite.

**Mg²⁺ and Ca²⁺ ratios during precipitation:**
Dolomite precipitation is favored when the Mg/Ca ratio of the fluid exceeds approximately 2–3 (compared to ~5 in modern seawater). Concentrated brines with evaporitic origins often exceed these thresholds.

## Structural Controls on Dolomitization Patterns

**Bioherm and organic mound structures:**
Elevated-relief carbonate structures (bioherms, reefs, coral-sponge mounds) focus fluid flow through their permeable interiors:

- **Capillary pressure contrast**: The contrast between high-porosity mound interiors and lower-porosity surrounding sediments creates pressure gradients that drive flow into mounds
- **Structural relief**: The topographic expression of mounds drives thermal convection
- **Connected pore networks**: Mound sediments maintain interconnected porosity even after moderate burial and compaction

**Fracture-controlled dolomitization:**
Later hydrothermal dolomite is often concentrated along fracture networks:

- **Fault zones and joints**: Provide pathways for focused fluid migration through otherwise impermeable rocks
- **Dissolution enlargement**: Acidic, CO₂-enriched brines progressively enlarge fractures through limestone dissolution, enhancing permeability
- **Mineral precipitation in voids**: Dolomite crystals in open fracture space indicate unconfined precipitation rather than replacement

## Regional Implications for Dolomite Distribution

**Basin-scale dolomitization patterns:**
The localization of hydrothermal dolomite to specific structures explains observed patterns of dolomite distribution in carbonate basins:

- **Patchy dolomitization**: Not all rocks at a given burial depth become dolomitized—only those in permeable pathways
- **Facies-dependent dolomitization**: High-porosity, high-permeability facies (grainstone, bioherm) preferentially dolomitized relative to fine-grained basinal facies
- **Temporal variability**: Dolomitization concentrated during specific burial stages when temperature and pressure conditions, combined with fluid pressure gradients, drive circulation

## Paleofluids and Basin Evolution

**Temporal evolution of hydrothermal systems:**
Dolomitization typically occurs during specific windows in basin burial history:

- **Early burial stage** (1–2 km depth): Warm but not hot fluids from burial-driven compaction
- **Peak burial stage** (2–4 km depth): Hottest conditions, potentially augmented by nearby magmatism
- **Late burial/exhumation stage**: Declining temperatures, but potentially enhanced fluid pressure gradients during tectonism

**Connection to basin tectonics:**
Fault movements and extensional tectonics can create pressure gradients and fault-related permeability structures that focus hydrothermal fluid flow and dolomitization.

## Distinction from Supergene Dolomitization

Unlike reflux dolomitization (driven by shallow, density-driven circulation of concentrated brines) and sabkha dolomitization (driven by surface evaporation), hydrothermal dolomitization occurs at burial depths with elevated temperatures. This distinction is critical for interpreting dolomite in deeply buried carbonate successions.
