# Petrogenic Organic Carbon

## Core Concept

Petrogenic organic carbon (OCpetro) is ancient, lithospheric organic matter locked in bedrock that was buried millions to billions of years ago. Unlike biospheric organic carbon (produced by living organisms), petrogenic carbon is derived from the geological record and becomes exposed through mountain exhumation and erosion.

## Sources and Distribution

Petrogenic organic carbon is present in:
- Shales and other sedimentary rocks
- Metamorphic rocks
- Bedrock being exhumed in rapidly eroding mountain belts

It accumulates in bedrock over geological timescales and becomes accessible to oxidation processes when rock is uplifted and exposed at the surface through tectonic activity and erosion.

## Oxidation and CO₂ Release

When bedrock containing petrogenic organic carbon is exhumed and eroded, the exposed carbon can be [[PetrogenicCarbonOxidation|oxidized]] to CO₂ through abiotic and microbial processes. In rapidly eroding tropical mountain soils, approximately **67 ± 11%** of petrogenic organic carbon initially present in exhumed bedrock is oxidized during soil formation.

## Petrogenic Carbon in Soil-Bedrock Systems

Petrogenic organic carbon is not limited to deep crustal rocks; it is also present at the active soil-bedrock interface where weathering processes expose kerogen and other resistant organic compounds from metamorphic and sedimentary rocks. In this environment, petrogenic carbon becomes subject to:

**Microbial assimilation and oxidation:**
The condensed aromatic structure of kerogen limits its biodegradability compared to fresh plant-derived organic matter, but microbes can oxidize it, particularly in the presence of fresh organic matter inputs.

**Priming effects:**
[[SoilPrimingEffects|Fresh organic matter inputs]] from roots and litter may trigger accelerated microbial mineralization of petrogenic carbon at the soil-bedrock interface. However, this interaction remains completely unstudied, representing a critical knowledge gap in understanding soil carbon dynamics.

**Radiocarbon complications:**
When petrogenic carbon is incorporated into soil systems through weathering, it creates a "pre-aged" carbon pool with ancient radiocarbon ages (Δ¹⁴C values highly depleted). This biases [[RadiocarbonOrganicMatter|radiocarbon measurements]] of total soil carbon and can lead to systematic overestimation of biospheric carbon residence times if petrogenic inputs are not accounted for.

**Land-use impacts:**
The effects of land management on petrogenic carbon cycling in soils are essentially unquantified. Processes that alter soil erosion rates, vegetation colonization depth, and root penetration into bedrock (such as afforestation, deforestation, or cultivation) may significantly change the rate at which petrogenic carbon is exposed and oxidized.

## Significance in the Carbon Cycle

Petrogenic organic carbon oxidation is a natural source of atmospheric CO₂ on multiple timescales:

**Geological timescale (million years):**
In rapidly eroding mountain systems, [[MicrobialOxidationOrganicCarbon|petrogenic organic carbon oxidation]] acts as a negative feedback on CO₂ drawdown from silicate weathering and organic burial, coupling tectonics directly to atmospheric CO₂ and climate.

**Land-surface timescale (centuries to millennia):**
In soil systems, petrogenic carbon weathering and oxidation contribute to total soil carbon stocks and responses to land-use change. Failure to account for petrogenic inputs results in overestimation of biospheric carbon stocks and misrepresentation of soil carbon dynamics in landscapes underlain by organic-rich bedrock.
