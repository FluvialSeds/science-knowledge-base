# Biogeochemical Cycling Reconstructed from Biomarkers

## Core Concept

Organic biomarkers preserve information about biogeochemical cycling processes in terrestrial systems, including soil carbon residence time, microbial decomposition dynamics, and methane cycling. Specific biomarker compounds and their isotopic composition record the fate of organic carbon as it moves through soils—from primary synthesis by plants through microbial oxidation and eventual burial. These biomarker-based reconstructions enable quantification of how biogeochemical cycling has responded to changes in climate, vegetation, and environmental conditions across millions of years, providing insight into the vulnerability of terrestrial carbon pools to future climate change.

## Soil Carbon Residence Time

The residence time of carbon in soils—the duration carbon persists in soil before oxidation to CO₂—controls long-term carbon sequestration and atmospheric CO₂ levels. Biomarkers with different chemical structures and bonding environments persist for different timescales in soils, allowing paleoceanographers to track carbon residence time through geological time. Recalcitrant biomarkers that resist microbial oxidation accumulate in soils and persist for millennia, while labile organic compounds are oxidized rapidly. The ratio of recalcitrant to labile biomarkers in paleosols and sediments indicates the soil carbon residence time and the efficacy of carbon protection mechanisms. Changes in soil carbon residence time in response to climate or vegetation shifts have implications for understanding how terrestrial carbon cycling will respond to future warming.

## Microbial Decomposition Dynamics

The degree of biomarker diagenesis—chemical alteration during burial and diagenesis—reflects the extent of microbial processing during soil formation and early sediment diagenesis. Biomarkers undergo systematic chemical transformations as microbes progressively oxidize and fragment organic molecules. The molecular structure of biomarkers in paleosols and sediments encodes information about microbial processing intensity and environmental conditions during decomposition. Molecular fossils that retain original structures indicate rapid burial or anaerobic conditions preventing complete decomposition, while highly altered biomarkers indicate extensive aerobic microbial processing. Biomarker diagenesis thus provides a window into paleodecomposition rates and paleosol development rates.

## Methane Cycling and Anoxic Conditions

Biomarkers produced by methane-oxidizing bacteria and methanogenic archaea provide direct evidence of past methane cycling. Lipids with distinctive isotopic signatures indicating utilization of methane-derived carbon serve as biomarkers for methanotrophy in paleoenvironments. The abundance and isotopic composition of methanotroph-derived biomarkers in sediments reflect the intensity of methane oxidation, which is suppressed in highly oxic conditions and enhanced in oxygen-minimum zones and anoxic sediments. Biomarkers of methanogenic archaea indicate conditions favorable for anaerobic decomposition. By reconstructing past methane cycling from biomarkers, paleoceanographers can quantify how microbial processes have responded to past climate changes and oxygen availability shifts.

## Temperature Control on Biogeochemical Cycling

Temperature is a primary control on decomposition rates and biogeochemical cycling rates in soils and sediments. Biomarker-based indicators of decomposition intensity (the degree of biomarker alteration and the ratio of recalcitrant to labile biomarkers) correlate with paleoclimate variables, enabling paleoceanographers to quantify temperature effects on biogeochemical cycling through time. Periods of rapid warming show acceleration of decomposition rates recorded in increased biomarker diagenesis, while cooling periods show slower decomposition. This temperature-dependent variation in biogeochemical cycling has implications for understanding how carbon sequestration and greenhouse gas production from soils may respond to future anthropogenic warming.

## Multivariate Biogeochemical Reconstruction

Comprehensive paleobiogeochemical interpretation integrates multiple biomarker indicators—including molecular composition, isotopic ratios, and degree of diagenesis—to reconstruct the combined effects of climate, oxygen availability, vegetation composition, and decomposition rate on terrestrial carbon cycling. This multivariate approach reveals causality and feedback relationships that individual biomarkers cannot capture in isolation. For example, warming that increases decomposition rates also increases atmospheric CO₂ production from soils, potentially creating a positive feedback on climate. Paleobiogeochemical records document how this feedback operated in the past and constrain predictions of future soil carbon responses to climate change.
