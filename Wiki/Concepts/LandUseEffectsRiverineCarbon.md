# Land-Use Effects on Riverine Carbon Pools and Ages

## Core Concept

Land-use changes—including deforestation, agricultural conversion, urban development, and erosion associated with human activities—systematically alter the age distribution, composition, and mobilization rates of carbon exported by rivers. Human-modified landscapes export substantially older dissolved organic carbon and support mobilization of geologically-aged carbon that would remain sequestered under natural conditions, fundamentally changing riverine carbon cycle dynamics at local, regional, and potentially global scales.

## Urbanization and Aged Carbon Mobilization

Urban streams and rivers draining developed landscapes export dramatically aged dissolved organic carbon compared to forested reference reaches. In temperate headwater streams (Chesapeake Bay region):

**Urban stream characteristics:**
- Dissolved organic matter (DOM): Δ¹⁴C = −248 to −202‰ (apparent ages 1,811–2,284 yr BP)
- Particulate organic matter (POM): Δ¹⁴C = −90 to −88‰ (apparent ages 669–887 yr BP)
- Background forested streams: DOM modern to slightly aged; POM modern to moderately aged

**Carbon sources in urban streams:**
- Fossil fuel combustion inputs (vehicle exhaust, heating fuel) introduce pre-aged carbon
- Soil disturbance and erosion mobilize accumulated soil carbon developed over centuries
- Wastewater discharge contributes anthropogenic carbon including packaging materials and synthetic compounds
- Leaking petroleum products and other pollutants contribute aged geogenic carbon

**Mobilization mechanisms:**
- Storm sewers connect to subsurface bedrock aquifers, mobilizing geologically-aged groundwater
- Construction activities expose deep soils and sediments, accelerating erosion and carbon export
- Stormwater runoff carries aged sediments from urban areas
- Reduced vegetation cover eliminates the routing of carbon through living plant pathways

## Agricultural Land-Use and Carbon Cycling

Agricultural conversion from forest to cropland or pasture substantially alters riverine carbon characteristics:

**Temperate agricultural headwaters:**
- Streams draining agricultural land export elevated concentrations of aged and geogenic carbon
- Tillage mobilizes soil carbon accumulated over centuries, exposing it to erosion and export
- Fertilizer application and pesticide use contribute bioavailable anthropogenic compounds

**Mechanisms of aged carbon mobilization:**
- Tillage disrupts organo-mineral associations, exposing previously protected organic carbon
- Enhanced runoff during storm events mobilizes accumulated soils before they can infiltrate
- Reduced organic matter inputs from vegetation mean that contemporary plant production cannot balance aged carbon export
- Nutrient loading stimulates algal growth, which may enhance respiration of aged carbon

## Arid/Semiarid Landscapes and Ancient Carbon Export

The Wuding River on the Chinese Loess Plateau demonstrates how agricultural development and human modifications amplify ancient carbon mobilization in arid systems:

**Carbon age characteristics:**
- Radiocarbon of degassed CO₂: Δ¹⁴C = 810–1,890 yr BP (ancient soil carbon)
- Much older than expected from contemporary carbon cycling rates
- Indicates mobilization of soil carbon accumulated over centuries to millennia

**Mobilization in arid systems:**
- High-intensity rainfall during monsoon seasons creates large discharge events
- Constructed check dams and reservoirs alter sediment transport, increasing sediment storage and carbon burial
- 42% of annual carbon export is buried in reservoirs, removing it from fluvial export
- Remaining carbon export represents preferential mobilization of dissolved species (DIC from carbonate weathering)
- The disconnect between large dissolved carbon ages and relatively young dissolved organic carbon suggests complex sourcing from different soil layers and subsurface compartments

**Climate sensitivity:**
- Arid/semiarid regions are particularly sensitive to discharge variability
- Changes in monsoon intensity or timing directly affect carbon mobilization and export
- Human water management (dams, irrigation) further modulates carbon cycling by altering discharge patterns

## Radiocarbon Ages as Proxies for Land-Use Intensity

Riverine carbon age provides a direct tracer of land-use intensity and effectiveness:

**Natural/forested reference reaches:**
- DOC: modern to ~50 years BP
- POM: modern to ~500 years BP
- Reflects rapid turnover of contemporary vegetation-derived carbon

**Moderately modified reaches:**
- DOC: slightly aged, 100–500 years BP  
- POM: moderately aged, 500–2,000 years BP
- Reflects increased contribution from older soil carbon and reduced vegetation connectivity

**Highly modified/urban reaches:**
- DOC: substantially aged, 1,000–2,500 years BP
- POM: aged to ancient, 500–5,000 years BP
- Reflects substantial mobilization of accumulated soil carbon and geogenic sources

**Management implications:**
- Radiocarbon-based monitoring of riverine carbon can track effectiveness of land-use restoration
- Reforestation or riparian restoration should progressively shift exported carbon toward younger ages
- Persistent aged carbon export despite restoration efforts indicates deep soil or geogenic carbon mobilization requiring longer timescales for recovery

## Implications for Global Carbon Cycle

The widespread anthropogenic modification of land surfaces means that human activities have become significant drivers of riverine carbon mobilization:

**Magnitude:** Land-use effects operate at large spatial scales (catchment to regional) and persist for decades to centuries

**Directionality:** Human land-use consistently mobilizes aged carbon, creating a global pattern of "anthropogenic aging" of riverine carbon export

**Climate feedbacks:** Enhanced mobilization of ancient soil carbon accelerates return of long-sequestered carbon to the atmosphere, potentially representing a positive feedback to climate warming

**Reversibility:** The persistence of aged carbon export even in recovering landscapes suggests that land-use effects create long-lasting changes in carbon cycling that require multi-decadal restoration timescales
