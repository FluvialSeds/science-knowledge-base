# Oceanic Anoxic Events

## Core Concept

 Oceanic anoxic events (OAEs) are widespread episodes of reduced oxygen (anoxia) in ancient ocean water columns, most notably during the Cretaceous Period and other intervals of Earth history. These events resulted in the deposition of organic-rich black shale sediments and fundamentally altered marine [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycling]] and biological communities. OAEs are typically tied to climate warming, changes in ocean circulation patterns, and elevated marine productivity.

## Characteristics and Evidence

OAEs are identified by:
- **Black shale deposition**: Organic-rich, poorly oxygenated sediments
- **Biomarker signatures**: Diagnostic lipids indicating specific microbial communities
- **Geochemical anomalies**: Unusual stable isotope ratios, trace metal enrichments
- **Fossil assemblages**: Changes in microbial and larger organism composition reflecting anoxic conditions

The duration of OAEs varied from thousands to millions of years, and their geographic extent ranged from local  to ocean-basin-wide anoxia.

## Nitrogen Cycle During OAEs

A key distinction between OAEs and other anoxic events (like [[SapropelNitrogenCycleCoupling|Mediterranean sapropels]]) is their [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycling]] signature:

**OAE nitrogen cycling** shows:
- Evidence of nitrite oxidation rather than [[Anammox|anammox]] activity
- Limited [[NitrogenFixation|nitrogen fixation]] by diazotrophs
- Different biomarker profiles reflecting different microbial communities

This contrasts with sapropel formation, where both [[Nitrification|nitrification]], [[Anammox|anammox]], and [[NitrogenFixation|nitrogen fixation]] are all elevated, reflecting different feedback mechanisms linking nutrient cycling to water column stratification.

## Paleoceanographic Causes

Leading hypotheses for OAE origin include:
- **Coastal upwelling**: Enhanced nutrient delivery and productivity leading to oxygen drawdown
- **Restricted circulation**: Reduced oxygen supply to deep water due to changes in ocean circulation
- **Productivity pulses**: Increased organic matter production that consumes oxygen upon decomposition
- **Climate warming**: Reduced oxygen solubility and altered circulation patterns during warm climates

## Implications for Carbon Cycling and Climate

During OAEs:
- Increased organic carbon burial influenced long-term atmospheric CO₂ levels
- Altered [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycling]] affected productivity patterns
- Marine ecosystem restructuring in response to redox changes
- Rapid reoxygenation events punctuated anoxic intervals

Understanding OAEs is crucial for predicting how future ocean [[DeepWaterAnoxia|deoxygenation]] from climate change might alter marine ecosystems and biogeochemical cycling.
