# Wastewater Carbon Coupling to River Biogeochemistry

## Core Concept

Municipal wastewater discharge to tropical rivers introduces large quantities of labile (readily biodegradable) dissolved organic carbon that stimulates heterotrophic respiration, amplifying aquatic CO₂ production and seasonal pCO₂ variations. In urbanized river systems without advanced wastewater treatment, sewage-driven carbon cycling represents a major but often-overlooked contributor to riverine CO₂ emissions, with implications for urban carbon budgets and atmospheric CO₂ budgeting.

## Wastewater as Labile Carbon Source

Municipal sewage contains multiple carbon fractions representing recent human food consumption, household products, and wastewater treatment residues. These carbon sources are highly bioavailable, containing amino acids, sugars, lipids, and other compounds that microorganisms readily metabolize:

**Composition of wastewater carbon:**
- Soluble carbohydrates from food waste and human metabolism
- Amino acids and proteins from organic waste
- Lipids and fatty acids from household cleaning products and food preparation
- Surfactants and polymers from detergents and personal care products
- Cellulose from paper and textile waste

**Bioavailability characteristics:**
- Wastewater DOC is ~2–10× more labile than natural river DOC
- Preferential utilization of these bioavailable compounds creates rapid heterotrophic respiration rates
- Microbial consumption produces both CO₂ (via respiration) and microbial biomass
- Energy from wastewater decomposition can support elevated heterotrophic productivity

## Coupled Carbon Cycling in Sewage-Receiving Rivers

The Mekong–Tonle Sap system in Cambodia demonstrates wastewater-enhanced carbon cycling around urban centers. Field surveys and controlled incubations reveal:

**Spatial patterns:**
- Sewage-receiving river reaches show large downstream increases in pCO₂ during dry season
- pCO₂ values exceed atmospheric equilibrium (>400 μatm), driving strong CO₂ evasion gradients
- Background reaches without sewage input maintain pCO₂ near atmospheric values

**Temporal dynamics:**
- Nocturnal (nighttime) pCO₂ increases are substantially larger in sewage-receiving reaches
- Nighttime increases reflect heterotrophic respiration in absence of photosynthetic CO₂ consumption
- Daytime photosynthetic uptake partially compensates for respiration but cannot fully prevent CO₂ supersaturation

**Respiration enhancement:**
- In-situ incubations with added sewage show dramatic increases in respiration rates
- Sewage-supplemented water produces larger nocturnal pCO₂ increases (200–500 μatm) compared to background water (50–150 μatm)
- Enhanced respiration persists for >12 hours in incubations, indicating sustained bioavailability of wastewater carbon

## Dissolved Organic Matter Characterization

Spectroscopic analysis of DOM from different reaches reveals compositional changes driven by wastewater:

**Excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence:**
- Sewage-receiving reaches show enhanced protein-like fluorophores (aromatic amino acids)
- Background reaches show more humic-like fluorophores (condensed aromatic material)
- The shift toward protein-like DOM indicates bioavailable, labile carbon sources

**Implications for carbon cycling:**
- Protein-like DOM is readily consumed by microbes, explaining high respiration rates
- Humic-like DOM is more resistant to decomposition, explaining lower respiration in background reaches
- Wastewater-driven shifts in DOM composition alter the fundamental biogeochemistry of affected river reaches

## Seasonal Variability and Discharge Effects

Wastewater carbon coupling varies seasonally in monsoon-influenced tropical rivers:

**Dry season (December–April):**
- Low natural discharge limits dilution of sewage inputs
- Wastewater carbon becomes proportionally more important to total river carbon
- pCO₂ increases are most dramatic during this season
- Reduced photosynthetic uptake (due to lower primary productivity with low discharge)  limits CO₂ consumption

**Wet season (May–November):**
- High discharge dilutes sewage inputs
- Wastewater carbon becomes proportionally less important
- pCO₂ values approach atmospheric equilibrium despite continued sewage input
- Enhanced photosynthesis and light availability suppress pCO₂

**Anthropogenic intensification:**
- Seasonal pCO₂ variations are amplified by wastewater inputs
- Dry-season CO₂ emissions are substantially higher in sewage-receiving reaches than background reaches
- This seasonal amplification means that regional and annual carbon budgets must account for anthropogenic sewage carbon

## Climate and Land-Use Implications

Wastewater carbon coupling to riverine biogeochemistry has several implications:

**Urban expansion:** As tropical cities expand and populations grow, wastewater discharge volumes increase, enhancing anthropogenic carbon coupling to river systems

**Treatment efficacy:** Advanced wastewater treatment (secondary or tertiary treatment) removes bioavailable organic carbon, reducing sewage-driven pCO₂ amplification. In contrast, primary treatment or untreated discharge provides minimal removal

**River-atmosphere flux:** Wastewater-enhanced CO₂ evasion directly increases riverine contributions to atmospheric CO₂, with implications for urban and regional carbon budgets

**Regional carbon cycling:** In regions where untreated sewage discharge is widespread (much of tropical Asia, Africa, and Latin America), anthropogenic wastewater carbon significantly enhances riverine CO₂ emissions and should be incorporated into regional carbon cycle models
