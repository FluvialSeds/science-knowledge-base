# Iron Oxide-Preserved Dissolved Organic Carbon as a Paleoceanographic Proxy

## Core Concept

[[DissolvedOrganicMatter|Dissolved organic carbon (DOC)]] is the largest reduced carbon reservoir in modern oceans and fundamentally regulates marine communities and atmospheric CO₂ levels. Yet the geologic history of marine DOC remains largely unconstrained because DOC is chemically labile and rarely preserved in the geological record. This constraint is lifted through a novel direct proxy: iron (oxyhydr)oxide minerals in marine sediments naturally adsorb and preserve dissolved organic molecules from ambient seawater. By analyzing the carbon-13 isotope composition (δ¹³C) and redox state of organic matter bound to iron oxides in paleoceanographic samples spanning billions of years, scientists can reconstruct how marine DOC reservoirs have evolved in response to ecosystem development, changes in sinking particle dynamics, and variations in deep-ocean oxygen availability.

## Why Iron Oxides Preserve DOC

Iron oxides (goethite, lepidocrocite, and related minerals) have high specific surface areas and strongly adsorb dissolved organic molecules. This adsorption process:
- Removes DOC from the water column and incorporates it into the mineral crystal structure
- Occurs at the sediment-water interface during diagenesis
- Preserves organic molecules in a chemically reduced state, preventing oxidative degradation
- Creates a direct, time-integrated archive of the DOC composition that was in contact with the oxide surface

The isotopic and chemical characteristics of adsorbed organic carbon reflect:
- The taxonomic source of the organic matter (autotrophic vs. heterotrophic)
- The metabolic state of organisms that produced the DOC
- The oxygen availability in the water mass
- The reactivity of different DOC compound classes

## Analytical Approach

Iron oxide-bound organic carbon is characterized through:

**Carbon Isotope Composition (δ¹³C):**
- Distinguishes between photosynthetically derived DOC from different depths
- Tracks ecosystem structure and autotrophic metabolism through time
- Reveals whether shallow-water photosynthesis or deep-water chemosynthesis dominates DOC production

**Redox State of Bound Organic Matter:**
- Measured through chemical oxidation protocols that quantify the extent of carbon oxidation
- Classifies DOC compounds into functional groups: labile, semi-labile, semi-refractory, and refractory
- Sensitive to oxygen availability and the balance between productive (anoxic) and oxidative (oxic) metabolic pathways

**Complementary Oxygen-Isotope Measurements:**
- δ¹⁸O of iron oxides provides paleoclimatic and paleohydrologic context
- Triple oxygen isotope composition (Δ¹⁷O) reveals atmospheric oxygen levels and productivity changes
- Integrated analysis of iron oxide δ¹⁸O and carbon signatures creates multi-proxy paleoceanographic reconstruction

## Key Finding: The Neoproterozoic DOC Reservoir

Before the evolution of large, rapidly-sinking eukaryotic organisms (pre-Neoproterozoic, >1 billion years ago), the dissolved organic carbon reservoir in the ocean was **100–1,000 times larger** than modern levels. This massive difference reflects:
- Slow sinking rates of organic particles (primarily small prokaryotic cells)
- Prolonged water column residence times for degradable DOC
- Limited ability to export carbon efficiently to sediments
- Resulting accumulation of semi-labile and refractory DOC in the dissolved pool

**The Neoproterozoic-Phanerozoic Transition:**
With the evolution of large eukaryotic organisms (algae, animals) capable of producing large, dense fecal pellets and macroscopic detritus, particle sinking speeds increased dramatically. This shift:
- Reduced DOC residence time in the water column
- Enhanced carbon export to sediments
- Shrank the dissolved organic carbon reservoir to modern-like levels within a few hundred million years
- Fundamentally altered the oxygen and nutrient cycling structure of the ocean

## Implications for Long-Term Carbon Cycling

The iron oxide DOC proxy demonstrates that:

**Ecosystem Evolution Controls Carbon Cycling:**
- The evolution of eukaryotic life fundamentally altered biogeochemical cycling on planetary scales
- Changes in particle sinking efficiency (a consequence of evolving body sizes and metabolic structures) altered the distribution of reduced carbon between the dissolved, particulate, and sedimentary pools

**Atmospheric Composition Regulation:**
- Ocean DOC reservoir size directly affects how much labile carbon is available for remineralization vs. burial
- The transition to efficient particle sinking altered long-term oxygen burial rates and atmospheric pO₂
- Future changes in ocean circulation, productivity, and oxygen availability will shift DOC dynamics in response to anthropogenic climate change

**Framework for Modern Ocean Change:**
- Predicts how ocean deoxygenation and eutrophication will affect DOC reservoir size and composition
- Reveals mechanistic links between ecosystem structure (organism size, sinking rates) and atmospheric composition
- Provides paleoceanographic context for understanding whether current perturbations to DOC cycling are unprecedented or within the range of natural variability
