# Nitrification

## Core Concept

 is the biological oxidation of ammonia (NH₃) to nitrite (NO₂⁻) and nitrate (NO₃⁻), a fundamental process in the [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycle]]. This two-step oxidation is carried out by specialized bacteria and archaea called nitrifiers, which use the oxidation of inorganic nitrogen as their primary energy source (chemolithoautotrophy). Nitrification occurs across diverse aquatic and terrestrial environments and is essential for converting bioavailable nitrogen into forms that can be further transformed by other microbes.

## Nitrifier Communities

The nitrification pathway involves two functional groups:

**Ammonia-oxidizing organisms (AOA/AOB)**: Convert ammonia to nitrite
- Ammonia-oxidizing bacteria (AOB): e.g., *Nitrosomonas*
- Ammonia-oxidizing archaea (AOA): More abundant in some marine and terrestrial environments

**Nitrite-oxidizing bacteria (NOB)**: Convert nitrite to nitrate
- *Nitrobacter* species (the most well-characterized genus)
- Other nitrite oxidizers in various environments

These organisms often function in syntrophic (mutually dependent) relationships, where the product of one organism (nitrite from AOA/AOB) becomes the substrate for the next.

## Biomarkers and Paleoceanographic Significance

Nitrifier metabolism produces characteristic biochemical compounds that serve as biomarkers in sedimentary records.  lipids, particularly [[MethylhopanoidBiosynthesisNitrification|2-methylhopanoids]], are diagnostic products of certain nitrifiers and their abundance in sediments reflects past nitrification activity. The abundance of nitrifier biomarkers can indicate periods of enhanced nutrient cycling and oxygen availability in marine systems.

## Environmental Conditions

Nitrification rates are controlled by:
- **Ammonia availability**: Limited by primary productivity and organic matter remineralization
- **Oxygen levels**: Most nitrifiers require dissolved oxygen (though some anaerobic pathways exist)
- **pH and temperature**: Affect enzyme kinetics and organism distribution
- **Inhibitors**: Some organic compounds and heavy metals inhibit nitrifier activity

## Paleoceanographic Applications

In paleoceanographic records, nitrifier biomarker abundance and carbon isotope signatures provide constraints on:
- Past [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycle]] perturbations
- Nutrient availability during [[OceanicAnoxicEvents|oceanic anoxic events]]
- Coupling between nitrogen cycling and 
- Changes in water column redox state over geological time
