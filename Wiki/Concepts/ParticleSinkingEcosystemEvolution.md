# Particle Sinking Efficiency and Long-Term Ocean Carbon Cycling

## Core Concept

The evolution of large eukaryotic organisms capable of producing large, dense fecal pellets and macroscopic detritus fundamentally transformed ocean carbon cycling on billion-year timescales. Before the evolution of rapid-sinking particles (pre-Neoproterozoic, >1 billion years ago), organic matter sank slowly through the water column as small prokaryotic cells and dissolved organic compounds. This slow sinking allowed extensive microbial degradation in the water column, accumulating a massive dissolved organic carbon (DOC) reservoir that was 100–1,000 times larger than modern levels. With the evolution of large eukaryotes in the Neoproterozoic, particle sinking speeds increased dramatically, reducing DOC residence times and fundamentally altering the partitioning of reduced carbon between dissolved, particulate, and sedimentary pools. This shift in particle sinking efficiency—driven by evolving organism body sizes and metabolic structures—altered atmospheric oxygen levels and nutrient cycling on planetary scales.

## The Slow-Sinking Pre-Neoproterozoic Ocean

**Before Large Eukaryotes:**
- Organic particles were primarily small prokaryotic cells (picoplankton and nanoplankton)
- Sinking rates were slow: 1–10 m day⁻¹ (vs. 100–1,000 m day⁻¹ for modern large particles)
- Water column residence times for organic matter were prolonged (hundreds of years)
- Extensive microbial decomposition occurred during sinking, converting labile organic matter to semi-labile and refractory compounds

**Consequences for DOC Dynamics:**
- High proportion of organic carbon remained in the dissolved pool
- Accumulation of semi-labile and refractory DOC (compounds resistant to further degradation)
- Large DOC reservoir (100–1,000× modern) regulated nutrient availability and supported diverse microbial metabolic pathways
- Reduced organic carbon export to sediments, limiting the burial of reduced carbon

## The Neoproterozoic-Phanerozoic Transition

**Evolution of Rapid-Sinking Particles:**
- Large eukaryotic algae and animals evolved, capable of producing:
  - Large, dense fecal pellets (>1 mm diameter, >100 μm³ volume)
  - Macroscopic detritus (animal carcasses, plant material)
  - Mucous aggregates and marine snow particles
- Particle sinking speeds increased to 100–1,000 m day⁻¹ (10–100 fold faster)
- Water column residence times shortened to days to weeks

**Rapid Shift in Carbon Cycling:**
- DOC residence time decreased, limiting water column degradation
- Increased organic carbon export to sediments
- DOC reservoir shrank to modern-like levels within a few hundred million years of eukaryotic evolution
- Fundamental restructuring of ocean productivity and nutrient cycling

## Mechanistic Links Between Particle Size and Ocean Biogeochemistry

**Sinking Speed and Degradation:**
- Degradation rate (first-order) depends on available surface area and microbial colonization
- Slow-sinking, small particles have high surface-area-to-volume ratios, enabling rapid degradation
- Fast-sinking, large particles have low surface-area-to-volume ratios, reaching sediments before extensive degradation
- Net effect: Evolution of large particles bypassed water column degradation

**Nutrient Cycling:**
- In slow-sinking systems, nutrients (N, P, Si) released during water column degradation support phytoplankton growth
- Regenerated nutrients cycle in the euphotic zone, supporting high productivity
- In fast-sinking systems, nutrients are exported with organic matter to deep sediments
- Reduced regenerated nutrient supply requires greater reliance on new nutrients (N₂ fixation, upwelling)

**Oxygen Availability:**
- DOC is a reduced carbon reservoir; its oxidation consumes dissolved oxygen
- Large water column DOC pool increases oxygen demand and supports anoxic zones at depth
- Reduced water column DOC (post-Neoproterozoic) lowers oxygen demand
- Enhanced carbon burial in sediments increases net oxygen production, raising atmospheric pO₂

## Implications for Planetary Oxygenation

**Long-Term Oxygen Regulation:**
- The particle sinking efficiency transition increased net oxygen production by enhancing carbon burial relative to oxidation
- This "oxygen surplus" contributed to rising atmospheric pO₂ in the Paleozoic and beyond
- Subsequent changes in particle sinking (due to ecosystem changes) modulate atmospheric oxygen on million-to-billion-year timescales

**Feedback to Ecosystem Structure:**
- Higher atmospheric oxygen enabled aerobic respiration of refractory organic compounds and colonization of new ecological niches
- Positive feedback: Increased oxygen → expanded aerobic ecosystems → more diverse body sizes and metabolic strategies → potentially altered particle sinking rates
- Over billions of years, this feedback regulated the coevolution of atmosphere, ocean, and biota

**Modern Implications:**
- Current ocean deoxygenation and changes in particle sinking (due to warming, acidification, and ecosystem shifts) may slow net oxygen production
- Reduced zooplankton grazing and fecal pellet production could shift carbon cycling back toward water column retention
- Understanding the particle sinking mechanism provides framework for predicting how future ecosystem changes affect atmospheric composition
