# Dryland River Carbon Dynamics

## Core Concept

Dryland (arid and semi-arid) river systems exhibit unique carbon cycling characteristics shaped by low water availability, episodic flow regimes, and high rates of evaporation and mineral weathering. Carbon dynamics in drying or seasonally dry rivers are sensitive to hydrological disturbance, with flow restoration or modification fundamentally altering carbon mobilization, processing, and export to the atmosphere and coastal waters.

## Hydrological Sensitivity and Carbon Mobilization

Dryland rivers experience dramatic changes in carbon cycling in response to hydrological manipulations such as dam closure, flow restoration, or altered precipitation patterns. Experimental flow restoration to a previously desiccated river reach can mobilize substantial quantities of accumulated organic matter and alter the balance between carbon export and in-stream processing. The sudden increase in water availability in dried streambeds promotes microbial decomposition of accumulated organic material, leading to rapid CO₂ production and potential atmospheric efflux.

## Coupled Carbon-Greenhouse Gas Dynamics

Flow restoration experiments reveal strong coupling between water availability and greenhouse gas dynamics in dryland systems. Increased water input promotes metabolism (respiration) of accumulated organic matter, elevating CO₂ production rates. In some systems, anoxic conditions developing in restored water bodies lead to methane (CH₄) production, creating complex multi-gas dynamics. The temporal evolution of greenhouse gas production following flow restoration reflects changes in redox conditions, substrate availability, and microbial community composition.

## Carbon Sources in Drying Environments

Dryland rivers accumulate organic matter during drought periods, including terrestrial vegetation, sediment-associated carbon, and in-stream biofilms. The age and reactivity of this accumulated carbon depend on the duration of drying and the characteristics of the vegetation and sediment in the riverbed. When flow is restored, this carbon becomes available for microbial processing, with the rate and extent of decomposition controlled by oxygen availability, water temperature, and microbial community composition.

## Climate Change Implications

Dryland regions are projected to experience increased hydrological extremes with longer drought periods interspersed with intense precipitation events or deliberate flow modifications. These changing hydrology patterns will alter carbon accumulation and mobilization dynamics in dryland rivers. Enhanced understanding of carbon-water-greenhouse gas coupling in these systems is essential for predicting how climate change and human water management affect carbon cycling and atmospheric CO₂ and CH₄ concentrations in arid and semi-arid regions.
