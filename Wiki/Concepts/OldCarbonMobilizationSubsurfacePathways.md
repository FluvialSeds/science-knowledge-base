# Old Carbon Mobilization in Subsurface Pathways

## Core Concept

Ancient organic carbon stored in subsurface reservoirs—including permafrost soils (thousands to hundreds of thousands of years old), deep peat layers (centuries to millennia old), and palaeogroundwater systems (up to 11,000+ years old)—becomes mobilized and exported to aquatic systems through multiple subsurface hydrological pathways. This aged carbon can reach aquatic ecosystems and potentially the atmosphere or ocean, with profound implications for understanding carbon cycling, food web structure, and climate feedbacks in regions undergoing environmental change.

## Groundwater Carbon Pathways

Groundwater represents a major subsurface reservoir of ancient dissolved organic carbon that can sustain contemporary aquatic ecosystems. In groundwater-fed streams of the Great Artesian Basin (Australia), dissolved organic carbon with radiocarbon ages up to 11,000 years supports full food webs including fish that bioaccumulate apparent ages of several thousand years. This extraordinary longevity of DOC in groundwater indicates either (1) extremely slow decomposition rates in anoxic subsurface environments or (2) physical isolation preventing contact with decomposer communities. The detection of metabolically-active biofilms and animals with ages reflecting extensive groundwater carbon integration demonstrates that isolation is incomplete—old carbon is mobilized and bioavailable when it reaches surface aquatic systems. The persistent availability of groundwater carbon despite millennial-scale ages challenges conventional models of carbon reactivity and suggests that subsurface reservoirs represent long-term carbon storage systems that can suddenly become bioavailable through hydrological shifts.

## Permafrost Mobilization During Thaw

Permafrost soil represents an integrated subsurface reservoir containing organic carbon from multiple time horizons spanning centuries to hundreds of thousands of years. As climate warming accelerates permafrost thaw, different soil layers become connected to surface hydrological pathways on staggered timescales. Near-surface permafrost (12–19% of stream DOC contributions) thaws rapidly, delivering carbon accumulated over recent centuries. Deep peat layers (39–47% of DOC contributions) thaw more slowly, releasing older carbon accumulated over millennia. This temporal staggering of carbon mobilization means that Arctic streams will experience sequential shifts in carbon age as thaw progresses, with ecological consequences for food web structure and function. Benthic biofilms, macroinvertebrates, and fish integrate this ancient carbon, creating age gradients through food webs that inversely correlate with animal growth and nutrition.

## Peatland Subsurface Connectivity

Peatland systems with active subsurface macropore flow pathways (peat pipes and other connectivity features) create direct linkages between deep, aged peat layers and surface drainage streams. These connections enable mobilization of ancient carbon pools that would otherwise remain isolated in deep peat. Radiocarbon analysis of peatland subsurface waters reveals age heterogeneity reflecting connectivity to different peat strata: peatland soils average 551 ± 133 years BP while riparian zones reach 1,055 ± 107 years BP, indicating access to older carbon pools through deep hydrological flowpaths. However, the actual respiration products (soil CO₂, CH₄, DOC) are predominantly modern, indicating rapid turnover of subsurface carbon upon contact with decomposer communities. This disconnect between aged carbon pools and young respiration products demonstrates that subsurface mobilization represents a key control on carbon accessibility and cycling rates—aged carbon that remains isolated in peat is not available for respiration, but mobilization through subsurface pathways makes it bioavailable.

## Ecological and Climate Implications

The mobilization of ancient carbon through subsurface pathways has several major implications:

**Ecosystem function:** Integration of old carbon into aquatic food webs correlates with reduced animal growth and nutritional status, suggesting that subsurface carbon mobilization can degrade aquatic ecosystem productivity despite providing carbon energy input.

**Carbon cycle feedback:** Rapid mobilization and respiration of ancient subsurface carbon accelerates the return of long-sequestered carbon to the atmosphere as CO₂, potentially amplifying climate warming. Conversely, if old carbon is transported to the ocean and buried as sediment, subsurface mobilization could represent a long-term carbon sequestration pathway.

**Hydrological sensitivity:** Subsurface carbon mobilization responds sensitively to changes in water table elevation, groundwater flux, permafrost distribution, and subsurface connectivity. These hydrological parameters are themselves sensitive to climate change and human water management, creating potential for rapid, nonlinear responses of aquatic carbon cycling to environmental perturbation.
