# Stream DIC Source Partitioning

## Core Concept

Dissolved inorganic carbon in small streams derives from three end-members: atmospheric CO₂ dissolution (47–90% depending on geology), organic matter degradation (4–35%), and carbonate weathering (4–22%). Carbonate rock weathering contributes a higher proportion in limestone streams (14–22%) compared to granite streams (4–6%), revealing how bedrock geology controls carbon cycling pathways in headwater systems.

## Three DIC End-Members

**Atmospheric CO₂ dissolution:**
- Dominant source in headwater streams (47–90%)
- Higher contribution in granitic systems (74–90%) due to minimal carbonate
- Lower but still substantial contribution in limestone systems (47–57%)
- Increases downstream as water equilibrates with atmosphere

**Organic matter degradation:**
- Biogenic respiration from soil and aquatic organic matter
- Contribution: 4–35% depending on system
- Higher in limestone streams (29–35%) due to greater organic matter availability
- Lower in granite streams (4–21%)

**Carbonate weathering:**
- Dissolution of CaCO₃ and other carbonate minerals
- Highest in limestone systems: 14–22%
- Minimal in granite systems: 4–6%
- Contributes radiocarbon-free carbon, affecting ¹⁴C signatures

## Mass Balance Approach

**Methodology:**
- Dual-isotope approach using δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C values
- Comparison of stream DIC with three end-member signatures
- Seasonal and replicatesampling (June, September, December, March-April)

**Radiocarbon variations:**
- Limestone stream: Δ¹⁴C = −238 to −174‰ (less variable)
- Granite stream: Δ¹⁴C = −23 to +10‰ (more variable, including modern)
- DOC and POC show greater variability than DIC

## Bedrock Geology Controls on Carbon Pathways

**Limestone-dominated streams:**
- 14–22% from carbonate weathering
- 29–35% from organic matter degradation
- 47–57% from atmospheric CO₂
- Higher total solute content
- Lower ¹⁴C values due to dead carbon input

**Granite-dominated streams:**
- Only 4–6% from carbonate weathering
- 4–21% from organic matter degradation
- 74–90% from atmospheric CO₂
- Lower total solute content
- Radiocarbon values range to modern

## DIC Source Partitioning in Large River Systems

Dual isotope approaches using δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C have been applied to large river systems to constrain DIC source contributions. In the northeast US rivers (Hudson, Delaware, Potomac, James, York, Rappahannock, Pamunkey), DIC ages vary substantially depending on local weathering regimes and in-stream processing. The Pearl River in south China demonstrates quantitative DIC source apportionment using mixing models, revealing that chemical weathering of carbonate and silicate rocks contributes variable proportions to DIC (silicate weathering: 62 ± 25% in middle reaches; carbonate weathering: 95 ± 5% in headwaters; organic matter respiration: 5 ± 5% in lower reaches). These analyses show that bedrock geology and downstream changes in hydrodynamics fundamentally control which carbon source dominates DIC production.

## Small Streams vs Larger Rivers

**Key difference:**
- Small streams show higher relative contribution from carbonate weathering and organic matter
- Larger rivers increasingly dominated by atmospheric CO₂ exchange
- Stream size and residence time affect equilibration with atmosphere

**Carbon budget implications:**
- Small streams represent significant contributors to total watershed carbon export
- Often overlooked in regional carbon budgets
- Need for comprehensive small-stream surveys across diverse geologies
