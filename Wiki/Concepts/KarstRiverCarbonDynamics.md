# Karst River Carbon Dynamics

## Core Concept

Karst river systems, formed in regions with soluble carbonate geology, exhibit distinctive carbon cycling characterized by high dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) concentrations from carbonate dissolution, complex mixing of waters from different flowpaths, and interplay between chemical weathering and aquatic photosynthesis. Multiple isotopic tracers (δ¹³C, Δ¹⁴C) reveal multiple controls on carbon dynamics, including carbonate weathering, silicate weathering, soil CO₂ production, and in-stream productivity.

## Carbonate Dissolution and DIC Production

In karst systems, carbonate rock dissolution represents the primary source of dissolved inorganic carbon. Carbonate weathering reactions produce DIC with distinctive carbon isotope signatures reflecting the combined contributions of atmospheric CO₂ (used in carbonic acid weathering), soil CO₂ (from root respiration and organic matter decomposition), and the carbonate mineral itself. The prevalence of carbonate weathering distinguishes karst systems from silicate-weathering dominated systems in terms of DIC production rates and isotopic composition.

## Water Mixing and Complex Flowpaths

Karst hydrology involves complex subsurface flowpath networks with rapid transport through fractures, caves, and conduit systems alongside slower diffuse flow through porous carbonate rock. These multiple flowpaths have distinct residence times and chemical compositions, creating water types with differing carbon isotope signatures. River water sampling integrates signals from these different flow components, and carbon isotope variations (δ¹³C, Δ¹⁴C) reflect the changing proportions of water from different sources along the river course.

## Multiple Controls on Carbon Dynamics

Karst rivers are influenced by multiple carbon cycling processes operating simultaneously: (1) carbonate and silicate weathering producing DIC; (2) soil respiration and terrestrial organic matter decomposition contributing CO₂; (3) in-stream aquatic photosynthesis consuming DIC and producing organic carbon; (4) heterotrophic respiration decomposing organic matter and producing CO₂. The relative importance of these processes varies seasonally with discharge, water temperature, and light availability, creating temporal variability in carbon dynamics and isotopic signatures.

## Photosynthesis in Karst Rivers

Unlike many non-karst river systems, karst rivers often support substantial aquatic photosynthesis due to high DIC availability and relatively clear waters (despite seasonal sediment transport). Photosynthetic CO₂ uptake removes DIC from solution and can substantially alter the carbon isotope composition of the dissolved carbon pool. The balance between DIC production from weathering and DIC consumption by photosynthesis influences the downstream carbon export and the age and source signatures of riverine carbon.
