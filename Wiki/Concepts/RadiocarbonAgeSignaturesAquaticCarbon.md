# Radiocarbon Age Signatures of Aquatic Carbon

## Core Concept

Radiocarbon analysis reveals distinct age signatures for different carbon pools in aquatic systems, with dissolved organic carbon (DOC) typically much younger than CO₂ gas lost by evasion. This age heterogeneity provides crucial information about carbon sources, cycling pathways, and the relative contributions of recently fixed versus ancient carbon to aquatic carbon export and atmospheric fluxes.

## Age Contrast Between Carbon Species

A fundamental finding across peatland and aquatic systems is that DOC released in drainage waters is consistently young (modern to ~200 years BP), while CO₂ lost by evasion from water surfaces is substantially older, ranging from modern to ~1,450 years BP. This age separation suggests different source pools and mobilization mechanisms for DOC versus gaseous carbon. The older ¹⁴C signature of evaded CO₂ compared to co-occurring DOC indicates that CO₂ derives from geogenic sources (carbonate weathering) and decomposition of soil organic matter accumulated over centuries, while DOC represents more recently fixed and decomposed plant material.

## Peatland Carbon Dynamics

In peatland-dominated systems, the age of evaded CO₂ varies with seasonal conditions, water pathways, and peat connectivity. Boreal peatland streams during snowmelt episodes show variable radiocarbon signatures, reflecting mixing of water from different subsurface pathways and peat layers with distinct carbon residence times. Peatland pipes, which are subsurface macropore flow pathways, transport carbon with highly variable ¹⁴C ages, indicating direct connectivity between different peat layers and differential mobilization of surface versus deep peat carbon pools.

## Forest-Stream Carbon Coupling

In contrast to peatland systems where old carbon dominates evaded CO₂, stream CO₂ emissions from old-growth boreal forests are fueled primarily by current forest carbon fixation. Radiocarbon analysis shows that ¹⁴C-CO₂ in streams remains consistently in tune with atmospheric ¹⁴C levels (modern to ~105% modern), demonstrating that soil respiration derived from current year photosynthetic products is the primary source of stream CO₂ fluxes. This linkage creates a rapid carbon shortcut returning recently fixed CO₂ to the atmosphere.

## Implications for Carbon Cycling

The age heterogeneity of aquatic carbon pools has important implications for understanding climate feedbacks. Destabilization of peatland carbon through climate change or hydrological alteration may preferentially release old carbon to the atmosphere as CO₂, potentially amplifying climate warming. Conversely, forest ecosystems coupled to streams may exhibit reduced climate feedback intensity if stream CO₂ represents recent rather than ancient carbon. Understanding how different carbon sources respond to environmental disturbance requires explicit radiocarbon characterization of aquatic carbon pools.
