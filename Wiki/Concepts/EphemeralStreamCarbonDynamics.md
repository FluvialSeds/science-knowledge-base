# Ephemeral Stream Carbon Dynamics

## Core Concept

Radiocarbon ages of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) in ephemeral streams respond dramatically to flow conditions: high-flow events mobilize atmospheric-like carbon (younger), while low-flow periods expose older terrestrial carbon from soil reservoirs accumulated over seasons. This flow-dependent carbon remobilization demonstrates how precipitation patterns and hydrologic extremes control aged carbon export from semi-arid watersheds.

## Seven-Year Radiocarbon Record

**Study site:**
- Sabino Creek, southern Arizona
- Ephemeral stream with highly variable discharge
- USGS stream gauge provides flow rate data for correlation with ¹⁴C measurements
- Seven-year sampling period (2009–2016) captures range of hydrologic conditions

**Measurements:**
- DIC and DOC radiocarbon (¹⁴C) and stable isotope (δ¹³C) analysis
- Correlation with stream discharge rates
- Relationship between carbon age, isotope values, and flow

## Flow-Dependent Carbon Age Patterns

**High-flow events:**
- DIC shows F(DIC) values closer to atmospheric equilibrium
- Less negative δ¹³C values
- Younger, modern carbon signatures
- Reflects rapid water movement and minimal contact with old soil carbon

**Low-flow periods:**
- More negative δ¹³C values for DIC
- Depressed ¹⁴C (old carbon signatures)
- DOC remains relatively unchanged despite seasonal variation
- Reflects groundwater contribution and extended soil-water contact

## Seasonal Carbon Mobilization Pattern

**Summer rainy season dynamics:**
- Large precipitation events mobilize older organic carbon from soils
- This stored carbon released during large runoff events
- Creates lagged response where old carbon peaks during or after high-flow periods

**Seasonal trends:**
- DOC consistently remains near modern carbon signatures
- Despite seasonal and flow variations
- Suggests DOC from recent productivity or rapid biological cycling
- DIC more responsive to flow and seasonal cycling than DOC

## Implications for Semi-Arid Carbon Export

**Hydrologic control on carbon mobilization:**
- Strong relationship between flow rate and carbon age indicates hydrology fundamentally controls carbon export
- Ephemeral streams show more dramatic responses than perennial systems
- Flow-driven mobilization distinct from chemical transport

**Soil carbon mobilization:**
- Older soil carbon (accumulated over extended drought periods) becomes labile during storms
- Transport during precipitation-driven runoff carries aged carbon to streams
- High-flow events transport disproportionate amount of old carbon

**Climate change implications:**
- Understanding carbon export under varying precipitation patterns critical
- Predictions of future climate must account for flow-carbon age relationships
- Semi-arid systems particularly sensitive to precipitation changes
- Potential for increased carbon export if precipitation extremes intensify

## Regional Applicability

**Semi-arid watershed characteristics:**
- Limited perennial flow means vegetation and soils accumulate carbon during dry periods
- Rainy season mobilizes this accumulated carbon
- Pattern likely applicable to other semi-arid systems globally

**Comparison with perennial systems:**
- Ephemeral streams show more pronounced carbon-flow relationships
- Perennial systems with continuous flow show more muted effects
- Threshold behavior may occur as systems transition from ephemeral to perennial
