# Sapropel Formation and Nitrogen Cycle Coupling

## Core Concept

Mediterranean sapropels—organic-rich sediments deposited during episodic anoxic events throughout the Plio-Pleistocene—are intimately linked to coupled changes in the [[NitrogenCycle|nitrogen cycle]]. During sapropel events, [[Anammox|anammox]] bacteria and diazotrophic endosymbionts of mat-forming diatoms both became abundant, creating a reinforcing feedback mechanism between nitrogen limitation, [[NitrogenFixation|nitrogen fixation]], and [[DeepWaterAnoxia|deep-water oxygen depletion]].

## Depositional Setting

The Eastern Mediterranean today is a well-ventilated, oligotrophic basin with low primary productivity. However, insolation-driven increases in monsoon intensity and freshwater runoff throughout the Plio-Pleistocene periodically disrupted ocean circulation, leading to [[DeepWaterAnoxia|deep-water oxygen depletion]] and the deposition of sapropel layers. These episodes represent transient shifts in ocean stratification and nutrient cycling with profound consequences for microbial communities and biogeochemical cycling.

## Nitrogen Cycle Perturbations

During sapropel events, the microbial community shifted to favor two seemingly opposite nitrogen transformations: [[NitrogenFixation|nitrogen fixation]] by cyanobacteria (acquiring N₂ from the atmosphere) and [[Anammox|anammox]] (anaerobic ammonia oxidation removing fixed nitrogen). This apparent paradox reflects a tightly coupled feedback: nitrogen limitation drives increased reliance on diazotrophic cyanobacteria that form mats and rafts with diatoms; these symbioses promote water column stratification and enhance anoxia; expanded oxygen-minimum zones stimulate [[Anammox|anammox]] activity, which removes fixed nitrogen and reinforces nitrogen limitation.

## Biomarker Evidence

[[BacteriohopanepolyolBiomarkers|BHPs]] diagnostic of [[Anammox|anammox]] bacteria and branched glycerol dialkyl glycerols (brGDGTs; see [[GDGTBiomarkers|GDGTs]]) from uncultured archaea and bacteria reveal both organisms' abundance during sapropel formation. Crucially, the carbon-13 isotopic composition of anammox biomarkers directly reflects progressive ¹³C-depletion in deep-water dissolved inorganic carbon, demonstrating that these microbial transformations are tightly coupled to organic carbon sequestration and burial. This isotopic linkage proves that [[Anammox|anammox]] activity, diazotrophic production, and carbon burial were all mechanistically linked during sapropel deposition.

## Paleoceanographic Implications

The sapropel-nitrogen cycle coupling reveals how biological feedbacks can rapidly shift ocean nitrogen transformations in response to physical forcing (insolation and circulation changes). Sapropel events differ fundamentally from [[OceanicAnoxicEvents|open-ocean anoxic events (OAEs)]] in their nitrogen cycle signatures, suggesting distinct mechanisms of anoxia development and microbial response. These results establish Mediterranean sapropels as a natural laboratory for understanding how nitrogen cycle perturbations and climate variations interact to regulate carbon sequestration and ocean habitability on millennial timescales.
