# Microbial Oxidation of Organic Carbon in Mountain Soils

## Core Concept

In rapidly eroding tropical mountain systems, petrogenic organic carbon exhumed from bedrock is oxidized primarily through **microbial processes** in hillslope and colluvial soils rather than through abiotic weathering alone.

## Process and Mechanism

Microbes in soils catalyze the oxidation of organic carbon, converting it to CO₂ that is released to the atmosphere. This process is:
- **Microbially mediated** — bacteria and other soil microorganisms drive the oxidation
- **Rapid** — occurs within soil profiles on timescales of years to decades
- **Efficient** — approximately 67% of exhumed petrogenic carbon is oxidized in soils

The molecular and isotopic evolution of organic carbon and lipid biomarkers during soil formation reveals the microbial nature of the oxidation process, distinguishing it from simple abiotic chemical weathering.

## Quantifying Oxidation Rates

In the Central Range of Taiwan (world's fastest-eroding mountain belt, 3–6 mm/year erosion), petrogenic carbon oxidation produces:
- **6.1 to 18.6 metric tons of carbon per km² per year** CO₂ emissions
- Rates are **coupled to erosion rate** — higher erosion drives higher oxidation fluxes

This tight coupling occurs because rapid erosion exposes more petrogenic carbon and creates favorable conditions (fresh surfaces, increased water infiltration, high organic carbon availability) for microbial activity.

## Controls on Oxidation

Factors promoting microbial oxidation include:
- **High erosion rates** — expose fresh bedrock surfaces
- **Steep hillslopes** — increase water infiltration and soil development
- **Tropical climate** — warm, wet conditions favor microbial activity
- **High hillslope sediment transport** — continuous exposure of reactive surfaces

## Implications

Microbial oxidation of [[PetrogenicOrganicCarbon|petrogenic carbon]] in mountain soils represents a significant source of atmospheric CO₂ that is directly controlled by tectonic and climatic factors driving erosion. This creates a feedback between tectonics and atmospheric composition on geological timescales, as described in the [[CarbonCycleMountainSystems|carbon cycle in mountain systems]].
