# Methane Radiocarbon Signatures

## Core Concept

Radiocarbon ages of dissolved and evaded methane from freshwater systems reveal distinct carbon sources and pathways compared to CO2, with CH4 typically much older (400–1,987 years B.P.) than CO2 (303–521 years B.P.) due to origins from deeper, older peat layers and anoxic zones. These signatures enable tracing of methane sources and identifying old carbon mobilization from soil profiles.

## Age Differentiation: CH₄ vs CO₂

**Evaded gases from peatland streams:**
- Evaded methane ages: 1,617–1,987 years B.P. (very old)
- Evaded CO₂ ages: 303–521 years B.P. (intermediate age)
- Age difference indicates distinct source depths within peat profiles

**Dissolved gases in peatland systems:**
- Dissolved CH₄ and DOC show different radiocarbon signatures
- Dissolved CH₄ ages vary by location: 400 to 3,000 years B.P. in peatland streams
- Large spatial variation reflects different depths within peat profiles

**Spatial variation in stream CH₄ ages:**
- Ditch in afforested peatland: ~400 years B.P.
- Bog perimeter stream: ~3,000 years B.P.
- Variation suggests both shallow and deep peat layers contribute to methane emissions

## Source Attribution Through Radiocarbon

**Peat layer stratification:**
- Shallow peat layers (younger, more recent) produce younger CO₂
- Deep peat layers (older) produce older CH₄ through anaerobic decomposition
- Radiocarbon ages allow inference of production depths without direct coring

**Mixing of carbon sources:**
- Isotope mass balance models show that evaded CO₂ and CH₄ derive from different source areas
- Most evaded CO₂ originates from younger peat layers near surface
- CH₄ comes from deeper, older peat layers

**Evidence for old carbon mobilization:**
- Ancient carbon (millennia-old) actively participates in greenhouse gas production
- Demonstrates that soil-water-atmosphere carbon transfers involve both recent and ancient carbon stores

## Implications for Peatland Carbon Cycling

The significant age difference between evaded CH₄ and CO₂ reveals important insights into peatland carbon cycling and greenhouse gas production pathways. This [[RadiocarbonAnalyticalMethods|methodological development]] enables investigation of soil-water-atmosphere carbon transfers through complementary analysis of both CH₄ and CO₂, advancing understanding of how ancient peat carbon is mobilized and released as greenhouse gases from [[PeatlandCarbonExportToAquaticSystems|peatland ecosystems]].

## Climate Change Implications

Old carbon mobilization from deep peat layers has important implications for understanding greenhouse gas production pathways and carbon mobilization in peatland ecosystems under climate change scenarios. As active layers deepen and permafrost thaws, access to deeper, older peat layers may accelerate old carbon emissions.
