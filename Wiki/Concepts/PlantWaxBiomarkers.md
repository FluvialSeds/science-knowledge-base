# Plant-Wax Biomarkers as Ecosystem Proxies

## Core Concept

Plant-wax lipids (n-alkanes, n-alcohols, and n-alkanoic acids) are organic compounds produced by plants that serve as biomarkers for reconstructing terrestrial ecosystem structure and vegetation composition in sedimentary records. These compounds are preserved in river sediments and can be analyzed for stable carbon isotope composition (δ¹³C) to determine the relative contributions of different photosynthetic pathways (C₃ vs C₄ plants).

## Key Compound Classes

**n-Alkanes** (C₂₅-C₃₅):
- High δ¹³C variability across chain lengths (8.1 ± 0.7‰)
- Weak correlations between homologues
- Represent a mixture of C₃ and C₄ sources
- Stable over time; catchment-integrated signal
- Low discharge sensitivity

**n-Alcohols and n-Alkanoic Acids** (C₂₆-C₃₆):
- Depleted δ¹³C values with low variability (2.6 ± 0.6‰ and 2.0 ± 1.1‰)
- Strong correlations between homologues (r = 0.70–0.99)
- Respond rapidly to seasonal discharge changes
- Recently entrained, local ecosystem sources
- High discharge sensitivity

## Photosynthetic Pathway Discrimination

δ¹³C values differ significantly between C₃ and C₄ plants due to differential fractionation of ¹³C during photosynthesis. This allows plant-wax lipids to act as tracers for vegetation type, with differences as high as 6‰ within a single compound class across different chain lengths. However, competing factors like light and water stress can cause secondary fractionation effects that complicate interpretation.

## Applications

Plant-wax biomarkers are used to reconstruct:
- Savanna landcover response to climate change
- Miocene C₄ grassland expansion
- Long-term paleovegetation changes in terrestrial and lacustrine sediments
- Modern ecosystem structure from fluvial sediments
