# Paleofires Reconstructed from Biomarkers

## Core Concept

Fire regimes—the frequency, extent, intensity, and seasonality of fires in terrestrial ecosystems—can be reconstructed from organic biomarkers preserved in sedimentary records. Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) produced during incomplete combustion of organic matter, combined with charcoal abundance records, provide complementary indicators of past burning intensity and frequency. By integrating fire biomarkers with paleovegetation records from other biomarkers and paleoclimate reconstructions, paleoceanographers can understand how fire regimes respond to climate variations and how fire-vegetation feedbacks have shaped ecosystem development across geological time.

## Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons as Fire Indicators

Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons are produced during the incomplete combustion of organic matter in fires and accumulate in sediments as direct evidence of past burning events. The distribution of PAH compounds reflects the temperature of combustion and the type of organic matter burned, allowing paleoceanographers to distinguish among different fire types and intensities. PAH concentrations in sediment cores provide a continuous record of fire activity through time, with peaks indicating periods of enhanced burning. The molecular composition of the PAH fraction (including the ratio of pyrogenic to petrogenic PAHs) allows quantification of fire intensity and discrimination of PAHs derived from wildfires versus anthropogenic sources or fossil fuel combustion.

## Charcoal as a Paleofires Proxy

Charcoal particles preserved in sediments represent partially combusted plant material and provide a physical record of past fire occurrence. Charcoal abundance, morphology, and size distributions in sediment cores indicate the frequency and intensity of burning events. High charcoal concentrations indicate periods of active burning, while low or absent charcoal suggests fire-free intervals. The size distribution of charcoal particles reflects burning intensity and transport distance from the fire source—smaller charcoal particles are transported farther, while larger charcoal remains near the burn site. Charcoal records extend beyond the range of PAHs in some depositional settings, making them valuable for reconstructing complete fire histories over million-year timescales.

## Fire-Climate-Vegetation Coupling

Fire regimes are fundamentally linked to climate and vegetation composition. Warming and drying conditions promote increased fire activity, while wet conditions reduce burning frequency. Changes in vegetation composition and fuel load alter the flammability of terrestrial ecosystems, creating feedbacks between fire, vegetation, and climate. By integrating fire biomarker records with paleovegetation data from plant wax biomarkers and paleoclimate reconstructions from biomarker isotopes, paleoceanographers can quantify the strength of fire-climate-vegetation feedbacks and understand how these interactions have evolved through time. Periods of rapid climate change often show coupled shifts in fire activity and vegetation composition, revealing the sensitivity of terrestrial ecosystems to climate forcing.

## Millennial-Scale Fire Variability

Charcoal and PAH records reveal that fire regimes vary on multiple timescales, from decadal to millennial and beyond. Enhanced fire activity during periods of rapid warming reflects both increased fuel dryness and altered ignition patterns. Conversely, shifts in vegetation composition in response to climate change can alter fuel characteristics and fire flammability. High-resolution paleofires records from sediment cores with abundant organic material enable detection of multi-millennial-scale oscillations in fire activity that correlate with paleoclimate variations, providing insight into how modern fire management and future fire regimes may respond to anthropogenic climate change.
