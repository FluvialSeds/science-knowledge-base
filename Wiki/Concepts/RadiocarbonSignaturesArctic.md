# Radiocarbon Signatures in Arctic Fluvial DIC

## Core Concept

Radiocarbon (¹⁴C) dating of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in Arctic rivers reveals the age of organic matter being respired by aquatic microbes. Because ¹⁴C decays with a half-life of 5,730 years, organic carbon stored in permafrost for thousands to hundreds of thousands of years produces distinctive radiocarbon-depleted (aged) signatures when respired. By measuring both the stable carbon isotope composition (δ¹³C) and radiocarbon age (Δ¹⁴C) of DIC before and after bioincubations, scientists can estimate the age of the substrate being metabolized by microbes using Keeling plot methods. This approach reveals whether permafrost carbon mobilization produces detectable aged carbon signals in fluvial networks, and how substrate availability controls whether microbes preferentially respire aged or modern carbon.

## The Radiocarbon System

**Modern Carbon:**
- Living organisms maintain near-equilibrium ¹⁴C/¹²C ratios with the atmosphere through photosynthesis and food web interactions
- Upon death, ¹⁴C decay causes progressive depletion of ¹⁴C
- Recently dead organic matter (< 100 years old) has Δ¹⁴C near 0‰

**Ancient Carbon:**
- Permafrost-stored organic matter (1,000–100,000+ years old) has severely depleted ¹⁴C signatures
- Radiocarbon age increases with storage duration: Δ¹⁴C values range from −200‰ (centuries old) to −700‰+ (tens of thousands of years old)
- Keeling plot analysis can determine whether microbial respiration consumes modern or aged substrates

## Keeling Plot Method for Respiratory Carbon

In a Keeling plot, δ¹³C of DIC is plotted against the inverse of DIC concentration (1/[DIC]). The slope and intercept reveal the isotopic composition of carbon being added to the system:

**During Bioincubations:**
- Microbes consume organic matter (with a specific δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C signature) and release CO₂
- Accumulated CO₂ progressively changes the δ¹³C and Δ¹⁴C of the DIC pool
- A linear Keeling plot indicates that carbon is being added from a single source (or homogeneous mixture)
- The y-intercept of the Keeling plot estimates the isotopic composition of the added (respiratory) carbon

**Applying to Radiocarbon:**
- The same Keeling plot principle applies to Δ¹⁴C vs. 1/[DIC]
- Respiratory Δ¹⁴C values reveal whether the substrate being respired is modern (Δ¹⁴C ≈ 0‰) or aged (Δ¹⁴C < −200‰)
- A shift from ambient Δ¹⁴C (modern) to respiratory Δ¹⁴C (aged) indicates preferential respiration of ancient carbon

## Application to Permafrost Carbon Detection

**In Permafrost Thaw Streams:**
- Ambient Δ¹⁴C-DIC is often depleted (−329 to −713‰) because water in contact with permafrost incorporates aged DIC
- Respiratory Δ¹⁴C derived from Keeling plots is also aged, indicating microbes respire aged organic matter
- FT-ICR MS analysis confirms substrate is permafrost-derived: nitrogen-containing and aliphatic compounds preferentially correlate with aged DIC

**In Non-Permafrost Streams:**
- Ambient Δ¹⁴C-DIC is near modern (≈ 0‰)
- Respiratory Δ¹⁴C is also near modern, indicating microbes consume modern terrestrial or aquatic carbon
- Shift in substrate composition (from permafrost-like to terrestrial/aquatic DOM) is reflected in both Δ¹⁴C and molecular composition

## The Challenge: Permafrost Carbon Masking

Despite the presence of aged substrates in permafrost-influenced waters, several factors obscure the permafrost carbon signal:

**Rapid Depletion:**
- Highly labile aged organic matter is consumed quickly by microbes
- The pool of readily-respirable aged carbon is exhausted within bioincubation timescales
- Downstream dilution reduces aged signals to detection limits

**Masking by Modern Production:**
- In-stream photosynthesis and microbial biomass synthesis generate modern (Δ¹⁴C ≈ 0‰) carbon
- Physical outgassing of CO₂ removes respiratory DIC before analysis
- Competitive substrate utilization may favor labile modern compounds over aged compounds

**Implication:**
The radiocarbon signature indicates that permafrost carbon, despite being highly biolabile, is respired in-stream rather than exported to downstream ecosystems. This has implications for quantifying permafrost carbon fluxes to the ocean versus atmosphere.
