# Ramped Pyrolysis/Oxidation (RPO)

## Core Concept

Ramped Pyrolysis/Oxidation (RPO) is a compound-specific analytical technique that heats organic samples across a temperature range (typically 150–1000°C) at a controlled rate (e.g., 5°C/min) while measuring the composition and age of carbon released at each temperature step. RPO reveals how organic carbon bond strength varies within a sample and links this bond-strength diversity to radiocarbon age, activation energy distributions, and preservation mechanisms.

## Analytical Approach

The RPO instrument heats a sample through a programmed temperature range, releasing organic carbon (as CO₂) that is trapped and analyzed for:

**Isotopic composition:**
- Stable carbon isotope ratios (δ¹³C)
- Radiocarbon activity (¹⁴C/¹²C), expressed as radiocarbon age (years before present)

**Bond-strength characterization:**
- Activation energy (E) distributions — the energy required to break chemical bonds holding organic carbon in place
- Broader activation energy distributions indicate diverse bond types and mineral-protection interactions
- Narrower distributions indicate more uniform, weakly-bound organic carbon

**Temperature windows:**
- Low-temperature releases (150–350°C): Weakly bound, labile organic carbon
- Mid-temperature releases (350–600°C): Intermediate-strength bonds
- High-temperature releases (600–1000°C): Strongly bound, mineral-protected organic carbon

## Applications

**Radiocarbon chronometry across bond strengths:**
RPO determines how radiocarbon age changes as bonds become stronger, revealing whether carbon of different ages is associated with different protective mechanisms. For example, strongly bonded (high-E) carbon often shows older radiocarbon ages, indicating longer preservation times.

**Mineral protection assessment:**
RPO can distinguish between mineral-bound and unbound organic carbon by examining activation energy distributions. Samples with mineral protection show broader E distributions and persistent high-energy carbon, indicating formation of organo-mineral bonds over time.

**Organic matter characterization:**
Different compound classes and source materials release carbon across different temperature ranges, allowing RPO to fingerprint organic matter composition and source without requiring molecular-level chromatography.

## Advantages

- Compound-specific analysis without molecular separation
- Links carbon age directly to thermal stability/bond strength
- Reveals preservation mechanisms at the molecular level
- Can analyze diverse sample types (soils, sediments, dissolved organic carbon)

## Limitations

- Kinetic factors (heating rate, sample size) can affect interpretation
- Blank carbon contribution must be carefully assessed and corrected
- Instrument-specific variations require careful calibration
- Interpretation requires careful consideration of the non-linear relationship between activation energy and actual bond strength

## Activation Energy Distributions as Organic Carbon Reactivity Proxies

The activation energy (E) distribution—the probability distribution of bond-breaking energies required to decompose organic carbon—is fundamentally related to **organic carbon thermal reactivity and preservation mechanisms**. Regularized inverse kinetic modeling of RPO data can estimate E distributions, revealing:

- **Low-energy organic carbon** (E < 150 kJ/mol): Weakly bound, labile compounds released at low temperatures. These represent recently-synthesized or recently-deposited organic matter and are rapidly consumed by microbial respiration.

- **High-energy organic carbon** (E > 250 kJ/mol): Strongly bound compounds requiring high temperatures for decomposition. These represent mineral-protected or chemically resistant compounds with long residence times in soils and sediments.

- **E-versus-age relationships**: Radiocarbon ages increase systematically with activation energy, revealing that **bond strength is a direct proxy for carbon preservation and residence time**. Old radiocarbon ages are associated with high-energy carbon, while young ages are associated with low-energy carbon. This coupling enables inference of preservation mechanisms from the E-¹⁴C relationship alone.

This approach reveals that **thermal reactivity (bond strength) is a more fundamental predictor of carbon age and preservation than either chemical structure or compound class**. Organic matter with the same molecular structure can show different E distributions and ages depending on its interactions with minerals, explaining why reactive carbon can persist for millennia when protected by mineral phases.
