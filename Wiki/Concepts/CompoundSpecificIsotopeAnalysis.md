# Compound-Specific Isotope Analysis (GC-IRMS)

## Core Concept

Compound-specific isotope analysis measures the stable carbon isotope composition (δ¹³C) of individual organic compounds separated by high-pressure liquid chromatography (UPLC) or gas chromatography (GC) and analyzed via isotope ratio mass spectrometry (IRMS). This technique enables precise isotopic characterization of biomarkers at compound-level resolution, rather than bulk samples.

## Analytical Approach

**Instrumentation:**
- Ultra-high pressure liquid chromatography (UPLC) or semi-preparative GC for compound separation
- High-temperature gas chromatography-isotope ratio mass spectrometry (HT-GC-IRMS) for δ¹³C measurement
- Precision controlled through careful standardization and replicate analysis

**Key Advantage:**
Intact biomarkers can be analyzed without destruction, preserving compound identity and allowing measurement of δ¹³C variability across homologous series (e.g., different chain lengths within a lipid class).

## Applications to Different Biomarker Classes

**Plant-Wax Lipids:**
- n-Alkanes, n-alcohols, and n-alkanoic acids show δ¹³C variability (2–8‰) across chain lengths
- Differences reflect mixing of C₃ and C₄ plant sources
- Used for paleovegetation reconstruction and photosynthetic pathway discrimination

**Bacteriohopanepolyols (BHPs):**
- Ancient bacterial lipid biomarkers preserved in sediments
- Intact δ¹³C measurement reveals isotopic heterogeneity within BHP pools
- Allows distinction of separate biosynthetic origins or methylation effects within cells

## Reproducibility and Standardization

Precision is controlled through:
- Replicate analysis with uncertainty propagation
- Vienna Pee-Dee Belemnite (VPDB) standard calibration
- Acetate carbon correction where applicable
- Reported uncertainties (±1‰ or better for well-characterized compounds)
