# Strontium Isotope Variability in Large Rivers

## Core Concept

The ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratio in dissolved river water reflects the weathering signatures of rocks and minerals in the upstream drainage basin. Radiogenic strontium (high ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) is preferentially released by weathering of ancient continental crust with high rubidium-to-strontium ratios, while unradiogenic strontium (low ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) dominates inputs from young oceanic basalts or unlithified sediments. On short timescales (days to weeks), dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr in major rivers shows extreme variability driven by rainfall distribution across the watershed, in-stream interactions with particulate materials, and tributary contributions. These fluctuations rival the largest isotopic shifts observed across geological time, highlighting the sensitivity of riverine Sr isotope compositions to modern hydrological and sediment transport processes.

## Dissolved Versus Suspended Particulate Strontium

Rivers transport strontium in two forms with distinct isotopic characteristics:

**Dissolved Strontium:**
- Mobile form in water column; reflects weathering inputs and in-stream chemical reactions
- Responds rapidly to discharge fluctuations because different tributaries have distinct Sr isotopic compositions
- Can be fractionated from particulate Sr through adsorption-desorption processes at different water column depths

**Suspended Particulate Strontium:**
- Incorporated into clay minerals and iron oxides during weathering and authigenic mineral formation
- Often more radiogenic than dissolved Sr because primary minerals (e.g., feldspars) have high ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr
- Strong correlations between dissolved and particulate ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr at all water column depths indicate active in-stream equilibration between the two phases

## Hydrogeochemical Mixing Model Framework

High-resolution Sr isotope time-series data are interpreted using hydro-geochemical mixing models that account for:

**Tributary Contributions:**
- Each major tributary has a characteristic ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr composition derived from its unique lithology and weathering patterns
- Rainfall-driven discharge changes alter the relative contribution of each tributary to the main stem
- Model inputs include literature-derived Sr concentrations and ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr values for major tributaries

**Spatio-Temporal Rainfall Variability:**
- 5-day integrated rainfall data serves as a proxy for discharge in each tributary
- Spatial heterogeneity in monsoon precipitation across the drainage basin drives tributary contribution shifts
- Model predicts dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr composition as a time-weighted mixture of tributary Sr

**In-Stream Interactions:**
- Depth-specific sampling reveals how dissolved-particulate Sr isotope relationships vary through the water column
- Consistent relationships across depths suggest that in-stream weathering and sediment-water interactions are secondary controls on riverine Sr isotope composition
- These processes can amplify or dampen the primary source signal from tributaries

## Key Result: Week-to-Week Variability

Dissolved ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr in the Ganga and Brahmaputra rivers showed week-to-week changes reaching 0.0134 ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr units—the largest short-term fluctuations documented in any major river globally. This variability is primarily explained by spatio-temporal rainfall distribution driving tributary discharge changes, with secondary contributions from in-stream sediment-water interactions. The results demonstrate that riverine Sr isotope compositions are exquisitely sensitive to watershed-scale hydrology and should not be interpreted as simple integration of steady-state weathering rates.

## Implications for Marine Strontium Isotope Records

The extreme short-term variability in riverine Sr isotope compositions has profound implications for interpreting marine ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr records used to trace weathering intensity, sea-level changes, and paleoclimate. Periods of enhanced monsoon variability or climate-driven precipitation changes will produce distinctive patterns in marine Sr isotope curves. The Ganga and Brahmaputra deliver disproportionately large radiogenic Sr fluxes to the ocean, substantially influencing global marine Sr isotope budgets; understanding how their Sr isotope compositions fluctuate provides critical context for interpreting long-term trends in the marine record.
